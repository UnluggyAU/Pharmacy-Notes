Crohn Disease

Chronic, relapsing, immune-mediated inflammatory bowel disease. Transmural inflammation, any part of GI tract mouth-to-anus, often discontinuous (skip lesions). Not curable — management aims for corticosteroid-free remission and mucosal healing.

🔬

Pathophysiology

Core process: Immune dysregulation in a genetically susceptible person → loss of tolerance to intestinal microbiota → chronic transmural GI inflammation.
Genetic susceptibility + environmental triggers (diet, smoking, microbiome alterations, infections, NSAIDs, stress) → abnormal innate immune sensing of bacteria/antigens by epithelial and immune cells
T-cell activation → sequential cascade of inflammatory mediators (IL-1, IL-6, IL-17, TNF-α) → imbalance between pro-inflammatory and anti-inflammatory factors
Chronic transmural inflammation → fibrogenesis, collagen production, metalloproteinase activation → stricture formation, fistulas, abscesses, perforation, malabsorption
💡
Key pathological features: Transmural inflammation (full bowel wall); patchy/discontinuous distribution with skip lesions; deep ulceration; fibrosis and stricture formation; fistula and abscess formation. Granulomas may be present but are NOT required for diagnosis.
⚠️

Risk Factors

🧬 Genetic
  • Family history (genetic influence stronger in Crohn than UC, ~30%)
  • Early onset / greater severity
🌍 Environmental
  • Smoking — worsens Crohn; ↑ relapse and surgery risk
  • Western diet (high fat, refined sugars, low fibre)
  • Altered microbiome
  • Previous infection
  • NSAIDs may exacerbate IBD
  • Antibiotics may cause relapse (alters gut flora)
  • Stress
  • Appendicectomy — may slightly increase Crohn risk
🩺

Clinical Features

🔴 Core Symptoms
  • Abdominal pain (colicky/postprandial)
  • Diarrhoea (less prominent than UC)
  • Weight loss
  • Fatigue
  • Fever (more common than UC)
  • Unexplained anaemia
⚠️ Complications
  • Bowel obstruction (inflammation, fibrosis, strictures)
  • Abscess formation
  • Fistulas — especially perianal (up to 40% of patients; characteristic of Crohn)
  • Perforation
  • Nephrolithiasis (renal stones)
  • Cholelithiasis (gallstones) — especially with ileal Crohn (impaired bile salt reabsorption)
  • Bile salt malabsorption → steatorrhoea
  • Malnutrition
  • ↑ Colorectal cancer risk — long-standing Crohn colitis; surveillance colonoscopy required
🌐 Extra-Intestinal Manifestations (20–30%)
  • Joints: peripheral arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, sacroiliitis
  • Skin: erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum, perianal skin tags, oral mucosal lesions
  • Eyes: uveitis, scleritis, episcleritis
  • Hepatobiliary: hepatic disease, primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC), fatty liver
  • Urinary: calculi, ureteric obstruction, fistulas
🥗 Nutritional Deficiency Risks
  • Iron, folate, B12 deficiency (terminal ileum affected)
  • Vitamin D deficiency
  • Zinc deficiency
  • Calcium and magnesium deficiency
  • Fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies

🔍

Diagnosis & Investigations

Diagnosis of Crohn disease is made if typical features are seen on endoscopic, histological, and radiological investigations. Chronic inflammation is generally observed on biopsies. Granulomas may be present but are not required for diagnosis.

Key investigations:
• Colonoscopy with biopsy — gold standard (may show discrete aphthous ulcers, skip lesions, cobblestone mucosa, pseudopolyps)
• Faecal calprotectin — cut-off 50 mcg/g: NPV ~100% (excellent for ruling out colitis); less sensitive for proximal small bowel inflammation
• MRI small bowel (MR enterography) — preferred for assessing small bowel extent and transmural activity
• CT/MRI abdomen/pelvis — assess complications (abscess, fistula, stricture, obstruction)
• FBC, CRP/ESR, iron studies, B12, folate, albumin — assess disease activity and nutritional impact
• Stool cultures — exclude infectious causes before starting immunosuppressive therapy
🎯

Treatment Goals

Primary
  • Induce clinical remission in active disease
  • Maintain corticosteroid-free remission
  • Achieve mucosal healing (endoscopic remission)
Long-term
  • Prevent relapse
  • Prevent hospitalisation and surgery
  • Maintain nutritional status
  • Manage extra-intestinal manifestations
⚠️
Corticosteroids are for induction ONLY — never for maintenance. Mucosal healing is important (associated with ↓ hospitalisation, ↓ surgery need).
📋
Before starting treatment, consider:
  • Active or latent infection — TB, Hepatitis B, HIV
  • Immunisation requirements — especially live vaccines before starting biologics; Hepatitis B vaccination if at risk
  • History of malignant disease
  • Advise all patients who smoke to stop — worsens clinical course and ↑ risk of surgery
💡
Non-pharmacological: Smoking cessation (essential — ↓ relapse rates); nutritional management (dietitian); monitor weight, nutritional status; micronutrient supplementation (Vit D, B12, iron, zinc, folate, Ca, Mg); polymeric oral nutrition supplements if needed; lower insoluble fibre may help control diarrhoea/symptoms with strictures. Multidisciplinary team essential.

Drug Summaries

Systemic Corticosteroids (Prednisolone, Hydrocortisone, Methylprednisolone)Corticosteroid — Induction Only
Doses (Crohn Induction)
  • Mild-moderate: Prednisolone/prednisone 40–50 mg PO daily in the morning → taper over 6–8 weeks
  • Severe (IV): Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV every 6 hours
  • OR Methylprednisolone sodium succinate 60 mg IV daily
  • Generally 3–7 days IV → switch to oral when disease activity subsides
Role & Key Points
Suppress intestinal immune inflammation → ↓ bowel wall / mucosal inflammation → improve acute flare symptoms. INDUCTION ONLY — NOT for maintenance. Corticosteroids are not more effective than placebo for relapse prevention and do NOT induce mucosal healing. Morning dosing minimises HPA axis suppression.
⚠️
IBD-specific: Corticosteroids are for INDUCTION only — never maintenance. Do NOT use for non-inflammatory residual pain. Greatest caution in patients with diabetes (hyperglycaemia risk is significant), active infection (masks fever), psychiatric history.
Mechanism of Action
Glucocorticoid receptor agonist → ↓ transcription of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) → broad anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive effects. Also ↓ vascular permeability and immune cell migration.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Active, uncontrolled infection — avoid (masks fever and may worsen infection)
  • Live vaccines — contraindicated during immunosuppressive doses
  • Diabetes — significant hyperglycaemia; monitor BGL closely
  • Hypertension / heart failure — fluid retention worsens; monitor BP
  • Peptic ulcer disease — ↑ ulcer risk; use with PPI
  • Osteoporosis — prolonged use causes bone loss; consider bisphosphonate prophylaxis
  • Psychiatric disorders — may precipitate or worsen psychosis, mania, depression
  • Glaucoma / cataracts — may worsen with prolonged use
  • Do NOT stop abruptly after prolonged use — taper to avoid adrenal insufficiency and disease rebound
Adverse Effects
Common (dose and duration dependent)
  • Weight gain, increased appetite, fluid retention, insomnia
  • Mood changes (euphoria or irritability)
  • Hyperglycaemia (may precipitate or worsen diabetes)
  • ↑ Blood pressure
  • GI: nausea, dyspepsia (take with food)
  • Skin: acne, easy bruising, striae, poor wound healing
  • Increased susceptibility to infection
Prolonged/High-dose — Serious
  • Adrenal suppression → adrenal crisis if stopped abruptly
  • Osteoporosis → fractures (especially vertebral)
  • Cushingoid features (moon face, buffalo hump, central obesity)
  • Avascular necrosis (especially femoral head)
  • Posterior subcapsular cataracts; glaucoma
  • Proximal myopathy
Monitoring
  • Blood glucose — especially in diabetic patients or with high doses
  • Blood pressure and fluid status; weight
  • Signs of infection (fever may be masked by corticosteroids)
  • BMD if prolonged use (≥3 months) — consider bisphosphonate prophylaxis
  • Eye pressure (IOP) if prolonged use
Counselling
  • Take in the morning with food to reduce GI upset and minimise sleep disturbance.
  • Do NOT stop suddenly — taper the dose gradually as directed. Stopping abruptly can cause adrenal crisis.
  • Carry a steroid card or medical alert if on prolonged therapy.
  • Report immediately: fever or signs of infection (the drug can mask fever).
  • Monitor blood glucose more frequently if diabetic.
  • Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D intake for bone protection.
  • Mood changes and insomnia are common — report severe psychiatric symptoms.
Budesonide (Enteric-coated)Topically Active Corticosteroid — Reduced Systemic Exposure
Dose (Crohn)
  • Entocort / Budenofalk capsules (Crohn — ileocaecal): 9 mg PO once daily in the morning, just before food, for up to 8 weeksTaper over 2–4 weeks after induction. Children >12 years.
  • If unable to swallow Budenofalk capsules: Can be opened and contents swallowed whole with a glass of water (do not chew granules)
Mechanism of Action
Potent topically active corticosteroid with high first-pass hepatic metabolism (~90%) → much lower systemic exposure than equivalent dose of prednisolone → reduced systemic steroid adverse effects while maintaining local GI anti-inflammatory effects.
Indications
  • Mild-moderate ileocaecal Crohn disease — preferred over prednisolone for this indication
  • Also used in microscopic colitis
  • Budesonide MMX (different formulation) used in UC
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Still has some systemic steroid effects — not completely systemic-effect-free
  • Do NOT substitute standard budesonide for budesonide MMX (different formulation/release characteristics)
  • NOT for maintenance therapy
  • Use with caution in hepatic impairment (↓ first-pass metabolism → ↑ systemic exposure)
  • Same general steroid precautions apply (diabetes, infection, osteoporosis, etc.) — but lower risk
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Nausea, dyspepsia
  • Headache
  • Less common steroid effects than prednisolone (acne, oedema, mood changes) but still possible
Adverse Effects
💡
High first-pass hepatic metabolism (~90%) → much lower systemic exposure than prednisolone. Adverse effects infrequent and usually mild, but adrenal suppression may still occur even with short courses.
Common (>1%)
  • Headache, nausea, dyspepsia
Important
  • HPA axis suppression — may occur even with short oral courses; taper dose, do not stop suddenly
  • Switching from high-dose systemic CS to budesonide — requires cautious tailoring to avoid adrenal crisis
Monitoring
  • Reassess response at 4–8 weeks; if insufficient → escalate or specialist review
Counselling
  • Take in the morning just before food. Swallow capsules whole — do not crush or chew (enteric coating is important).
  • If unable to swallow Budenofalk capsules, open them and swallow the contents whole with a glass of water — do not chew the granules.
  • This form of steroid has fewer body-wide side effects than standard prednisolone because it works locally in the intestine.
  • Do NOT stop suddenly — taper as directed.
  • Short-term induction only — not for long-term or maintenance treatment.
Azathioprine / Mercaptopurine / TioguanineImmunomodulator — Purine Antimetabolite
Doses
  • Azathioprine: 2–2.5 mg/kg PO daily (range 1–3 mg/kg/day)Take with food to reduce stomach upset.
  • Mercaptopurine: 1–1.5 mg/kg PO once dailyRound to nearest 25 mg half-tablet. Take on empty stomach (1h before or 2h after food) — avoid dairy products.
  • Tioguanine (if aza/mercaptopurine not tolerated): 20 mg PO once or twice dailyCross-resistance with mercaptopurine — if one is ineffective, the other is likely also ineffective.
Mechanism of Action
Azathioprine → converted to mercaptopurine → converted to thioguanine nucleotides (6-TGN). 6-TGN interfere with purine synthesis → ↓ lymphocyte proliferation → immunosuppression + anti-inflammatory. Tioguanine → directly metabolised to 6-TGN.
Indications
  • Crohn disease — maintenance therapy (frequent relapses, corticosteroid-dependent)
  • UC — maintenance therapy
  • Transplant immunosuppression
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Hypersensitivity to azathioprine or mercaptopurine — contraindicated
  • TPMT / NUDT15 genotype testing BEFORE starting — poor metabolisers: negligible enzyme activity → myelosuppression at standard doses → avoid or significantly reduce dose
  • Renal impairment (CrCl <20 mL/min) — use with caution
  • Hepatic impairment — use with caution
  • Bone marrow impairment — caution
  • Tioguanine: cross-resistance with mercaptopurine — if one ineffective, other likely also ineffective
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Dose-related myelosuppression (leucopenia)
  • GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, anorexia)
  • Alopecia
  • Mouth ulceration
Infrequent / Serious
  • Hepatotoxicity (↑ LFTs, hepatitis)
  • Lymphopenia, pancytopenia
  • Pancreatitis (azathioprine — idiosyncratic; usually early)
  • Infection susceptibility
Rare
  • Lymphoma (long-term use), non-melanoma skin cancer
Monitoring
  • TPMT / NUDT15 genotyping before starting
  • FBC: weekly for first 2 months → monthly thereafter. Reduce or stop if white count low or falling rapidly.
  • LFTs: baseline and regularly (watch for hepatitis)
  • Monitor for pancreatitis (severe abdominal pain) — early drug hypersensitivity reaction
  • Thiopurine metabolite levels if suspected shunting or inadequate response
  • Sun protection — ↑ photosensitivity and skin cancer risk
Counselling
  • Swallow tablets whole. Take with food to reduce GI upset (azathioprine). Mercaptopurine: empty stomach.
  • Mercaptopurine: avoid dairy products (may reduce absorption).
  • Seek urgent review for: temperature >38°C, infection, bruising/bleeding, yellowing of skin/eyes (jaundice), severe abdominal pain.
  • Protect skin from sun — wear protective clothing and SPF30+ sunscreen.
  • Regular blood tests are essential — do not skip them.
  • Takes weeks to months to show full effect — be patient.
Methotrexate + Folic Acid (Crohn)Immunomodulator — csDMARD
Dose (Crohn Maintenance)
  • Methotrexate: 10–25 mg PO, SC, or IM once weekly on the same day each week
  • Folic acid: 5–10 mg PO once weekly on a DIFFERENT day
  • Assess alcohol intake before starting; investigate any pulmonary symptoms immediately
⚠️
Crohn-specific: Used as alternative to thiopurines when aza/merca not tolerated or ineffective. If Crohn is refractory to methotrexate → refer specialist for biological therapy.
Mechanism of Action
Folate antagonist → inhibits dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR) → ↓ purine and pyrimidine synthesis → ↓ rapidly proliferating immune cells → ↓ inflammation. Also increases adenosine release → additional anti-inflammatory effect.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Pregnancy & breastfeeding — absolutely contraindicated (teratogenic, category X)
  • Significant renal impairment — avoid (methotrexate accumulates)
  • Significant hepatic impairment — avoid
  • Alcohol — avoid (synergistic hepatotoxicity risk)
  • Bone marrow suppression — caution
  • NSAIDs — may ↑ methotrexate levels (↓ renal clearance); use with caution
  • Trimethoprim — folate antagonist; ↑ methotrexate toxicity; avoid combination
  • Reliable contraception required in BOTH males and females during treatment and ≥3 months after stopping
Adverse Effects
Common
  • GI: nausea, vomiting, mouth ulcers, diarrhoea (↓ with folic acid co-prescription)
  • Fatigue, headache, alopecia, photosensitivity
  • ↑ Liver enzymes (dose-related; folic acid reduces risk)
Serious / Rare
  • Myelosuppression (pancytopenia) — dose-reduce or stop; folate deficiency worsens risk
  • Hepatotoxicity / hepatic fibrosis — cumulative dose-related; monitor LFTs; consider fibroscan if cumulative dose >1.5 g
  • Methotrexate pneumonitis — dry cough, dyspnoea, fever → STOP IMMEDIATELY and refer urgently
  • Teratogenicity
Monitoring
  • FBC + LFTs + renal function: baseline → every 2–4 weeks for first 3 months → every 3 months for 1 year → every 6 months
  • Chest X-ray at baseline
  • Reduce or stop if: WBC <3.5 × 10⁹/L, platelets <150 × 10⁹/L, LFTs >2× upper limit of normal
  • Investigate any new pulmonary symptoms immediately (pneumonitis risk)
Counselling
  • ONCE WEEKLY only — never daily. Take on the same day each week. Fatal errors have occurred with daily dosing.
  • Always take folic acid on a different day from methotrexate.
  • Avoid all alcohol during treatment.
  • Reliable contraception required — both males and females must avoid conception during treatment and for ≥3 months after stopping.
  • Seek urgent medical attention for: fever, unusual bruising/bleeding, mouth sores, shortness of breath, or new cough.
  • Tell your doctor and pharmacist about all other medications — especially trimethoprim and NSAIDs.
  • Use sun protection — photosensitivity risk.
  • Regular blood tests are mandatory — do not skip them.
Advanced Therapies (Crohn) — Infliximab, Adalimumab, Vedolizumab, Ustekinumab, UpadacitinibBiological / Advanced Therapy — Moderate-Severe Crohn
Doses
  • Infliximab (TNFα): 5 mg/kg IV at 0, 2, 6 weeks → every 8 weeks maintenanceOften combined with azathioprine. First-line if IV CS fails or fistulising disease.
  • Adalimumab (TNFα): 160 mg SC at week 0 → 80 mg at week 2 → 40 mg every 2 weeks maintenance
  • Vedolizumab (gut-selective integrin antagonist): 300 mg IV at 0, 2, 6 weeks → every 8 weeks; or switch to SC after inductionSlower onset than TNFα; fewer systemic infections
  • Ustekinumab (IL-12/23 inhibitor): Single IV induction dose (weight-based 260–520 mg) → 90 mg SC every 12 weeks maintenance

Mechanism of Action
  • Infliximab / Adalimumab: Anti-TNF-α monoclonal antibodies → ↓ inflammatory signalling → ↓ GI inflammation. Infliximab also effective for fistulising disease.
  • Vedolizumab: Binds α4β7 integrin on leucocytes → blocks binding to MAdCAM-1 in GI tract → ↓ lymphocyte trafficking into gut → gut-selective immunosuppression (less systemic)
  • Ustekinumab: Inhibits IL-12 and IL-23 → ↓ T-cell-mediated inflammatory pathways → ↓ GI inflammation
  • Upadacitinib: Selective JAK inhibitor → ↓ downstream cytokine signalling → ↓ intestinal inflammation. Oral.
Shared Precautions & Contraindications
  • Serious/untreated infection — contraindicated
  • Active TB — contraindicated; screen before starting
  • Active Hepatitis B — contraindicated; screen before starting
  • Live vaccines — avoid during treatment
  • Infliximab: moderate-severe heart failure, demyelinating disease — caution/avoid
  • Antibodies to drug (anti-drug antibodies) can reduce response + ↑ infusion reactions (infliximab)
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Infections (URTI, other)
  • Infusion/injection site reactions
  • Headache, arthralgia
Rare / Serious
  • TB reactivation, serious opportunistic infections
  • Malignancy (lymphoma — especially with TNF-α)
  • Vedolizumab: hypersensitivity reaction (GI symptoms, headache, dizziness, itch)
Monitoring
  • Screen for TB and Hepatitis B before starting
  • Check FBC, LFTs, infection status
  • Infliximab: stop if no response within 14 weeks (UC); consider monitoring drug levels and antibody levels
  • Monitor for infection throughout treatment
Counselling
  • Report fever or signs of infection urgently — especially cough, persistent fever, or unusual symptoms.
  • Check with doctor before getting any vaccines — live vaccines avoided during treatment.
  • Consider immunisation before starting treatment.
  • Infliximab: tell doctor/nurse immediately if: shortness of breath, chest tightness, rash, or chills during or after infusion (infusion reactions).
  • Vedolizumab: if anaphylaxis occurs → further use is contraindicated.

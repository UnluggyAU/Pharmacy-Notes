Fibromyalgia

Chronic widespread non-inflammatory musculoskeletal pain with fatigue, sleep disturbance, and cognitive symptoms. Central sensitisation is the dominant mechanism. Non-pharmacological management is the foundation of treatment.

🔬

Pathophysiology

💡
The pathophysiology of fibromyalgia is complex and poorly understood. A biopsychosocial understanding is preferred — however, this does not imply that symptoms are a direct consequence of psychological distress. The pain is real.
🧠 Central Sensitisation
  • Dominant mechanism — the nervous system's "volume knob" is turned up
  • Altered neurotransmitter levels in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)
  • Abnormalities on functional imaging studies
  • ↑ pain signal amplification throughout the CNS
  • Pain occurs without ongoing tissue damage or nerve lesion
🔄 Sensitisation Phenomena
  • Allodynia: pain from normally non-painful stimuli (light touch, clothing)
  • Hyperalgesia: exaggerated pain response to normally painful stimuli
  • Pain that does not follow a consistent diurnal pattern (unlike inflammatory arthritis)
  • Symptom fluctuation — "two steps forward, one step back"
🧩 Not Caused By
  • Tissue damage — no structural pathology
  • Joint inflammation — not inflammatory arthritis
  • Nerve injury — no demonstrable nerve lesion
  • Fibromyalgia is not progressive — does not cause permanent damage to muscles, bones, or joints
  • It is a nociplastic pain disorder
⚠️

Risk Factors & Associations

👤 Risk Factors
  • Female sex (more commonly affected)
  • Genetic predisposition — family history of widespread pain
  • History of "growing pains" or other childhood painful disorders
  • Psychological distress
  • Sleep disorders
  • Trauma or stressful life events
🔗 Common Comorbidities
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS)
  • Irritable bladder
  • Depression and anxiety
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome
  • Migraines and headache disorders
  • Osteoarthritis and rheumatoid arthritis (can coexist)
  • Restless legs syndrome
⚠️ Important Points
  • Fibromyalgia frequently coexists with other rheumatological diseases
  • Always assess for joint inflammation and signs of inflammatory rheumatic disease
  • Symptoms from comorbidities may be difficult to distinguish from fibromyalgia symptoms
  • Any change in symptom nature or pattern → thorough re-assessment to exclude comorbid illness
🩺

Clinical Features

🔥 Pain
  • Widespread — soft tissues and joints
  • Fluctuating severity — not consistently diurnal
  • Allodynia often present (light touch causes pain)
  • May initially present as regional pain
  • Diffuse soft-tissue tenderness on examination
😴 Fatigue & Sleep
  • Fatigue — often severe and persistent
  • Non-restorative sleep — wake unrefreshed
  • Insomnia
  • Sleep dysfunction both a consequence of pain and a perpetuating factor
🧠 Cognitive & Other
  • "Fibrofog" — cognitive clouding, impaired concentration and memory
  • Depression and/or anxiety (common comorbidity)
  • Gastrointestinal dysfunction (IBS symptoms)
  • Urogenital dysfunction (irritable bladder)
  • Joint hypermobility — common but rarely indicates serious heritable disorder
Common tender sites: Lateral epicondyles, trapezius muscles, anserine bursae. Soft-tissue tenderness is diffuse. Pain induced by sphygmomanometer cuff inflation is a useful screening tool for allodynia. Dermographism may be present.
🔍

Diagnosis

💡
Fibromyalgia is a clinical diagnosis. There are no characteristic laboratory or imaging abnormalities. The absence of objective findings can be challenging — this does not mean the condition is not real.
📋 Diagnostic Criteria (ACR)
  • Widespread pain (bilateral upper and lower body, axial)
  • Other typical fibromyalgia symptoms (fatigue, sleep disturbance, cognitive symptoms)
  • A formal tender point count is NOT required
  • Criteria support but do not replace clinical judgement
  • Symptoms exist on a spectrum and may vary within an individual over time
🔬 Investigations
  • Routine investigations usually not required if presentation is typical
  • Unnecessary investigations risk: overdiagnosis, false-positive findings, and patient distress
  • Always screen for inflammatory rheumatic disease — FBC, ESR/CRP, ANA, RF if clinically indicated
  • Thyroid function (exclude hypothyroidism)
  • Imaging only if will change management
🔄 When to Refer
  • Diagnosis uncertain or presentation atypical → rheumatologist
  • Refractory symptoms despite adequate treatment → rheumatologist or pain service
  • Prominent mood/anxiety features, adjustment disorder → psychologist or psychiatrist
  • Rheumatology referral is NOT required for every patient
🎯

Treatment Goals

Primary Goals
  • Improve function and quality of life
  • Reduce symptom impact (pain, fatigue, sleep, cognitive)
  • Establish and sustain self-management
Realistic Expectations
  • Complete pain elimination is usually not possible
  • Goal = "muffle the alarm" so it interferes less with daily life
  • Fibromyalgia is not progressive and does not cause permanent damage
Patient Education (Core Messages)
  • Pain is real — not caused by tissue damage or psychological distress
  • Fibromyalgia is an oversensitive alarm system
  • Symptoms fluctuate — this is expected and does not mean worsening
  • Management aims to improve function, not achieve a pain-free state
🏃

Non-Pharmacological Management

🚨
Non-pharmacological management is the FOUNDATION of fibromyalgia treatment. A combination of non-pharmacological approaches is most likely to reduce symptom impact and improve function. Drug therapy provides only modest benefit.
1
Foundation — all patients (multidimensional combination is most effective)
Physical Activity (Graded Exercise)
Graded Aerobic Exercise
Walking, pool-based exercise, cycling, gentle aerobic activity — any form the patient can realistically sustain
✅ ↓ Pain, ↓ fatigue, ↑ quality of life — most evidence for aerobic exercise ✅ Start very light (walking, pool exercise) → increase duration and intensity in small increments over several months ✅ Progress based on time spent, not pain level — prevents unhelpful boom-bust cycles ⚠️ Pacing is essential: start below current tolerance (~80%) → ↑ by 10–20% per week; time-contingent, not pain-contingent
2
Foundation — all patients
Psychological Techniques
CBT, ACT, Mindfulness, Relaxation
✅ CBT — may ↓ pain and ↑ function; helps identify and challenge unhelpful thoughts and behaviours ✅ Mindfulness-based approaches — may reduce pain intensity and improve mood and function ✅ Good sleep practices are particularly valuable — address consistent wake time, screen time, caffeine ✅ Clinicians can help patients: reduce situational stress, identify coping strategies, set realistic goals
3
Foundation — all patients
Social Connection & Supported Self-Management
Social Reconnection & Peer Support
✅ Social connection and meaningful activity are core components of self-management ✅ Fibromyalgia support groups available through state affiliates of Arthritis Australia ✅ Strong therapeutic relationship between clinician and patient is crucial ✅ Include significant others (partner, family) in education and self-management planning

Pharmacological Treatment

⚠️
Drug therapy usually provides only modest benefit in fibromyalgia. Medicines should be used together with non-pharmacological strategies, not as a standalone treatment. Multiple drugs may need to be trialled sequentially due to inadequate response or adverse effects. Note: TCAs, gabapentinoids, and duloxetine are not TGA-approved specifically for fibromyalgia — used off-label.

🚨
Drugs NOT recommended in fibromyalgia:
  • Opioids (buprenorphine, codeine, fentanyl, morphine, oxycodone) — do NOT effectively manage fibromyalgia pain; may worsen central sensitisation over time; carry significant harm
  • NSAIDs and paracetamol — generally little benefit in nociplastic/fibromyalgia pain
  • Exceptions with limited role (specialist prescription only): tramadol, tapentadol, low-dose naltrexone
  • Cannabinoids — limited evidence; not clearly effective; not well tolerated; role unknown

Drug Summaries

TCAs — Amitriptyline, Nortriptyline, DosulepinTricyclic Antidepressant — First-line in Fibromyalgia
Doses (Fibromyalgia)
  • Amitriptyline: 10–25 mg PO in early evening initially → ↑ by ≤25 mg every 2–4 weeks → max 50 mg each eveningMuch lower than antidepressant doses. Do not increase beyond fibromyalgia maximum without specialist advice.
  • Dosulepin: 25 mg PO in early evening initially → ↑ by ≤25 mg every 2–4 weeks → max 75 mg each evening⚠️ Higher toxicity in overdose than other TCAs — avoid starting in new patients where possible; prescribe small quantities in patients at overdose risk.
  • Nortriptyline: 10–25 mg PO in early evening initially → ↑ by ≤25 mg every 2–4 weeks → max 100 mg each eveningPreferred in elderly — fewer anticholinergic effects, less sedation, less hypotension. Serum drug concentration can guide dosage.
  • Take in early evening (not at bedtime) — allows drowsiness to facilitate sleep onset
  • Titrate in increments every 2–3 days as needed
Mechanism of Action
Inhibit reuptake of noradrenaline and serotonin → ↑ descending inhibitory pain pathways → ↓ central sensitisation and pain amplification. Also block cholinergic, histaminergic, alpha1-adrenergic receptors (source of most adverse effects). Antihistaminergic effect → ↑ sleep quality (particularly valuable in fibromyalgia).
Why First-line in Fibromyalgia
  • May reduce pain AND improve sleep — dual benefit at low doses
  • Generally well tolerated at low fibromyalgia doses
  • Well established, low cost
  • Note: not TGA-approved specifically for fibromyalgia — used off-label
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Second- or third-degree heart block, severe ischaemic heart disease, recent MI — contraindicated
  • MAOIs — contraindicated (serotonin syndrome)
  • High overdose/suicide risk — prescribe small quantities; overdose is potentially fatal. Dosulepin: higher toxicity in overdose than other TCAs — avoid in new patients at risk.
  • QT prolongation risk — TCAs prolong QT; avoid if uncorrectable QT risk factors
  • Epilepsy — ↓ seizure threshold; caution, low doses, slow titration
  • BPH, narrow-angle glaucoma — anticholinergic effects may worsen
  • Elderly — ↑ falls, confusion; prefer nortriptyline (fewer anticholinergic effects)
  • Bipolar disorder — may trigger manic episode
  • Hepatic impairment — halve dose in severe impairment
  • Check BP (supine and standing) before and after starting, and after each dose change
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%) — at fibromyalgia doses
  • Sedation (next-day grogginess) — usually improves after 1–2 weeks; take in early evening
  • Dry mouth, constipation, urinary hesitancy, blurred vision (anticholinergic)
  • Orthostatic hypotension, sinus tachycardia — ↑ falls risk; especially elderly
  • Weight gain, tremor, dizziness, sweating, agitation, insomnia, confusion
  • Sexual dysfunction, anticholinergic delirium (elderly/Parkinson's)
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Slowed cardiac conduction, arrhythmias (higher doses)
  • Nausea, manic episodes (susceptible patients)
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Blood dyscrasias, hepatitis, paralytic ileus
  • Prolonged QT, seizures, hyponatraemia (SIADH), increased intraocular pressure
Monitoring
  • BP (supine and standing) before starting, after starting, and after each dose change
  • Review efficacy and tolerability after 4–6 weeks at each dose level — if no benefit by maximum dose, taper and discontinue
  • ECG if cardiac risk (especially at doses >100 mg in elderly, >200 mg in adults <60 years)
  • Monitor suicidal ideation early in treatment, particularly in young people
Counselling
  • Take in the early evening (not at bedtime) — allows drowsiness to be useful for sleep onset without causing next-morning grogginess.
  • Drowsiness and dry mouth often lessen or disappear after about 7 days — discuss with doctor if troublesome.
  • You may feel dizzy on standing — get up gradually; sit or lie down if dizzy.
  • Do not stop suddenly — reduce dose gradually to avoid withdrawal effects.
  • Avoid alcohol — increases CNS depression.
  • May take 4–6 weeks to see full benefit at each dose.
Gabapentinoids — Gabapentin & PregabalinAdjuvant — Second-line in Fibromyalgia
⚠️
Misuse and dependence are increasingly reported. Monitor for signs of misuse (dose escalation, drug-seeking behaviour). History of substance misuse or psychiatric disorders — ↑ risk; avoid if possible. Use lowest effective dose for shortest time.
Doses (Fibromyalgia)
  • Gabapentin: 100–300 mg PO in early evening initially → ↑ to TDS as toleratedDo not increase more frequently than every 4 days. Maximum in fibromyalgia: 2400 mg/day in 3 divided doses.
  • Pregabalin: 25–75 mg PO in early evening initially → ↑ to BD after 3–7 days → slowly titrateMaximum in fibromyalgia: 450 mg/day in 2 divided doses (lower than neuropathic pain maximum of 600 mg/day). Uneven dosing (larger evening dose) may ↑ sleep and ↓ daytime sedation.
  • Reasonable to switch between gabapentin and pregabalin if one is ineffective — individual response may differ
Renal Dose Adjustment
  • Gabapentin: CrCl 50–80: 0.6–1.8 g/day | CrCl 30–50: 300–900 mg/day | CrCl <30: ≤600 mg/day
  • Pregabalin: CrCl 30–60: max 300 mg/day | CrCl 15–30: max 150 mg/day | CrCl <15: max 75 mg/day
Mechanism
Bind to α2-δ protein subunit of voltage-dependent calcium channels → ↓ release of excitatory neurotransmitters (glutamate, substance P, noradrenaline) → ↓ central sensitisation and pain signal amplification. Note: structurally related to GABA but do NOT significantly affect GABA or its receptors.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Opioids or CNS depressants — ↑ risk of CNS and respiratory depression; deaths reported
  • History of substance misuse or psychiatric disorders — ↑ misuse/dependence risk; avoid if possible
  • Pregabalin — pregnancy: avoid if possible; ↑ risk of major congenital malformations; ensure effective contraception
  • Renal impairment — dose reduction required for both (renally excreted)
  • Pregabalin — heart failure: use with caution (rare worsening)
  • Elderly/frail — ↑ falls and sedation; lower doses, slower titration
  • Do not stop abruptly — taper over ≥1 week
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%) — Class
  • Drowsiness, sedation, dizziness, fatigue, ataxia, vertigo
  • Confusion, amnesia, euphoria (contributes to misuse potential)
  • Peripheral oedema, weight gain, dry mouth, blurred vision
Common — Pregabalin specific
  • Irritability, lethargy, insomnia, constipation, muscle cramps, arthralgia, depression
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Agitation, rash, sexual dysfunction
  • Pregabalin: hallucinations, cognitive impairment, urinary incontinence, tachycardia, first-degree heart block
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Respiratory depression (with opioids/CNS depressants), anaphylaxis/angioedema, SJS
  • Rhabdomyolysis; pregabalin: heart failure, pancreatitis
Deprescribing
  • <3 months: reduce by 25–30% per week (over ≥1 week)
  • >3 months: reduce by 25–30% every 2 weeks
Counselling
  • Take the largest dose at night — reduces daytime sedation; drowsiness usually improves after 1–2 weeks.
  • Do not stop suddenly — always reduce gradually as directed.
  • Avoid alcohol and other sedating medications.
  • Take care with driving and machinery, especially at initiation and after dose changes.
  • Pregabalin: use effective contraception. Do not crush or chew capsules.
  • Allow 4–6 weeks to assess benefit at each dose level.
DuloxetineSNRI — Second-line in Fibromyalgia
Dose (Fibromyalgia)
  • 30 mg PO daily initially (morning)
  • Increase after 1 month to 60 mg daily
  • Titrate according to response and tolerability
  • Maximum maintenance dose: 60 mg twice daily (120 mg/day)
Mechanism & Advantages
Inhibits reuptake of serotonin AND noradrenaline → ↑ descending inhibitory pain pathways → ↓ central sensitisation. Dual analgesic and antidepressant effect makes it particularly useful when there is a significant emotional or depressive component to fibromyalgia symptoms.
Preferred When
  • Comorbid depression or anxiety is prominent
  • Patient has obesity (less weight gain than TCAs)
  • TCAs not tolerated (e.g. anticholinergic effects)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • MAOIs — contraindicated (serotonin toxicity); wait ≥14 days after stopping MAOI before starting
  • Hepatic impairment — contraindicated
  • Strong CYP1A2 inhibitors (e.g. fluvoxamine) — contraindicated
  • Renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min) — use 30 mg/day only
  • Bleeding risk — ↓ platelet aggregation; caution with anticoagulants and NSAIDs
  • Bipolar disorder — may provoke mania; angle-closure glaucoma risk
  • Risk of hyponatraemia — especially elderly; check Na at baseline and early treatment
  • Do not stop suddenly — taper gradually (discontinuation syndrome: dizziness, "brain zaps", flu-like)
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Nausea — most common; usually transient (1–2 weeks); take with food; 30 mg starting dose ↓ risk
  • Dry mouth, constipation, yawning, sweating, decreased appetite, rash
  • Dizziness, headache, somnolence, insomnia, blurred vision, tremor, weakness
  • ↑ BP — less common with duloxetine than venlafaxine; monitor
  • Sexual dysfunction (impotence, ↓ libido)
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Orthostatic hypotension, palpitations, tachycardia, ↑ LFTs
  • Hyponatraemia (SIADH), urinary retention
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Liver failure, GI bleeding, SJS, cutaneous vasculitis, seizures
Counselling
  • Take with food in the morning to reduce nausea.
  • Allow 4–8 weeks to assess full benefit at each dose.
  • Do not stop suddenly — always taper gradually as directed.
  • Report unusual bleeding, significant mood changes, or increased BP.

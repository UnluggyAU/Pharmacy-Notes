Renal Disorders

AKI, CKD, Renal Replacement Therapy, Renal Bone Disease, and Post-Transplant Pharmacology. Critical for high-risk prescribing — many common drugs are nephrotoxic or require dose adjustment in renal impairment.

🔬

Core Kidney Principles

eGFR (estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate): Standard measure of kidney function. Calculated from serum creatinine, age, sex, and weight (CKD-EPI equation). Used to stage CKD and guide drug dosing.

Key measurements:
• Creatinine clearance (CrCl) — used for drug dosing (Cockcroft-Gault formula). Different from eGFR.
• Urine albumin:creatinine ratio (ACR) — assesses proteinuria (marker of glomerular damage and CKD progression risk)
• Urinary protein:creatinine ratio (PCR) — similar use
💡
Important distinction: AKI = acute, sudden deterioration in kidney function (hours to days). CKD = chronic, progressive, irreversible loss of kidney function (months to years). Both can coexist (AKI on CKD). Markers of chronic injury (anaemia, hyperphosphataemia, small kidneys on imaging) help differentiate. CKD does not resolve, though progression can be slowed.
⚡

Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

Definition: A sudden drop in kidney function (hours to days) — kidneys can't clear wastes, balance fluids/electrolytes, or manage acid-base properly.
If CrCl falls to 10–30 mL/min → renal clearance drops to ~20% of normal. Can be asymptomatic even with 90% function lost.
AKI Symptoms (as disease progresses):
🚽 Urine Changes
  • ↓ Urine volume (oliguria)
  • Discolouration
  • Blood or pus in urine
🤢 General
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Appetite loss
  • Fatigue and lethargy
  • Weakness
  • Darkened skin
  • Muscle cramps
💧 Fluid
  • Water retention
  • Peripheral oedema
KDIGO Diagnostic Criteria (any of):
  • • Serum creatinine ↑ by ≥26.5 μmol/L within 48 hours
  • • Serum creatinine ↑ to ≥1.5× baseline within prior 7 days
  • • Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h for ≥6 hours

Causes of AKI:
⬆️ Pre-Renal (↓ Perfusion)
  • Dehydration, poor fluid intake, vomiting
  • Haemorrhage, sepsis
  • Heart failure, cardiogenic shock
  • Burns
  • Hepatorenal syndrome
  • Drugs: NSAIDs (↓ renal prostaglandins → ↓ perfusion), ACEi/ARB (↓ efferent arteriolar tone → ↓ GFR), diuretics (↓ volume)
🔴 Intrinsic Renal
  • Acute tubular necrosis (ATN) — ischaemia or toxins (most common intrinsic)
  • Glomerulonephritis, vasculitis
  • Pyelonephritis
  • Acute interstitial nephritis (AIN) — drug-induced: NSAIDs, penicillins, cephalosporins, PPIs, allopurinol, rifampicin
  • Contrast nephropathy (see below)
  • Nephrotoxins: aminoglycosides, vancomycin, ciclosporin, tacrolimus, cisplatin
  • Rhabdomyolysis, haemolysis
⬇️ Post-Renal (Obstruction)
  • BPH (benign prostatic hyperplasia)
  • Bilateral renal calculi (kidney stones)
  • Blood clot
  • Pelvic malignancy
  • Retroperitoneal fibrosis
⚠️
Triple Whammy: NSAID + ACEi/ARB + Diuretic → all three lower eGFR → markedly ↑ AKI risk (synergistic ↓ renal perfusion). Avoid this combination, especially in elderly, dehydrated, or unwell patients.
🔬
Contrast Nephropathy:
Used in: cardiac angiogram/angioplasty, diagnostic CT scans.
Mechanism: Renal ischaemia (↓ blood flow or ↑ oxygen demand) + direct tubular epithelial cell toxicity.

Prevention:
  • Assess necessity — is contrast truly required?
  • Use iso-osmolar non-ionic contrast and minimise dose
  • IV hydration with NaCl 0.9% before and after (benefit limited by cardiac/renal function)
  • Withhold nephrotoxic drugs 24–48h before: NSAIDs, COX-2 inhibitors, ACEi/ARB, metformin and other antidiabetic drugs
AKI Management by Complication:
💡
General supportive priorities: Remove nephrotoxic agents; reverse underlying cause if possible; maintain homeostasis; correct biochemical and haematological abnormalities; control dietary intake.

Sequential Hyperkalaemia Treatment
💡
Treat in order of priority: 1. Cardiac membrane stabilisation → 2. Shift K⁺ into cells → 3. Remove K⁺ from body
1. Calcium Gluconate10 mL of 10% solution IV over 2–3 min (continuous ECG monitoring)
✅ Membrane stabiliser — does NOT lower K⁺; protects myocardium from hyperkalaemia-induced arrhythmia⚠️ Repeat every 5–10 min if ECG changes persist; use with extreme caution in digoxin toxicity

✅ Drives K⁺ into cells; onset 15–30 min; additive with insulin+glucose⚠️ Tachycardia, tremor; ~40% of patients are non-responsive

L
Metformin-Associated Lactic Acidosis (MALA)
Supportive Treatment + Remove Cause
⚠️
Metformin-associated lactic acidosis is life-threatening.

Mechanism: Metformin → ↑ lactate production + ↓ hepatic lactate uptake → ↑ anaerobic glycolysis → pyruvate → lactate → ↑ H⁺ → ↑ anion gap metabolic acidosis. Also ↓ clearance in CKD or poor liver perfusion → lactate stays elevated → worsening acidosis.

Risk scenarios: Contrast + metformin combination; AKI; sepsis/shock (↓ O₂ delivery); severe dehydration; hepatic impairment.

Management:
  • Supportive therapy — airway, breathing, circulation
  • Remove the cause — withhold metformin immediately
  • Bicarbonate — controversial; may use if severe acidosis
  • Dialysis may be required to clear metformin and correct acidosis
Prevention: Withhold metformin before contrast procedures; avoid in eGFR <30; use with caution in eGFR 30–45.
D
Drug management in AKI
Stop / Withhold Nephrotoxic & Renally-Cleared Drugs
🚨
Stop or withhold in AKI:
  • NSAIDs / COX-2 inhibitors — stop (↓ renal perfusion, worsen AKI)
  • ACEi / ARBs — withhold during AKI (↓ GFR via efferent vasodilation)
  • Metformin — withhold if CrCl <30 or acutely unwell (lactic acidosis risk); withhold before contrast
  • K⁺-sparing diuretics — avoid if K⁺ elevated
  • Aminoglycosides — avoid if possible; if essential, single daily dosing + TDM
  • Vancomycin — dose-adjust using AUC-guided TDM
  • Digoxin, lithium — narrow TI; accumulate in renal failure; monitor levels

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

Definition: Kidney damage (structural or functional abnormality) present for >3 months, with implications for health. Includes: eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73m² for >3 months, OR albuminuria (ACR ≥3 mg/mmol), OR other markers of kidney damage (haematuria, structural abnormality, pathological abnormality).
⚠️
Staging rule: Stage 1–2 is NOT classified as CKD unless there is also evidence of kidney damage (e.g. haematuria, structural abnormality, or pathological abnormality). GFR alone is insufficient to diagnose CKD at these stages.

Memory aid (GFR cut-offs): G2→G3a→G3b→G4→G5 = 60 → 45 → 30 → 15. Decrease by 15 each time except first step (60→45 = 15, 45→30 = 15, 30→15 = 15).
Planning RRT: Dialysis planning typically begins at eGFR ~20; most start at eGFR <12 (symptoms-guided, not threshold-guided). Takes 3–6 months to set up access — plan early.

💡
Both eGFR stage AND albuminuria category determine CKD risk. Higher ACR = higher progression risk at any given eGFR. ACEi/ARB reduces albuminuria and slows CKD progression in proteinuric disease.
Major Risk Factors for CKD:
🔝 Most Common Causes
  • Diabetes (up to ~50% when combined with hypertension/CVD)
  • Hypertension / cardiovascular disease
  • Glomerulonephritis (immune-mediated — FSGS, IgA nephropathy, lupus, vasculitis)
🔒 Other Causes
  • Polycystic kidney disease (genetic)
  • Reflux nephropathy (backflow)
  • Recurrent pyelonephritis
  • Renovascular disease
  • Drug-induced (NSAIDs, calcineurin inhibitors)
  • Obstruction / malignancy
⚠️ Risk Factors for Developing CKD
  • Diabetes
  • High blood pressure
  • Heart problems or stroke
  • Family history
  • Obesity
  • Smoking
  • Age ≥60 years
  • Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander peoples
  • History of AKI
CKD Complications & Management:
❤️ Cardiovascular Disease / Fluid Overload
  • CKD → ↑ CV mortality (leading cause of death in CKD) via RAAS activation, arrhythmias, LV hypertrophy, cardiac failure
  • Hyperphosphataemia → arterial stiffness, valve calcification; calciphylaxis in advanced CKD (severe, painful)
  • BP control: ACEi/ARB first-line (also slows progression in proteinuric CKD)
  • Diuretics for fluid overload (loop diuretics remain effective even at low GFR; higher doses may be needed)
  • Lipid management; fluid + sodium restriction
🧬 Hyperkalaemia
  • CKD → reduced K⁺ excretion → hyperkalaemia → cardiac arrhythmias
  • ACEi/ARB further ↑ K⁺ by ~0.5 mmol/L (but benefit usually outweighs risk — monitor carefully)
  • Potassium-sparing diuretics → hyperkalaemia risk in CKD — avoid if K⁺ elevated
  • Management: low-K⁺ diet; stop exacerbating drugs; resonium calcium; dialysis if severe
🩸 Anaemia of CKD
  • Cause: ↓ EPO production by failing kidneys + iron deficiency + inflammation
  • Symptoms: lethargy, dyspnoea, dizziness, palpitations
  • Target Hb: 100–120 g/L (avoid >130 g/L — ↑ CV events, hypertension)
  • Step 1 — Iron first: Perform iron studies before starting EPO. IV iron preferred (oral poorly absorbed in CKD). IM iron causes skin staining and is painful. Oral iron poorly absorbed.
  • Step 2 — EPO (ESA) if iron replete: Epoetin alfa/beta or darbepoetin — only if TSAT >20%, ferritin >100 μg/L. Monitor BP — EPO worsens hypertension.
🔴 Symptoms & Other CKD Features
  • Volume overload → mild oedema → life-threatening pulmonary oedema
  • Pruritus (itch) — can be severe; chlorphenamine may help symptomatically
  • Restless legs — leg jerks disrupt sleep; clonazepam 0.5–1 mg at night may help
  • Neurological: neuropathy, encephalopathy in advanced disease
  • Diet: Restrict high water-content foods (gravy, custard, fruit) to reduce fluid retention; restrict phosphate-rich foods (dairy, cola, nuts)
Renal Bone Disease (CKD-MBD) — Pathophysiology & Treatment
🔬
Pathophysiology chain:
↓ Kidney function → two parallel problems:
(1) ↓ phosphate excretion → ↑ serum phosphate → phosphate binds free Ca²⁺ → ↓ free Ca²⁺
(2) ↓ 25(OH)D → 1,25(OH)₂D (calcitriol) conversion → ↓ active vitamin D → ↓ gut Ca²⁺ absorption → ↓ free Ca²⁺

Both → ↑ PTH release (secondary hyperparathyroidism):
• PTH → ↑ bone resorption (releases Ca + phosphate from bone → blood)
• In healthy kidneys: PTH → ↑ phosphate excretion + ↑ calcitriol production → corrects imbalance
• In CKD: kidneys can't excrete phosphate OR convert vitamin D → vicious cycle perpetuates

Result: Continued ↑ PTH, ↑ phosphate, ↓ calcitriol, ↓ free Ca²⁺ → impaired bone mineralisation + high-turnover bone disease (osteitis fibrosa).

Long-standing secondary hyperparathyroidism → tertiary hyperparathyroidism (autonomous PTH secretion, often with hypercalcaemia).
Types of Renal Osteodystrophy (bone histology):
  • Osteitis fibrosa (~50%): High-turnover; caused by excessive PTH; bone broken down and rebuilt too fast
  • Osteomalacia (5–10%): Inadequate mineralisation of osteoid matrix; calcitriol deficiency
  • Adynamic bone disease (~20–25%): Low-turnover; over-suppression of PTH (iPTH + BSAP); associated with diabetes, old age, aluminium. Risk with aggressive cinacalcet/vitamin D use.
  • Mixed pathology (~10–15%)

Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)

💡
When to start RRT: No strict eGFR threshold — start when symptoms of kidney failure develop AND are expected to improve with dialysis (not just because GFR hits a number). Symptoms: severe fatigue, nausea, decreased appetite, shortness of breath. Planning typically begins at eGFR ~20; most start at eGFR <12. Takes 3–6 months to set up access — plan at least a year early.
✅ Consider Kidney Transplant
  • Best option for most eligible patients — better survival and QoL than dialysis
  • Living donor (family/friend): tends to last longer; can happen before dialysis starts
  • Deceased donor: long waiting lists (often many years)
⚖️ Consider Conservative Care
  • Not a transplant candidate; other comorbidities make dialysis burdensome
  • QoL and comfort trade-off — does not stop progression
  • Focus: symptom management, QoL, advance care planning
ModalityDetailsKey Points
Haemodialysis (HD) ~4 hours, 3×/week (in-centre most common). Blood pumped through artificial kidney (dialyser) — osmosis, diffusion, convection. Access: AV fistula (preferred — takes ~3 months to mature, plan ahead), graft, or tunnelled central line (↑ infection risk). Most common modality. AV fistula = gold standard access. Home HD available for selected patients (nocturnal HD for flexibility). Usually requires diet changes + fluid restriction.
CAPD (Continuous Ambulatory PD) ~4 manual exchanges/day using bags. Dialysate infused via catheter near belly button → left to dwell (waste diffuses into dialysate via peritoneum) → drained and replaced. Each cycle = one exchange. Home-based; patient moves freely between exchanges. Peritonitis = main complication. May preserve native urine output longer than HD.
APD (Automated PD) Machine (cycler) runs overnight exchanges (~7–10h) while asleep. During day, normal movement generally possible. Freedom during day — better fit for work/school. Otherwise similar to CAPD.
Kidney Transplant Living or deceased donor. Requires lifelong immunosuppression. Best QoL + survival. Waitlist often years for deceased donor.
Conservative Care Maximal medical management without RRT. Symptom management, QoL, advance care planning. Appropriate when dialysis risks/burdens outweigh benefits.
✅ Reasons to Choose PD
  • Flexibility and control; home-based
  • Avoid travel to centre 3×/week
  • Better fit for work, study, caring responsibilities
  • Easier travel; more independence
  • May preserve urine output longer
❌ Reasons Against PD / Choose HD
  • Difficulty self-managing or problem-solving reliably
  • Cognitive impairment; severe physical limitations
  • Alcohol/drug addiction; psychiatric disorders
  • Extensive prior abdominal surgery (contraindication to PD)
  • PD complications: peritonitis (infection), hernias (↑ intra-abdominal pressure)
  • Requires strong cleanliness technique — non-compliance → peritonitis

Post-Transplant Pharmacology

💡
Post-transplant immunosuppression prevents rejection but ↑ infection and malignancy risk. All decisions made in conjunction with transplant team. Overall medication compliance is a strong predictor of long-term outcomes.

Long-term Post-Op Maintenance:
⚠️
Standard triple immunosuppression: Tacrolimus + Mycophenolate mofetil (or mycophenolate sodium) + Prednisolone (often tapered and eventually withdrawn over months). Anticoagulants, analgesia, laxatives, and gastric ulcer/reflux prophylaxis added as needed.
⚠️
Tacrolimus — Critical Points: Narrow therapeutic index — TDM essential (target trough varies by time post-transplant). Many drug interactions via CYP3A4/P-glycoprotein. Do NOT switch brands without specialist advice (different formulations NOT interchangeable). Avoid grapefruit juice. Key adverse effects: ↑ BGL (new-onset DM post-transplant), nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity (tremor, PRES), hypertension, hyperkalaemia, alopecia, QT prolongation.

Drug Summaries

AKI-Specific Drugs: Frusemide, Sodium Bicarbonate, Calcium Gluconate, ResoniumAKI Supportive Therapy
Frusemide (Furosemide) — Loop Diuretic
  • Hypervolaemia in AKI: 40–80 mg IV (or PO if absorbing); may require higher doses
  • Renal bone disease oliguria/fluid overload: Up to 80–250 mg IV (concentrated infusion possible)
  • Oral: 40–80 mg PO daily-BD in CKD for fluid overload
MOA: Inhibits Na⁺-K⁺-2Cl⁻ cotransporter in thick ascending limb of loop of Henle → ↓ renal Na⁺ reabsorption → ↑ water excretion. Effective even at low GFR when thiazides are not.
Precautions: Anuric AKI — no role. Hypovolaemia. Hypokalaemia and hyponatraemia — monitor electrolytes. ↑ ototoxicity risk if given rapidly IV or with aminoglycosides.
Counselling: Take in morning (or morning + noon if twice daily) to avoid nocturia. Report muscle cramps, weakness, dizziness.
Sodium Bicarbonate
  • Metabolic acidosis (IV): 1–2 mmol/kg IV slowly; or per deficit (see arterial blood gas)
  • CKD acidosis prevention (PO): 500 mg – 1 g PO 3×/day; titrate to HCO₃⁻ ≥22 mmol/L
  • Urine alkalinisation: 1–2 g PO every 4 hours
MOA: Exogenous bicarbonate → buffers H⁺ → ↑ pH.
Precautions: Hypernatraemia, fluid overload — avoid (↑ Na⁺ and water load). Metabolic alkalosis risk. Interactions: can alter absorption of other drugs.
Counselling (PO): Take between meals if possible. Avoid in severe fluid retention.
Calcium Gluconate IV (Hyperkalaemia)
10 mL of 10% solution IV over 2–3 min under continuous ECG monitoring; repeat if ECG changes persist
MOA: Does NOT lower K⁺. Membrane stabiliser: raises cardiac cell action potential threshold, protecting against K⁺-induced arrhythmias.
Precautions: Digoxin toxicity — use with extreme caution (↑ calcium → ↑ digoxin toxicity risk). Monitor for bradycardia.
Calcium Resonium (Polystyrene Sulfonate)
15–30 g PO (with sorbitol 70% to prevent constipation) 3–4×/day; or 30 g as retention enema
MOA: Cation exchange resin — exchanges Na⁺ for K⁺ in GI tract → K⁺ excreted in faeces.
Precautions: Bowel disease, ileus — risk of intestinal necrosis (avoid). Hypomagnesaemia. Separate from other oral medications (reduces absorption).
Counselling: Can cause constipation — take with sorbitol or laxative. Takes hours to work (not for immediate acute treatment alone).
CKD Bone Disease: Phosphate Binders, Vit D Analogues, CinacalcetMineral Metabolism / Renal Bone Disease
Phosphate Binders
  • Calcium carbonate 1.25 g PO with meals (500 mg elemental Ca per dose)
  • Sevelamer carbonate 800–1600 mg PO 3×/day with meals (non-Ca, non-Al)
  • Lanthanum carbonate 250–1000 mg PO 3×/day with meals — chew or dissolve tablets
MOA: Bind dietary phosphate in GI tract → prevent absorption → ↓ serum phosphate.
Precautions (calcium-containing): Hypercalcaemia; metastatic calcification — avoid. Ca × phosphate product >5.4 mmol²/L² — avoid.
Precautions (sevelamer): Swallowing difficulties; bowel obstruction. Reduces absorption of some drugs (separate by 1–3h — especially quinolones, levothyroxine, fat-soluble vitamins, some anticoagulants). May cause GI upset.
Counselling: Take WITH meals (not before/after) — must be present in gut while food is being digested. If using lanthanum: chew tablets thoroughly or dissolve in water. Do not swallow calcium carbonate tablets without chewing if used as phosphate binder.
Vitamin D Analogues (Calcitriol, Alfacalcidol)
  • Calcitriol: 0.25–1 mcg PO or IV daily
  • Alfacalcidol: 0.25–1 mcg PO or IV daily
MOA: Active Vitamin D analogues do NOT require renal hydroxylation. Bind VDR → ↑ intestinal Ca²⁺ absorption → ↑ serum Ca → ↓ PTH secretion.
Precautions: Hypercalcaemia — contraindicated. Monitor Ca regularly (↑ absorption risk). Stop if Ca >2.6 mmol/L.
Counselling: Regular blood Ca checks essential. Report: nausea, weakness, confusion, increased thirst or urination (hypercalcaemia signs).
Cinacalcet (Calcimimetic)
30 mg PO daily with meals → titrate every 2–4 weeks → max 180 mg/day
MOA: Allosteric activator of calcium-sensing receptor (CaSR) on parathyroid cells → ↑ CaSR sensitivity to Ca²⁺ → ↓ PTH secretion → ↓ bone resorption.
Precautions: Hypocalcaemia — contraindicated (check Ca before each dose; correct before starting). Take with food. CYP2D6 inhibitor — interacts with codeine, metoprolol, some antidepressants. Discontinue if PTH repeatedly falls below 100 pg/mL (adynamic bone disease risk).
ADEs: Nausea, vomiting (take with food); hypocalcaemia (muscle cramps, spasms, paraesthesiae, seizures, QT prolongation).
Counselling: Always take with food or shortly after a meal. Report muscle cramps, tingling, or spasms immediately (low calcium). Regular blood tests (Ca, phosphate, PTH) are essential.
Post-Transplant: Tacrolimus, Mycophenolate, TMP-SMX, Valganciclovir, NystatinTransplant Immunosuppression & Prophylaxis
Tacrolimus
  • Oral: highly variable dose — target blood level (trough) guides dosing(typically higher in early post-transplant; lower over time)
  • IV: only if oral route unavailable; hospital specialist only
  • Many formulations: immediate release, extended release, LCPT — NOT interchangeable
MOA: Calcineurin inhibitor → binds FKBP12 → inhibits calcineurin → ↓ NFAT transcription → ↓ IL-2 and T-cell activation → ↓ rejection.
Critical precautions: Narrow TI — TDM essential. Many CYP3A4 drug interactions (azoles, macrolides, rifampicin, St John's Wort, grapefruit). Nephrotoxic (especially at high levels). Hypertension; hyperglycaemia (new-onset DM post-transplant). Neurotoxicity (tremor, headache, posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome — PRES). QT prolongation. Hyperkalaemia. Alopecia. Do NOT switch brands without specialist advice.
Counselling: Grapefruit and grapefruit juice — avoid completely. Consistent administration time (same hour daily). Check with pharmacist/doctor before starting ANY new medication, supplement, or herbal product. Report tremor, confusion, vision changes (PRES), fever, or signs of infection urgently. Never stop suddenly without specialist guidance.
Mycophenolate Mofetil / Mycophenolate Sodium
MMF: 500–1500 mg PO BD; Sodium: 360–1080 mg PO BD
MOA: Inhibits IMPDH → ↓ de novo purine synthesis → ↓ lymphocyte proliferation.
Precautions: Teratogenic — reliable contraception required during and 6 weeks after stopping. ↑ lymphoma/skin cancer risk. GI ADEs common (diarrhoea, nausea — may need dose split or formulation change). Monitor FBC regularly (leucopenia).
Counselling: Highly teratogenic — must use reliable contraception. Report unusual bruising, pallor, fever. Regular blood tests required.
TMP-SMX (Trimethoprim-Sulfamethoxazole) — PJP Prophylaxis
80/400 mg PO daily (standard) or 160/800 mg 3×/week for minimum 6–12 months
Also effective against: Toxoplasma, Nocardia, some bacterial UTIs.
Precautions: Sulfonamide allergy; severe renal/hepatic impairment; may cause false ↑ in serum creatinine (blocks tubular secretion — does NOT reflect true GFR decline).
Counselling: Take with food. Adequate fluid intake. Report rash promptly.
Valganciclovir — CMV Prophylaxis
Prophylaxis: 450–900 mg PO daily (dose adjusted by eGFR)
MOA: Prodrug of ganciclovir → inhibits CMV DNA polymerase.
Precautions: Teratogenic; myelosuppressive (monitor FBC); renal dose adjustment required.
Counselling: Take with food (↑ absorption). Regular blood count monitoring. Avoid direct contact with crushed/broken tablets (carcinogen risk).

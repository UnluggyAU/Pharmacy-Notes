Osteoarthritis (OA)

Chronic, progressive degenerative joint disease. Not curable — management focuses on symptom control, function, and quality of life.

🔬

Pathophysiology

Core process: Progressive breakdown of articular cartilage → friction → inflammation → structural joint failure. OA is no longer purely "wear and tear" — local and systemic inflammation are key contributors.
Mechanical stress / risk factors (age, obesity, injury, genetics, inflammation) → chondrocyte activation → increased expression of degradative enzymes (matrix metalloproteinases) → ECM breakdown
Chondrocytes attempt repair: initially lay down Type II collagen (normal) → under sustained injury, switch to Type I collagen (less elastic, weaker, poor proteoglycan binding) → cycle of progressive weakening
Pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) amplify catabolism → ↑ cartilage breakdown, ↓ new cartilage formation → self-reinforcing cycle of damage
Chondrocyte exhaustion: Overworked chondrocytes → apoptosis → cartilage softens, fibrillations form, cartilage flakes into synovial space
Synovitis: Type A synoviocytes clear debris → recruit lymphocytes/macrophages → inflammatory cytokines → synovial inflammation → fibrillations → erosion → bone-on-bone rubbing (eburnation)
Structural changes: Subchondral bone exposure → osteophyte formation at joint margins → joint widening/deformity → muscle wasting from disuse
💡
Key insight: Pain severity does not always correlate with radiographic severity. Pain arises from multiple intra- and periarticular sources — not cartilage loss alone. OA inflammation is secondary to cartilage damage (not a primary autoimmune process like RA).
Types:
  • Primary OA: Often hereditary; may involve 3+ joints
  • Secondary OA: Occurs after another cause — joint trauma, connective tissue disease, prior joint inflammation (e.g. RA, gout), joint infection, cartilage removal
⚠️

Risk Factors

🔒 Non-Modifiable
  • Age >50 years
  • Female sex
  • Genetic factors / family history
  • Joint abnormalities (e.g. congenital hip dysplasia)
  • Possible hormonal influence (hand OA peaks around menopause)
✏️ Modifiable
  • Obesity / raised BMI (especially knee OA; BMI >25)
  • Joint injury / trauma
  • Physically demanding occupation (farming, manual labour, repetitive bending/lifting)
  • Secondary to other joint disease (RA, gout)
🏥 Comorbidities (Impact Management)
  • Hypertension (limits NSAID use)
  • Diabetes (limits CS injections; consider duloxetine if comorbid depression)
  • Depression / anxiety
  • Renal impairment (limits NSAIDs)
  • GI disease / PUD (limits NSAIDs)
  • CVD (limits NSAIDs)
🩺

Clinical Features

🦴 Pain Characteristics
  • Dull, aching pain in and around the joint
  • Pain worsened by activity, better with rest
  • As disease progresses, pain may occur at rest
  • Chronic symptoms with intermittent flares
  • Pain does NOT correlate with X-ray severity
⏱️ Stiffness
  • Morning stiffness: typically <30 minutes (cf. RA >1 hour)
  • After inactivity: brief stiffness, usually only a few minutes
🔎 Examination Findings
  • Bony enlargement / osteophyte swelling (hard)
  • Deformity or malalignment
  • Soft tissue swelling, effusion
  • Warmth (limited, less than RA/gout)
  • Muscle wasting around affected joint
  • Crepitus (grating sensation on movement)
🗺️ Joints Commonly Affected
  • Knees (most common)
  • Hips
  • Hands (DIP, PIP, 1st CMC — base of thumb)
  • Facet joints of cervical/lumbar spine
  • Ankles
😔 Associated Symptoms
  • Fatigue
  • Sleep disturbance
  • Depression and anxiety
  • Reduced mobility and independence
  • Reduced work and social participation
🔍 Differentiating Features from Other Arthritides
  • vs RA: OA is NOT symmetrical (or less so); morning stiffness <30 min (RA >1hr); negative RF/anti-CCP; no systemic features; affects DIP joints (RA does not typically); inflammation secondary not primary
  • vs Gout: OA has chronic pain; gout has acute episodic severe monoarthritis; no hyperuricaemia in OA; gout often targets 1st MTP joint first
  • vs Inflammatory arthritis generally: OA — inflammatory markers (CRP/ESR) usually normal; joint aspirate WBC <1000×10⁶/L
  • Imaging: X-ray may show joint space narrowing, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis — but imaging findings correlate poorly with symptoms, so imaging is NOT required for diagnosis
🔍

Diagnosis & Investigations

✅
Diagnosis is largely clinical. No specific diagnostic test is required in typical presentations.
Clinical assessment must include: thorough history, physical examination (identify joints affected, presence of inflammation, degree of joint disruption, muscle weakness), functional assessment, psychosocial assessment.
When to investigate:
  • Imaging (X-ray) only if atypical presentation or another diagnosis suspected
  • Joint aspiration: if gout/infection suspected (synovial fluid WBC typically <1000×10⁶/L in OA)
  • Inflammatory markers (CRP/ESR) usually normal in OA — if raised, reconsider diagnosis
🎯

Treatment Goals

Pain
  • Optimise pain management
Function
  • Maintain/optimise physical function
  • Maintain daily, work, social activities
Disability
  • Minimise disability
  • Maximise health-related QOL
Individualisation
  • Shared decision-making
  • Create 'bad day' management plan
  • Trial-intervention approach with regular review
💡
Non-pharmacological measures are the foundation of OA management and must accompany all pharmacological treatment: exercise (aerobic + strengthening ≥3×/week), weight loss if BMI raised (target >5% body weight), appropriate footwear/insoles, walking aids, thermal therapy (heat/cold), education and self-management.
💊

Pharmacological Treatment Ladder

💡
Local therapies are preferred first — they target pain locally with fewer systemic adverse effects. Escalate to systemic therapy when local measures are inadequate or unsuitable. Use minimum effective dose for shortest necessary duration. Pain management alone should not prevent engagement with exercise and non-pharmacological strategies.
1
First-Line Local
Topical Analgesia

Preferred first pharmacological step — local pain relief with minimal systemic exposure. Most benefit in superficial/accessible joints (especially knee).

Topical NSAIDs Apply to affected area
• Diclofenac 1% gel — up to QID  |  Diclofenac 2% gel — up to BD
• Ibuprofen 5% gel — up to QID  |  Piroxicam 0.5% gel — up to QID
✅ Best for superficial joints (knee, hand) ✅ Lower systemic ADR risk than oral NSAIDs ✅ Preferred if GI/renal/CV risk precludes oral NSAIDs ❌ Not for deep joints (hip) — poor penetration ❌ Avoid on broken/irritated/infected skin ❌ Caution in pregnancy/lactation 🔵 Monitor: skin integrity; if also using oral NSAIDs, consider total NSAID exposure
Topical Capsaicin 0.025% cream, 3–4×/day
✅ Alternative when topical NSAIDs not tolerated/appropriate ❌ NOT recommended for hand or hip OA ❌ Burning side effect limits compliance ❌ Requires consistent use for weeks before benefit
2
Second-Line Systemic
Oral Analgesia

When local measures are inadequate or unsuitable. Use lowest effective dose for shortest duration. Stop if no benefit — switch NSAIDs before trying a different class.

Paracetamol IR: 1g q4–6h PRN (max 4g/day) | MR: 2.33g q8h PRN (max 4g/day)
✅ Lower harm profile — suitable when NSAIDs are higher risk ✅ MR formulation better for continuous symptoms throughout the day ❌ Limited anti-inflammatory effect — less effective for inflammatory-component pain ❌ Chronic liver disease — increased hepatotoxicity risk 🔵 Monitor: total daily dose; check for combination products containing paracetamol
Oral NSAIDs See individual agents below
More effective than paracetamol for inflammatory-component pain but greater harm potential. Use minimum effective dose and duration. Assess GI, renal, and CV risk before use.

3
Third-Line / Procedural
Intra-articular Corticosteroid Injection

For persisting functional impairment and pain despite pharmacological and non-pharmacological strategies. Provides short-term relief. Useful for travel, important events — fast onset.

Intra-articular Corticosteroid Repeat q3 months if needed
• Betamethasone Na phosphate + Betamethasone acetate — smaller joints, superficial soft tissue
• Methylprednisolone acetate — larger joints (knee, hip)
• Triamcinolone hexacetonide — children and needle-phobic patients (longer half-life, less frequent injections)
✅ Knee / hip OA with significant inflammation or effusion ✅ Fast onset — suitable for time-sensitive events ❌ Hip requires image guidance ❌ Avoid if joint replacement planned within 6 months (infection risk) ❌ Do not give >4 injections/year — ↑ cartilage damage risk ❌ Avoid if no response after 2 injections ❌ Intra-articular injection contraindicated in infective arthritis ⚠️ Diabetes — BGL monitoring for up to 7 days post-injection (hyperglycaemia risk) 🔵 Monitor: response duration, signs of infection post-injection, BGL in diabetics
4
Selected Later-Line Option
Duloxetine

Not a routine analgesic — reserved for specific clinical scenarios. Does NOT replace earlier-line therapies.

Duloxetine 30mg daily (1 wk) → 60mg daily (max 120mg/day)
✅ Comorbid depression — dual benefit (pain + depression) ✅ Widespread pain or central sensitisation component ✅ Chronic knee OA with functional impairment despite other strategies ❌ Significant hepatic impairment — contraindicated ❌ Caution: renal impairment, elderly, cardiac issues ❌ Caution: serotonin toxicity if combined with MAOIs or serotonergic drugs 🔵 Monitor: BP before and after starting; Na+ concentration; suicidal ideation (esp. young pts early in therapy); taper over weeks when stopping
⚠️
Not recommended: Hyaluronic acid (no reliable benefit over placebo; costs/harms outweigh value). PRP, adipocyte cell suspensions, mesenchymal stem cells — not routinely recommended.
5
Last Resort
Surgery

For persistent, debilitating symptoms despite maximal non-surgical management. Includes osteotomy and joint replacement. ~30% of OA patients will require surgery eventually. Encourage weight loss and exercise before surgery to optimise outcomes and reduce post-operative complications.

Drug Summaries

Topical NSAIDs Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory — Topical
Dose & Administration (OA)
  • Diclofenac 1% gel: apply to painful area up to QID
  • Diclofenac 2% gel: apply to painful area up to BD
  • Ibuprofen 5% gel: apply to painful area up to QID
  • Piroxicam 0.5% gel: apply to painful area up to QID
Mechanism of Action
Inhibit prostaglandin synthesis by inhibiting COX-1 and COX-2 enzymes. Topical delivery provides local reduction in prostaglandin-mediated pain and inflammation with minimal systemic absorption compared with oral formulations.
Indications
  • OA pain relief (superficial joints) — first-line local option
  • Other inflammatory arthropathies with accessible joints
  • Soft tissue musculoskeletal pain
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Avoid: mucosal surfaces, open wounds, bruises, irritated skin, rash
  • Caution: pregnancy and lactation
  • Caution: children under 12 years
  • Caution: allergy to NSAIDs or salicylates
  • Consider total NSAID exposure if concurrent oral NSAIDs used
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Dermatitis, itching, redness (local application site)
Infrequent
  • Swelling, blistering at application site
Monitoring
  • Check skin integrity before each application
  • Monitor for local irritation or hypersensitivity reactions
  • Consider cumulative NSAID exposure if oral NSAIDs also prescribed
Counselling
  • Apply only to intact skin. Avoid eyes and mucous membranes.
  • Wash hands after applying unless treating hands.
  • Stop and seek advice if severe skin irritation, rash, or blistering develops.
  • Allow to dry before covering skin.
Capsaicin (Topical) Topical Analgesic — TRPV1 Agonist
Dose (OA)
Capsaicin 0.025% cream: apply to painful area 3–4 times daily
Mechanism of Action
Capsaicin → TRPV1 agonist on skin nociceptors → initially increases pain/burning sensation → with repeated use causes nociceptor defunctionalisation (fewer active pain nerve endings) → decreased pain over time. Requires consistent use for weeks before benefit is seen.
Indications
  • OA pain relief in accessible joints — first-line local option when topical NSAIDs not tolerated/appropriate
Precautions & Contraindications
  • NOT recommended for hand or hip OA
  • Avoid broken/irritated/damaged skin
  • Hot water worsens stinging sensation
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Burning/stinging sensation at application site (especially initially) — main side effect, often limits compliance
Counselling
  • Expect burning sensation initially — it improves with regular use over 2–4 weeks.
  • Wash hands after application (or wear gloves) — contact with eyes, mouth, or face causes significant irritation.
  • Do not apply to broken or irritated skin.
  • Benefit requires consistent use for weeks.
Paracetamol (Acetaminophen) Analgesic / Antipyretic
Dose (OA)
  • Paracetamol IR: 1g PO q4–6h PRN (max 4g/day = 4 tabs/day)
  • Paracetamol MR: 2.33g PO q8h PRN (max 4g/day = 3 tabs/day)— MR preferred for continuous symptoms throughout day
Mechanism of Action
Not fully understood. Acts centrally — may involve inhibition of COX enzymes in the CNS and modulation of descending serotonergic pain pathways. Provides analgesia with little anti-inflammatory effect.
Indications
  • OA — mild to moderate pain, second-line systemic option
  • Mild to moderate pain of various causes
  • Fever
  • Opioid-sparing (adding paracetamol to opioid reduces opioid dose by ~30%)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Chronic liver disease — increased hepatotoxicity risk; use cautiously or avoid
  • Sodium-restricted diet (effervescent tablets contain sodium)
  • Alcohol excess — increased hepatotoxicity risk
  • Review all medications for hidden paracetamol (combination products)
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Increased aminotransferases (elevated liver enzymes) — usually not clinically significant at therapeutic doses
Rare
  • Rash, fixed drug eruption
  • Toxic epidermal necrolysis, Stevens–Johnson syndrome
  • Neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, pancytopenia
  • Acute hepatitis (at therapeutic doses in susceptible patients; overdose risk)
  • Hypotension
Monitoring
  • Check total daily dose from all sources (paracetamol is in many combination products)
  • Review in liver disease
  • Do not exceed 4g/day in adults
Counselling
  • Do not exceed 4 grams (8 standard tablets) per day.
  • Check labels of all other medications — many cold/flu, pain, and sleep products also contain paracetamol. Double-dosing is a significant risk.
  • Seek medical attention immediately if you take too much, even if you feel well — liver damage may not be apparent until hours later.
Oral NSAIDs Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs — Systemic
Doses (OA / Gout)
  • Celecoxib: 100–200 mg PO daily or BD
  • Etoricoxib: 30–60 mg PO daily
  • Ibuprofen IR: 200–400 mg PO 3–4×/day
  • Indometacin: 25–50 mg PO 2–4×/day
  • Ketoprofen MR: 200 mg PO daily
  • Meloxicam: 7.5–15 mg PO daily
  • Naproxen IR: 250–500 mg PO BD
  • Naproxen MR: 750–1000 mg PO daily
  • Piroxicam: 10–20 mg PO daily
  • Diclofenac: 25–50 mg PO 2–3×/day— Use minimum dose for shortest effective duration
Mechanism of Action
Inhibit COX-1 and COX-2 enzymes → ↓ prostaglandin synthesis.
COX-2 inhibition → analgesic and anti-inflammatory effects
COX-1 inhibition → impaired gastric mucosal protection, antiplatelet effects
Both → ↓ renal prostaglandins → ↓ renal blood flow and GFR (worsened by dehydration)
Indications
  • OA — second-line systemic analgesia
  • Gout — acute flare treatment and flare prophylaxis
  • RA — symptomatic pain relief (not disease-modifying)
  • Other inflammatory arthropathies and musculoskeletal pain
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Contraindicated/avoid: active PUD or GI bleeding, history of GI bleeding
  • Contraindicated/avoid: renal impairment (CrCl <25–30 mL/min), dehydration
  • Avoid: severe hepatic impairment, pregnancy, perioperative period
  • Caution: CVD, heart failure, hypertension
  • Caution: elderly, asthma (bronchospasm risk)
  • Caution: coagulation disorders, concurrent anticoagulants
  • Caution: inflammatory bowel disease
  • Caution: women trying to conceive
  • Triple Whammy: NSAID + ACEi/ARB + Diuretic → high AKI risk
Key Drug Interactions:
  • Antihypertensives — ↓ BP control, ↓ renal function
  • Anticoagulants (warfarin, DOACs) — ↑ bleeding risk
  • Methotrexate — ↑ methotrexate toxicity
  • Lithium — ↑ lithium levels/toxicity
  • Corticosteroids — ↑ GI bleed risk
  • Digoxin — NSAIDs ↓ renal function → ↑ digoxin levels
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Nausea, dyspepsia, diarrhoea
  • Headache, dizziness
  • GI ulceration or bleeding
  • Raised liver enzymes
  • Fluid retention, hypertension
Infrequent
  • Heart failure, hyperkalaemia, renal impairment
  • Bronchospasm, rash
Rare / Red Flags
  • Acute renal failure, MI, stroke
  • Severe hepatitis, severe skin reactions, anaphylaxis
Monitoring
  • Assess GI, renal, and CV risk before prescribing
  • Monitor renal function in high-risk patients or prolonged use
  • Check BP in hypertensive patients
  • Use minimum effective dose for shortest necessary duration
Counselling
  • Do NOT take when dehydrated (vomiting, diarrhoea, hot weather, fasting).
  • Seek urgent medical review for: ankle swelling, breathlessness, chest pain (CV events), black/tarry stools or coffee-ground vomit (GI bleed).
  • Do not take with aspirin for pain unless specifically advised.
  • Only one NSAID at a time — do not double up.
  • GI protection: if at high GI risk and NSAID essential, take with food or add a PPI.
Intra-articular Corticosteroid Injection Corticosteroid — Local
Preparations & Use
  • Betamethasone Na phosphate + acetate: smaller joints / soft tissue
  • Methylprednisolone acetate: larger joints (knee, hip)
  • Triamcinolone hexacetonide: children/needle-phobic (longer duration)— Repeat approx. every 3 months if clinically needed— Hip injection requires image guidance— Knee may be landmark-guided
Mechanism of Action
Suppresses inflammatory gene expression → ↓ inflammatory mediator production (cytokines, prostaglandins, leukocyte activity) → ↓ joint inflammation, pain, and effusion.
Indications
  • OA — short-term symptom relief, third-line option
  • Gout — acute flare (up to 2 sites)
  • RA — flare management in accessible joints
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Contraindicated: infective/septic arthritis
  • Avoid within 6 months of planned joint replacement surgery (infection risk)
  • Diabetes — monitor BGL for up to 7 days post-injection
  • Maximum 4 injections per joint per year (cartilage damage risk)
  • Avoid further injections if no response after 2 attempts
  • Children — risk of impaired linear growth/bone mass with repeated use
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Post-injection flare (temporary worsening 24–48h post-injection)
Infrequent
  • Skin hypopigmentation at injection site
  • Subcutaneous tissue atrophy
  • Hyperglycaemia (diabetics)
Rare
  • Septic arthritis (infection of the joint) — seek urgent review if increasing pain/redness/fever post-injection
  • Cartilage damage with repeated use
Monitoring
  • Avoid overusing the joint after injection — may reduce benefit and increase deterioration
  • Monitor response duration to guide retreatment decisions
  • Watch for signs of infection post-injection (pain, redness, swelling, fever)
  • Monitor BGL in diabetic patients for up to 7 days post-injection
Counselling
  • This injection provides mainly short-term relief (weeks to months).
  • You may experience a temporary worsening of pain for 24–48 hours after injection — this is normal (post-injection flare).
  • Seek urgent medical review if pain, redness, swelling, or fever develops after the injection.
  • Do not overexert or heavily use the joint immediately after injection.
  • If you have diabetes, monitor your blood glucose carefully for the next week.
Duloxetine SNRI — Serotonin-Noradrenaline Reuptake Inhibitor
Dose (OA)
  • 30 mg PO daily for 1 week
  • → then 60 mg PO daily
  • → maximum 120 mg PO daily— Taper dose over weeks/months when stopping (avoid abrupt withdrawal)
Mechanism of Action
Inhibits serotonin and noradrenaline reuptake → ↑ serotonin + noradrenaline in descending pain pathways → enhances central pain inhibition → ↓ chronic OA pain. Also has antidepressant effects via same mechanism.
Indications
  • OA — chronic knee OA pain with functional impairment and widespread pain or comorbid depression, despite first-line strategies
  • Major depressive disorder
  • Generalised anxiety disorder
  • Diabetic peripheral neuropathic pain
  • Fibromyalgia
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Contraindicated: significant hepatic impairment
  • Caution: renal impairment
  • Caution: elderly patients — ↑ fall risk
  • Caution: cardiac disease, uncontrolled hypertension
  • Serotonin toxicity: do not combine with MAOIs (washout required)
  • Caution: concurrent serotonergic drugs (SSRIs, tramadol, triptans)
  • Caution: history of mania or bipolar disorder
  • Caution: glaucoma (can increase intraocular pressure)
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Nausea, dry mouth, constipation
  • Dizziness, headache
  • Sweating
  • Blood pressure increase
  • Insomnia or somnolence
Rare / Important
  • Increased suicidal thoughts/behaviour — especially young patients early in therapy
  • Serotonin syndrome (if combined with other serotonergic drugs)
  • Hyponatraemia (↓ Na⁺) — especially elderly
  • Withdrawal syndrome if stopped abruptly
Monitoring
  • Check BP before starting and within a few weeks of starting
  • Check serum sodium (Na⁺) soon after starting, especially in elderly
  • Monitor for suicidal ideation particularly in young patients in early weeks of therapy
  • Review mood and mental health status regularly
Counselling
  • It may take several weeks to notice the full pain-relieving effect. Be patient and consistent with the medication.
  • Do NOT stop suddenly — taper the dose gradually over weeks/months to avoid withdrawal symptoms (dizziness, nausea, "electric shock" sensations).
  • Report any increase in depressive thoughts, suicidal ideation, or significant mood changes — seek urgent help if this occurs.
  • Nausea is common early on and usually improves. Take with food if it helps.
  • Avoid alcohol — increases CNS depressant effects and increases liver toxicity risk.
  • Take care with driving until you know how the medication affects you.

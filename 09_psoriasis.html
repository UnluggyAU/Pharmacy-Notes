Psoriasis

Chronic, immune-mediated skin disease characterised by keratinocyte hyperproliferation. Affects ~2–3% of population. T-cell mediated; TNFα/IL-17/IL-23 pathways central. Not curable — management aims for sustained clearance and quality of life.

🔬

Pathophysiology

Core process: T-cell-mediated autoimmune/immune-dysregulation → keratinocyte activation → hyperproliferation → abnormal skin turnover (3–6 days vs normal 28 days) → characteristic plaques.
Genetic susceptibility (HLA-Cw6; IL-23R variants; PSORS1 locus) + environmental trigger → dendritic cell activation
DC activation → T-cell differentiation to Th1 and Th17 → release of TNFα, IFNγ, IL-17A, IL-22, IL-23
IL-17, TNFα, IL-22 → keratinocyte proliferation ↑↑ (turnover: 28 days → 3–6 days) + abnormal differentiation → thick scaly plaques; abnormal vasculature → erythema
Chronic cycle: Plaques → ongoing immune stimulation → perpetuating inflammation. Köbner phenomenon — new lesions develop at sites of trauma/injury to skin.
💡
Key signs: Auspitz sign — pinpoint bleeding when scale removed (dilated superficial vessels exposed). Köbner phenomenon — psoriasis appearing at sites of skin injury. Both are characteristic of psoriasis.

Risk Factors & Triggers

🧬 Non-Modifiable
  • Genetic predisposition (family history)
  • HLA-Cw6 positive
🌍 Modifiable / Triggers
  • Infections — streptococcal throat (guttate psoriasis)
  • Stress (emotional or physical)
  • Smoking (↑ risk and severity)
  • Alcohol (↑ risk and severity; worsens adherence)
  • Obesity (↑ risk, worsens severity)
  • Skin trauma (Köbner phenomenon)
💊 Drug Triggers (Exacerbate or Induce Psoriasis)
  • Beta-blockers
  • NSAIDs
  • Lithium
  • Hydroxychloroquine
  • ACE inhibitors
  • Interferon alfa
  • GM-CSF
  • Withdrawal of systemic or potent topical corticosteroids — may precipitate severe rebound, including erythrodermic or generalised pustular psoriasis
🤝 Comorbidities
  • Psoriatic arthritis (~30%)
  • Depression / anxiety (~50%)
  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Metabolic syndrome / diabetes
  • IBD (Crohn, UC)
  • Uveitis
🩺

Clinical Features

🔴 Typical Plaque Psoriasis
  • Well-demarcated erythematous plaques
  • Silvery-white scale on surface
  • Itching (variable)
  • Extensor surfaces, scalp, sacrum, nails
  • Auspitz sign (pinpoint bleeding on scale removal)
🌐 Extra-Cutaneous
  • Psoriatic arthritis (30%) — nail psoriasis is a marker
  • Uveitis
  • IBD association
  • Metabolic syndrome
🔍 Key Differentials
  • Eczema/atopic dermatitis: Less defined borders, more flexural distribution, more itchy, usually without thick scale, family atopy history
  • Tinea corporis: Central clearing with advancing active edge, asymmetric, KOH positive
  • Seborrhoeic dermatitis: Greasy scale in sebaceous areas (scalp, face, chest), less well-defined
  • Pityriasis rosea: Herald patch followed by secondary rash; self-limiting; follows lines of cleavage
🚨

Psoriasis Emergencies

🚨 Generalised Pustular Psoriasis
  • Waves of sterile pustules on widespread erythematous skin
  • Systemic features: fever, malaise, leucocytosis
  • Often triggered by abrupt CS withdrawal
  • Management: Hospital admission; avoid systemic CS; specialist dermatologist; ciclosporin or acitretin (specialist); supportive care
🚨 Erythrodermic Psoriasis
  • >90% BSA erythema + skin barrier failure
  • Complications: high-output cardiac failure, infection, hypoproteinaemia, hypothermia, GI malabsorption, anaemia
  • Management: Hospital admission; specialist dermatologist; supportive care (fluid, temperature, protein replacement); systemic therapy decided by specialist
🔍

Diagnosis & Severity Assessment

Diagnosis: Primarily clinical. Skin biopsy if diagnosis uncertain. Specific features: well-defined plaques with silvery scale; Auspitz sign; Köbner phenomenon; characteristic sites.
SeverityBSA / PASITreatment Implication
MildBSA <10% OR PASI <10Topical therapy alone
ModerateBSA 10–30% OR PASI 10–20Topical ± phototherapy; consider systemic
SevereBSA >30% OR PASI >20Systemic / biologic therapy (specialist)
💡
Note: BSA/PASI thresholds are guides — any involvement of special sites (face, genitals, hands/feet, nails) may justify more aggressive treatment even if BSA is small. Quality of life (DLQI) also informs treatment decisions.
🎯

Treatment Goals & Non-Pharmacological Management

Primary Goals
  • Achieve adequate disease control (skin clearance)
  • Improve quality of life and psychological wellbeing
  • Reduce itch, pain, and discomfort
  • Minimise medication adverse effects
  • Treat according to site — scalp, face, flexures, nails, and palmoplantar behave differently
Topical Rotation Principle
  • Rotation and combination of topical treatments (as opposed to continuous monotherapy) minimises adverse effects and maximises efficacy
  • E.g. topical CS on weekends; tar/calcipotriol during the week
  • When combining CS + tar: CS in the morning, tar at night
🚨
Oral corticosteroids must NOT be used for psoriasis — they can cause exacerbation to generalised pustular psoriasis on withdrawal.
Non-Pharmacological — Basic Supportive Care for All Patients
🧴 Emollients (Base Therapy — Continue Always)
  • Use regularly to restore skin pliability and reduce scale shedding
  • Reduce pruritus and help prevent painful cracking and bleeding
  • Urea- and glycerin-containing products may be better moisturisers
  • Continue even when active lesions settle — steroid-sparing effect
  • Suitable products: Eucerin dry skin intensive 10% urea cream; Hydromol intensive; Neutrogena Dermatological
  • An emollient bath additive can counteract the drying effects of bathing
🚿 Soap Substitute & Skin Care
  • Use a soap substitute — soap strips natural oils and worsens dryness/itch
  • Avoid hot water; use lukewarm baths/showers
  • Pat skin dry gently — do not rub
  • Apply emollient immediately after bathing while skin is still slightly damp
⚠️ Avoid Triggers & Irritants
  • Avoid skin trauma (Köbner phenomenon — new lesions form at injury sites)
  • Avoid irritants (harsh chemicals, friction, tight clothing)
  • Smoking cessation — strong risk factor, worsens severity
  • Reduce alcohol intake (exacerbates psoriasis)
  • Stress management — stress is a known trigger
  • Cautious sun exposure is beneficial (avoid sunburn — can trigger flare)
💊 Medication Review
  • Review all medicines for trigger drugs — consider alternative agents where possible
  • Trigger drugs: beta-blockers, NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, lithium, hydroxychloroquine, interferon alfa, GM-CSF
  • Withdrawal of systemic or potent topical CS → severe rebound/flare risk
🧠 Psychological & Comorbidity Management
  • Psychological impact is commonly unrecognised — assess proactively; does not always correlate with severity
  • Depression/anxiety common (~50%)
  • Cardiovascular risk management (lifestyle: exercise, weight loss, diabetes screening)
  • Screen for psoriatic arthritis (~30%)
  • Offer HIV testing if severe or recalcitrant psoriasis (indicator condition)
🥗 Diet
  • Special diets do NOT have a routine role in psoriasis therapy
  • A gluten-free diet may improve severity in patients with coeliac disease or gluten sensitivity — but is not a cure
  • Weight management — obesity worsens inflammation and reduces drug efficacy

Plaque Psoriasis — Treatment by Location

Plaque psoriasis is the most common type (~80–90%). Lesions are symmetrical, well-circumscribed, salmon-pink with a loose silvery scale. Common sites: elbows, knees, lower back, sacrum, scalp. Treat according to location.

Guttate Psoriasis

Numerous droplet-shaped lesions (2–10 mm) on the trunk and proximal limbs. Pink with less prominent scale than plaque psoriasis. Most common in adolescents and young adults. Often triggered by Streptococcus pyogenes throat or perianal infection (typically 2–3 weeks after). Treat active infection with antibiotics if confirmed. Plaques are often thin and respond to milder topical treatments.
✅
Treat as for plaque psoriasis on the trunk and limbs — follow the Trunk & Limbs ladder above. If no response to treatment → refer to dermatologist. Guttate psoriasis responds well to narrowband UVB phototherapy.

🚨 Generalised Pustular Psoriasis — Dermatological Emergency

🚨
Urgently refer to hospital — do not manage in primary care.

Presents as a generalised eruption of small pustules on erythematous skin, with fever and systemic symptoms.

Generalised pustular psoriasis of pregnancy — rare, explosive form — urgently refer for hospital assessment by dermatologist.

Common triggers: Withdrawal of systemic or potent topical CS; lithium; hydroxychloroquine; interferon alfa; GM-CSF; paradoxical reaction to biologic therapy (rare).
🚨

Erythrodermic Psoriasis — Refer to Hospital

🚨
Erythrodermic psoriasis — refer for hospital assessment and management by a dermatologist. Do not manage in primary care.

Definition: Affects the entire skin surface (often entire body). May develop gradually as an extension of plaque psoriasis or erupt suddenly. The eruptive variant may be accompanied by widespread pustulation, fever, and systemic symptoms.

Complications: High-output cardiac failure; infection; gastrointestinal malabsorption; anaemia; temperature dysregulation.

Triggers: Withdrawal of systemic or potent topical corticosteroids; lithium; certain biologics (paradoxical — rare).
🏥

Drug Summaries

Topical CorticosteroidsTopical Anti-inflammatory — Mainstay of Psoriasis Treatment
Doses by Agent and Site
  • Hydrocortisone 1% cream or ointment (Class I): Apply once daily. For face in children; nappy area; flexures (long-term).
  • Methylprednisolone aceponate 0.1% cream, ointment, fatty ointment or lotion (Class III): Apply once daily until clear (usually 2–6 weeks). Adults and children ≥4 months. Lotion formulation for scalp.
  • Mometasone furoate 0.1% cream, ointment or hydrogel (Class III): Apply once daily until clear (usually 2–6 weeks). Hydrogel or lotion for scalp; cream/ointment for face (ears/hairline) and body.
  • Betamethasone dipropionate 0.05% cream, ointment or lotion (Class III): Apply once daily until clear (body) or twice daily for 6 weeks (scalp lotion) when escalating from Class III agents.
  • Clobetasol propionate 0.05% shampoo (Class IV): Apply once daily for 6 weeks — scalp only. Very potent; short courses only.
  • Mometasone furoate 0.01% lotion (Class III — for nails): Apply under nail once daily for up to 3 months (nail psoriasis, onycholysis or subungual hyperkeratosis).
Mechanism of Action
Bind glucocorticoid receptors in skin → ↓ cytokine production (anti-inflammatory) → ↓ keratinocyte proliferation (anti-proliferative) → ↓ inflammatory cell migration and vascular permeability → ↓ erythema, scale, and plaque thickness. Effects are anti-inflammatory, anti-proliferative, and immunosuppressive.
Key Adverse Effects by Duration of Use
Local (excessive/prolonged use)
  • Skin atrophy, striae, telangiectasia — especially face, flexures, under occlusion
  • Tachyphylaxis (diminishing response with continuous use) — rotate agents
  • Perioral/periorbital dermatitis, rosacea exacerbation (face)
  • Acneiform eruptions; hypopigmentation; poor wound healing
Systemic (high dose, large area, prolonged, occluded)
  • HPA axis suppression — risk of adrenal insufficiency on abrupt stopping
  • Hyperglycaemia, hypertension, Cushingoid features (prolonged large-area use)
  • Rebound flare on withdrawal — can precipitate generalised pustular or erythrodermic psoriasis with potent agents
Counselling
  • Apply a thin film only to affected areas. A little goes a long way — more is not better.
  • Use only on the area instructed. Do not use potent/very potent preparations on the face, flexures, or genitals unless specifically directed.
  • Do NOT cover with a bandage or wrap without medical advice — occlusion increases absorption and adverse effects.
  • Do NOT stop potent topical CS abruptly after prolonged widespread use — taper gradually to prevent rebound flare.
  • Rotate to a less potent agent or emollient between courses to avoid tachyphylaxis.
  • Avoid eyes — glaucoma risk with periorbital use.
  • Apply emollient first, wait 30 minutes, then apply topical CS.

Mechanism of Action
Calcipotriol: Analogue of 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (active vitamin D). Binds to vitamin D receptor → induces keratinocyte differentiation and suppresses proliferation → reverses the abnormal keratinocyte changes in psoriasis. Inhibits keratinocyte differentiation and production. Maximal response at 8 weeks.

Betamethasone dipropionate: Potent topical corticosteroid (Class III) → anti-inflammatory, anti-proliferative, immunosuppressive → ↓ cytokine production → ↓ inflammatory cell migration → ↓ erythema and scale.

The combination provides both anti-proliferative (calcipotriol) and anti-inflammatory (betamethasone) effects — more effective than either alone.
Indications
  • Chronic stable plaque psoriasis (PBS-subsidised: not controlled by potent topical CS monotherapy)
  • Scalp psoriasis (foam)
  • Nail psoriasis (ointment — applied to proximal nail fold and under nail)
  • Maintenance therapy (foam: twice-weekly proactive dosing)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Disorders of calcium metabolism — contraindicated
  • Do not apply to face or skin folds (irritating — itch and redness)
  • Do not apply to >30% BSA — risk of hypercalcaemia from excessive calcipotriol absorption
  • Pregnancy — appears safe for localised psoriasis; avoid extensive use
  • Breastfeeding — ensure breast area free from ointment/foam before breastfeeding
  • Calcipotriol is unstable with salicylic acid — apply at a different time of day
  • Apply after UVA treatment (not before) — calcipotriol is unstable in the presence of UVA
  • Do not apply immediately before NB-UVB phototherapy — blocks UV light
  • Do not mix with other preparations unless directed — mixing can destroy the calcipotriol
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Skin irritation (usually mild burning or stinging), itch, erythema, rash
  • Topical CS component: skin atrophy, striae (with prolonged use at same site)
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Exacerbation of psoriasis
  • Changes in pigmentation
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Hypercalcaemia — especially if max dose exceeded or >30% BSA treated
  • Allergic contact dermatitis
  • Photosensitivity
Monitoring
  • Routine monitoring of plasma calcium is not required at the recommended dosage (hypercalcaemia is rare at recommended doses)
  • Monitor for hypercalcaemia symptoms if large areas are treated: GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, constipation), muscle weakness, headache, increased thirst/urination
  • Reassess response at 4 weeks (foam) or 4–6 weeks (ointment). If inadequate → refer to dermatologist.
  • When used for 4 weeks (trunk/limbs): foam works faster and is more effective than ointment
  • Monitor for local skin thinning (atrophy) from betamethasone component with prolonged use
Counselling
  • Do not apply to the face or skin folds — this can cause itching and redness.
  • Do not mix with other skin preparations unless your doctor has instructed you to — mixing can destroy the calcipotriol and reduce effectiveness.
  • Wash hands thoroughly after applying to avoid unintentional transfer to other body areas (unless your hands are being treated).
  • Protect treated areas from sunlight — wear protective clothing or apply a broad-spectrum sunscreen (SPF 30 or SPF 50+ with a physical agent such as titanium dioxide). Avoid sunlamps and tanning beds.
  • If using salicylic acid as well — apply at a different time of day, not simultaneously (calcipotriol is inactivated by salicylic acid).
  • If having phototherapy — apply the foam or ointment after treatment, not before.
  • Foam: A 2-second spray provides 0.5 g, which covers approximately the area of a flat hand. Do not use more than 15 g per day (what is sprayed in 1 minute). One can should last at least 4 days.
  • Breastfeeding: ensure the breast area is free from product before feeding your baby.
  • Do not stop abruptly after prolonged use — taper the betamethasone component to avoid rebound.

Coal tar (LPC): Anti-proliferative, anti-inflammatory, anti-pruritic. Counselling
  • Coal tar: Strong smell — use old or dark-coloured clothing/bedlinen when applying. Do not use on inflamed, broken, or weeping skin.
  • Apply emollient first, allow to absorb, then apply tar or salicylic acid preparation.
  • Salicylic acid: Start low and increase concentration gradually. Do not use on large areas of skin or on children in high concentrations.
  • Ichthammol: Less odour than coal tar but still has characteristic smell.
Methotrexate + Folic Acid (Psoriasis)Systemic Immunomodulator — csDMARD
Dose (Psoriasis)
  • Starting dose (psoriasis): 5–10 mg PO once weekly
  • Usual maintenance: 10–20 mg PO/SC/IM once weekly
  • Always with: Folic acid 5–10 mg PO once weekly on a DIFFERENT day from methotrexate
⚠️
Psoriasis-specific: Preferred when psoriasis co-exists with psoriatic arthritis (treats both). Hepatotoxicity risk increases with cumulative dose (>1.5 g total) — consider fibroscan or liver biopsy monitoring with prolonged use.
Mechanism of Action
Folate antagonist → inhibits dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR) → ↓ purine and pyrimidine synthesis → ↓ rapidly proliferating immune cells → ↓ inflammation. Also increases adenosine release → additional anti-inflammatory effect.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Pregnancy & breastfeeding — absolutely contraindicated (teratogenic, category X)
  • Significant renal impairment — avoid (methotrexate accumulates)
  • Significant hepatic impairment — avoid
  • Alcohol — avoid (synergistic hepatotoxicity risk)
  • Bone marrow suppression — caution
  • NSAIDs — may ↑ methotrexate levels (↓ renal clearance); use with caution
  • Trimethoprim — folate antagonist; ↑ methotrexate toxicity; avoid combination
  • Reliable contraception required in BOTH males and females during treatment and for at least 6 months after stopping (psoriasis — avoid conception for 6 months after stopping)
Adverse Effects
Common
  • GI: nausea, vomiting, mouth ulcers, diarrhoea (↓ with folic acid co-prescription)
  • Fatigue, headache, alopecia, photosensitivity
  • ↑ Liver enzymes (dose-related; folic acid reduces risk)
Serious / Rare
  • Myelosuppression (pancytopenia) — dose-reduce or stop; folate deficiency worsens risk
  • Hepatotoxicity / hepatic fibrosis — cumulative dose-related; monitor LFTs; consider fibroscan if cumulative dose >1.5 g
  • Methotrexate pneumonitis — dry cough, dyspnoea, fever → STOP IMMEDIATELY and refer urgently
  • Teratogenicity
Monitoring
  • FBC + LFTs + renal function: baseline → every 2–4 weeks for first 3 months → every 3 months for 1 year → every 6 months
  • Chest X-ray at baseline
  • Reduce or stop if: WBC <3.5 × 10⁹/L, platelets <150 × 10⁹/L, LFTs >2× upper limit of normal
  • Investigate any new pulmonary symptoms immediately (pneumonitis risk)
Counselling
  • ONCE WEEKLY only — never daily. Take on the same day each week. Fatal errors have occurred with daily dosing.
  • Always take folic acid on a different day from methotrexate.
  • Avoid all alcohol during treatment.
  • Reliable contraception required — both males and females must avoid conception during treatment and for ≥3 months after stopping.
  • Seek urgent medical attention for: fever, unusual bruising/bleeding, mouth sores, shortness of breath, or new cough.
  • Tell your doctor and pharmacist about all other medications — especially trimethoprim and NSAIDs.
  • Use sun protection — photosensitivity risk.
  • Regular blood tests are mandatory — do not skip them.
AcitretinSystemic Retinoid
Dose
25–50 mg PO daily with main meal (take with fatty food — ↑ absorption)
Mechanism of Action
Synthetic retinoid (vitamin A derivative) → binds nuclear retinoic acid receptors (RAR) → normalises keratinocyte differentiation and proliferation → ↓ plaque formation. NOT an immunosuppressant — maintains normal immune function.
Indications
  • Palmoplantar pustular psoriasis (particularly effective)
  • Generalised pustular psoriasis
  • Erythrodermic psoriasis (specialist)
  • Severe plaque psoriasis (especially when immunosuppression undesirable)
  • Can be combined with NB-UVB phototherapy (reti-UVB)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Pregnancy — teratogenic for UP TO 3 YEARS after stopping (acitretin is converted to etretinate, which has a very long half-life)
  • Women of childbearing potential — contraindicated unless highly reliable contraception used during AND for 3 years after stopping
  • Alcohol during or 2 months after acitretin — converts to etretinate → extends teratogenic risk window to unknown duration
  • Severe hepatic or renal disease — caution
  • Hyperlipidaemia — often worsens with acitretin; monitor and manage
  • Do NOT donate blood during or for 3 years after treatment
Adverse Effects
Common / Expected
  • Mucocutaneous: dry skin, lips (cheilitis), nasal mucosa, eyes; skin fragility; peeling palms/soles
  • Alopecia (hair thinning — reversible)
  • ↑ Triglycerides and cholesterol
Rare / Serious
  • Hepatotoxicity (↑ LFTs)
  • Skeletal changes (hyperostosis, periosteal thickening — with long-term use)
  • Pseudotumour cerebri (↑ intracranial pressure) — especially with tetracyclines
Monitoring
  • Fasting lipids and LFTs at baseline → monthly for first 3 months → 3-monthly
  • Blood glucose (diabetic patients)
  • Pregnancy test (female) before starting and monthly during treatment
Counselling
  • Take with your main meal — fatty food improves absorption.
  • Women: Must not become pregnant during treatment or for 3 years after stopping. Use reliable contraception. Do NOT drink alcohol during or for 2 months after stopping.
  • Do not donate blood during treatment or for 3 years after stopping.
  • Skin, lips, and eyes will become dry — use lip balm, moisturisers, and eye drops regularly.
  • Avoid UV exposure without sunscreen — increased skin sensitivity. Wear SPF30+ sunscreen daily.
  • Report: severe headache, vision changes, nausea (pseudotumour cerebri); joint/bone pain (skeletal effects); yellowing of skin/eyes.
ApremilastPDE4 Inhibitor (Oral Small Molecule)
Dose
  • Titration (days 1–5)
  • Day 1: 10 mg morning
  • Day 2: 10 mg morning + 10 mg evening
  • Day 3: 10 mg morning + 20 mg evening
  • Day 4: 20 mg morning + 20 mg evening
  • Day 5: 20 mg morning + 30 mg evening
  • Day 6 onwards: 30 mg BD
  • Reduce to 30 mg daily if CrCl <30 mL/min
Mechanism of Action
Inhibits phosphodiesterase 4 (PDE4) → ↑ intracellular cAMP → ↓ pro-inflammatory cytokine production (TNFα, IL-17, IL-23) + ↑ anti-inflammatory cytokines (IL-10) → ↓ inflammation.
Precautions
  • Depression / suicidality — screen before starting; monitor during treatment; weigh risk-benefit carefully in patients with existing psychiatric history
  • Underweight patients — may cause further weight loss (monitor)
  • No routine blood test monitoring required (advantage over methotrexate)
  • Drug interactions: rifampicin (strong CYP3A4 inducer) → ↓ apremilast levels → may reduce efficacy
Adverse Effects
Common (especially first 2 weeks)
  • Diarrhoea, nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramping (usually improves spontaneously after 2–4 weeks)
  • Headache, URTI
Infrequent / Serious
  • Depression, mood changes, insomnia (monitor)
  • Weight loss
Counselling
  • Start with the lower doses and build up gradually as directed — this minimises GI side effects.
  • GI symptoms (nausea, diarrhoea) are common in the first 2 weeks but usually improve. Take with food or milk to reduce GI upset. If severe or persistent, contact your doctor.
  • Report mood changes, depression, thoughts of self-harm immediately.
  • Does not require regular blood tests — an advantage of this medication.

Shared Precautions (All Psoriasis Biologics)
  • Active infection — contraindicated
  • Latent TB — screen (Mantoux or IGRA + CXR) before starting; treat if positive
  • Hepatitis B — screen before starting (reactivation risk)
  • Live vaccines — avoid during treatment; vaccinate before starting if possible
  • IL-17 inhibitors — caution/avoid in IBD (may worsen Crohn/UC)
  • TNFα inhibitors — avoid in MS, demyelinating disease, moderate-severe heart failure
  • Annual TB screening, skin checks, and infection monitoring throughout treatment
Counselling (All Biologics)
  • Report fever or any signs of infection urgently — infections can be serious on these medications.
  • Ensure all recommended vaccinations are given BEFORE starting biologic therapy where possible.
  • Regular skin cancer checks and sun protection.
  • Tell all healthcare providers you are on biologic therapy before any procedure or new medication.

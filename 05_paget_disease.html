Paget Disease of Bone (PDB)

Focal disorder of bone remodelling — excessive osteoclast resorption followed by disorganised osteoblast formation. Produces structurally weak, enlarged, deformed bone. Most common metabolic bone disease after osteoporosis. Often asymptomatic and found incidentally.

🔬

Pathophysiology

Core process: Markedly increased and disorganised bone turnover. Osteoclast activity ↑ first → osteoblasts respond → new bone laid down abnormally → structurally impaired, enlarged, deformed bone with ↑ fracture risk and ↑ secondary complications.
Phases of Paget Disease:
PhasePathologyImaging
LyticMarked osteoclastic activity (osteoclasts may have up to 100 nuclei; resorption up to 20× normal rate)Aggressive lytic lesions
MixedBoth lytic and blastic activity presentMixed lytic + sclerotic change
ScleroticBone formation exceeds resorption → thickened but abnormally architected bone → weaker despite appearing denserCortical thickening, bone expansion, deformity
Burned-outOsteoblastic activity slows → disease relatively dormantStable sclerotic changes
💡
Treatment logic: Main abnormality is excessive bone resorption and high bone turnover → bisphosphonates suppress osteoclast activity → induce remission. ALP is the main marker of disease activity (reflects high bone turnover).
🧬
Cause: Not fully understood — likely genetic + environmental. SQSTM1 gene mutations associated with familial and some sporadic cases. Possible viral factors (historically proposed). Family screening: consider serum ALP in siblings/children of affected patients after age 40.
⚠️

Risk Factors & Epidemiology

🔒 Non-Modifiable
  • Age >55 years
  • Family history (autosomal dominant pattern possible)
  • SQSTM1 gene mutation
  • British ancestry (more common in Australia, NZ, North America, UK)
🌍 Environmental (proposed)
  • Viral factors (historically proposed, still controversial)
  • Local bone trauma
  • Environmental toxins
🩺

Clinical Features

⚠️
Often asymptomatic! Frequently found incidentally on X-ray or raised ALP on routine bloods. Prevalence ~2–4% of adults >55.
🦴 Bone Pain
  • Dull, boring, poorly localised pain
  • Present at rest and especially at night (cf. OA pain — usually with activity)
  • Mechanical pain if microfractures, lytic lesions, or adjacent joint OA
  • Difficult to distinguish from secondary osteoarthritis pain
🗺️ Bones Commonly Affected
  • Pelvis (most common)
  • Lumbosacral spine
  • Skull (leontiasis — lion-like facial appearance with severe involvement)
  • Femur (bowing deformity)
  • Tibia
🧠 Neurological Complications
  • Skull involvement → compresses cranial structures
  • Auditory foramen narrowing → hearing loss (deafness)
  • Optic canal narrowing → vision loss
  • Vertebral deformity → spinal cord compression → lower limb weakness
⚠️ Other Complications
  • Deformity (develops over time — femur/tibia bowing, pelvic asymmetry, kyphosis)
  • Fracture risk
  • Secondary osteoarthritis near affected joints
  • Arthropathy, joint inflammation
  • Very rarely: osteosarcoma (malignant transformation)
🔍 Differentiating Paget from Osteoporosis / Malignancy
  • vs Osteoporosis: Paget — focal disease with markedly elevated ALP; bone enlargement and thickening (not just thinning); X-ray shows characteristic changes (lytic lesions, cortical thickening, bone expansion). Osteoporosis — generalised, normal ALP.
  • vs Metastatic bone disease / myeloma: Paget — characteristic X-ray changes; normal protein electrophoresis; elevated ALP. Malignancy — may have lytic lesions but different distribution; often elevated ESR; paraprotein in myeloma. CT/MRI/biopsy if uncertain.
  • vs Osteomalacia: Osteomalacia — very low Vit D + very low Ca/phosphate + elevated ALP. Paget — ALP elevated from bone (not liver); different bone histology.
🔍

Diagnosis & Investigations

🥇
Diagnosis is usually made by combination of: raised ALP + characteristic X-ray findings (± bone scan). Many diagnoses are accidental.
InvestigationRole / Notes
Serum Total ALPMain diagnostic marker and monitoring tool. ALP >125 U/L without another explanation suggests active Paget disease. However: Normal ALP does NOT exclude Paget (if only one bone involved or disease is low activity). If liver disease present → use bone-specific ALP or bone resorption markers.
Liver function testsExclude hepatic cause of ALP elevation
Serum 25-hydroxyvitamin DCheck before bisphosphonate — Vit D deficiency can also cause bone pain + raised ALP; must correct before treatment (reduces hypocalcaemia risk)
X-ray of affected bonesTypical findings: lytic lesions (early), mixed lytic/sclerotic, thickened trabeculae, cortical thickening, bone expansion, deformity. Used for diagnosis, monitoring response, evaluating complications.
Isotope bone scanSensitive (not specific). Determines extent and distribution. Identifies which bones are involved (useful at diagnosis to establish baseline). NOT primarily for diagnosis.
CT / MRIIf diagnosis uncertain; exclude malignancy / metastasis; if neurological complications suspected.
Bone biopsyRarely required — excludes malignancy if diagnosis in doubt.
🎯

Treatment Goals & When to Treat

⚠️
Not all patients require treatment. Many are asymptomatic and may be monitored without intervention.
✅ Treat if Symptomatic
  • Pain in pagetic bone
  • Neurological complications
  • Significant osteolytic lesions
⚠️ Consider Treatment if Asymptomatic but High-Risk Site
  • Involvement of long bones (deformity/fracture risk)
  • Vertebrae (cord compression risk)
  • Base of skull (cranial nerve compression)
  • Significant joint involvement (e.g. hip)
  • Before surgery involving pagetic bone
  • Younger patients at risk of progression
  • Active disease with elevated ALP
Treatment goals:
  • Relieve symptoms (pain, neurological)
  • Prevent complications (fracture, deformity progression, hearing/vision loss)
  • Normalise ALP (secondary goal — if ALP remains elevated despite treatment, consider alternative cause first)
💡
Non-pharmacological: Simple analgesics and NSAIDs for pain; orthotics/shoe raises for deformity-related gait problems; surgical referral for: severe secondary OA, spinal cord/nerve compression, major deformity.

Monitoring Response

Main monitoring test: Serum total alkaline phosphatase (ALP)

Schedule:
• Check ALP every 3–6 months after treatment until it normalises
• After remission/stopping treatment: annually or every 2 years in stable remission
• Note: ALP may take >3 months to reach its lowest level after treatment (do not prematurely judge response)

If ALP stays elevated: Consider persistent active disease; alternative diagnosis; malignancy; need for repeat treatment later

Relapse: Common, often years later. Signs: rising ALP, recurrent bone pain, osteolytic lesion progression on imaging. Treat with further bisphosphonate course. Relapse less common and later with zoledronic acid; more common and earlier with risedronate.
💡
Normal ALP with persistent symptoms → consider alternative cause of pain (secondary OA, other bone disease). Continuous long-term treatment is NOT recommended — benefit beyond 2–3 years is unknown; treat based on disease activity.

Drug Summaries

Zoledronic Acid (Paget Disease)Bisphosphonate — Osteoclast Inhibitor (IV)
Dose (Paget Disease)
  • 5 mg IV over AT LEAST 15 minutes — single dose
  • Pre-treatment: 25-OHD >50 nmol/L | Corrected Ca normal | eGFR >35 | Well hydrated
  • Post-infusion calcium supplementation: Calcium carbonate 1.25–1.5 g PO daily for ≥10 days
Mechanism of Action
Binds hydroxyapatite at bone remodelling sites → internalized by osteoclasts → inhibits farnesyl pyrophosphate synthase → disrupts osteoclast function → ↓ bone resorption → normalises bone turnover → biochemical and clinical remission of Paget disease.
Indications
  • Paget disease of bone — treatment of choice
  • Osteoporosis (yearly dosing)
  • Hypercalcaemia of malignancy
  • Bone metastases / skeletal-related events in malignancy
Precautions & Contraindications
  • eGFR <35 mL/min — contraindicated
  • Uncorrected hypocalcaemia or Vit D deficiency — contraindicated (correct before treatment)
  • History of uveitis — caution (rare ADE)
  • Do NOT infuse more quickly than 15 minutes (acute renal failure risk)
  • Patient must be well hydrated before infusion
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Transient flu-like illness (fever, myalgia, bone pain) — especially first dose; self-limiting; paracetamol helps
Rare / Serious
  • Acute renal failure (if infused too quickly or dehydrated)
  • Uveitis (eye inflammation)
  • ONJ (very rare in Paget disease treatment)
  • Hypocalcaemia (especially if Vit D deficient or malabsorption)
Monitoring
  • ALP: check every 3–6 months until normalised → then annually
  • Pre-infusion: renal function, corrected Ca, 25-OHD
  • Hydration status before and after infusion
  • Signs of uveitis (eye pain, redness, visual changes) — rare but possible
Counselling
  • You will receive a single infusion. It works for a prolonged period — sometimes years before any repeat is needed.
  • Drink at least 2 glasses of water before and after the infusion.
  • Flu-like symptoms (fever, aching muscles, headache) may occur in the first few days — paracetamol can help. These are self-limiting.
  • Take calcium supplementation as prescribed for at least 10 days after the infusion.
  • Report: eye pain or redness (uveitis), jaw pain, unusual pain or swelling.
  • Regular blood tests (ALP) needed to monitor disease response.
Risedronate — Paget DiseaseBisphosphonate — Antiresorptive (Oral)
Dose (Paget Disease)
  • Risedronate: 30 mg PO once daily for 2 monthsTake on an empty stomach with a full glass of plain water. Remain upright ≥30 minutes. Do not eat, drink (except water), or take other medications for 30 minutes after dose.Note: this is a much higher dose than for osteoporosis (5 mg/day). Clinically significant remission is achievable but relapse occurs more often and earlier than with zoledronic acid.
Mechanism of Action
Bind hydroxyapatite in bone → internalised by osteoclasts → inhibit farnesyl pyrophosphate synthase → ↓ osteoclast function and survival → ↓ bone resorption → normalise bone turnover (↓ ALP) → biochemical and clinical remission.
Indications (Paget)
  • Paget disease — when IV zoledronic acid is not suitable (only oral bisphosphonate option for Paget)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Hypocalcaemia — contraindicated (correct before starting)
  • Significant upper GI disease, oesophageal stricture, Barrett's oesophagus — contraindicated
  • Renal impairment (CrCl <35 mL/min) — avoid
  • Vitamin D deficiency — correct before starting
  • Dental surgery — complete invasive dental work before starting; inform dentist
  • Pregnancy — contact pregnancy medicines information service; long half-life
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, abdominal pain, headache, musculoskeletal pain
  • Oesophagitis / oesophageal erosions — if not taken correctly (must remain upright 30 min)
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Gastritis, duodenitis, rash
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Osteonecrosis of the jaw (ONJ) — risk lower with oral than IV bisphosphonates
  • Atypical femoral fracture (prolonged use)
  • Ocular inflammation (uveitis, iritis, scleritis)
  • Severe musculoskeletal pain (rarely severe/disabling)
Monitoring
  • ALP every 3–6 months until normalised → then annually
  • Renal function, corrected calcium, and 25-OHD before starting
  • Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D supplementation during treatment
  • Dental review before starting where possible
Counselling
  • Take on an empty stomach with a full glass of plain water — first thing in the morning.
  • Remain upright (sitting or standing) for at least 30 minutes after taking — do NOT lie down.
  • Do not eat, drink (except plain water), or take other medications for 30 minutes after dose.
  • Tell your dentist you are taking a bisphosphonate before any dental work.
  • Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D intake daily.
  • Report: jaw pain, loose teeth, or exposed bone in mouth (ONJ risk); unexplained thigh, hip or groin pain (atypical fracture risk).
Pamidronate IV — Paget DiseaseBisphosphonate — Antiresorptive (IV)
Dose (Paget Disease)
  • Pamidronate IV: 60 mg as a single IV infusionDilute in sodium chloride 0.9% or glucose 5%. Infuse over ≥4 hours. Use a relatively large vein to reduce local reactions.If renal or hepatic impairment (mild-moderate) — reduce infusion rate to <20 mg/hour. Avoid if CrCl <30 mL/min unless life-threatening indication.
Mechanism of Action
Bind hydroxyapatite in bone → internalised by osteoclasts → inhibit farnesyl pyrophosphate synthase → ↓ osteoclast function and survival → ↓ bone resorption → normalise bone turnover. A single bisphosphonate course will often induce a long-lasting remission; consider re-treatment if clinically indicated.
Indications
  • Paget disease — IV alternative when zoledronic acid is not appropriate
  • Hypercalcaemia of malignancy
  • Osteolytic bone metastases (breast cancer, multiple myeloma)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Hypocalcaemia — contraindicated (correct before starting)
  • CrCl <30 mL/min — avoid unless life-threatening hypercalcaemia
  • Cardiac disease — risk of heart failure
  • Previous thyroid surgery — risk of hypocalcaemia
  • Mild-moderate renal or hepatic impairment — reduce infusion rate to <20 mg/hour
  • Dental surgery — complete invasive dental work before starting; inform dentist
  • Nephrotoxic drugs (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast) — additive renal risk
  • Pregnancy — limited data; contact pregnancy medicines information service
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%) — IV
  • Fever, flu-like symptoms (headache, myalgia, bone pain) — common in days after infusion
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, headache, musculoskeletal pain
  • Hypocalcaemia, hypophosphataemia, hypertension
  • ↑ Serum creatinine, injection site reaction
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Hypotension (rarely fainting or circulatory collapse)
  • Hypomagnesaemia, hypokalaemia
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Osteonecrosis of the jaw (ONJ) — higher risk than oral bisphosphonates; most reports with IV bisphosphonates in cancer patients
  • Atypical femoral fracture (prolonged use)
  • Renal impairment / acute renal failure — monitor creatinine before each dose
  • Nephrotic syndrome — monitor for early signs; prompt cessation may enable full recovery
  • Anaphylactic shock
  • Ocular inflammation (iritis, uveitis, scleritis)
  • Heart failure
Monitoring
  • Electrolytes (calcium, magnesium, phosphate) — monitor during treatment
  • Serum creatinine before each dose — withhold if deteriorates >10% above baseline (resume when returns to within 10% of baseline)
  • ALP every 3–6 months until normalised → then annually
  • Adequate hydration — maintain with IV sodium chloride 0.9% in hypercalcaemia
  • Monitor for early signs of nephrotic syndrome (proteinuria, oedema) with long-term use
  • Dental review before starting where possible
Counselling
  • Flu-like symptoms (fever, headache, bone pain) may occur in the days after the infusion — paracetamol can be taken to ease symptoms.
  • Tell your dentist you are taking a bisphosphonate before any dental work.
  • Report: jaw pain, loose teeth, or exposed bone in mouth (ONJ risk); unexplained thigh, hip or groin pain (atypical fracture).
  • Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D intake daily.
NSAIDs — Symptom Relief in Paget DiseaseAnalgesic / Anti-inflammatory
Doses (Paget Symptom Relief)
  • Celecoxib: 100–200 mg PO daily or BD until symptoms subside
  • Etoricoxib: 30–60 mg PO daily until symptoms subside
  • Ibuprofen IR: 200–400 mg PO 3–4×/day until symptoms subside
  • Diclofenac: 25–50 mg PO 2–3×/day until symptoms subside
  • Naproxen IR: 250–500 mg PO BD until symptoms subside
  • Ibuprofen IR: 200–400 mg PO 3–4×/day until symptoms subside
  • Diclofenac: 25–50 mg PO BD–TID until symptoms subside
  • Meloxicam: 7.5–15 mg PO daily until symptoms subside
Role in Paget Disease
Used for symptomatic pain relief (bone pain, secondary OA pain). Do NOT modify the underlying disease. Stop if no benefit, symptoms subside, or treatment is harmful.
Mechanism of Action
Inhibit cyclo-oxygenase (COX) enzymes → ↓ prostaglandin synthesis → ↓ inflammation, pain, and fever. COX-2 selective agents (celecoxib, etoricoxib, meloxicam) have lower GI adverse effects but similar renal and cardiovascular risks.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Peptic ulcer disease / GI bleeding history — contraindicated or use with PPI cover
  • Significant renal impairment (eGFR <30) — avoid
  • Severe heart failure — avoid
  • Allergy to aspirin or NSAIDs — contraindicated
  • Cardiovascular disease — ↑ risk of MI, stroke; use lowest dose for shortest duration
  • Hypertension — NSAIDs cause fluid retention and raise BP; monitor
  • Elderly — ↑ all risks; use lowest effective dose for shortest time
  • Anticoagulants (warfarin, DOACs) — ↑ bleeding risk; avoid or monitor closely
  • Renal impairment, dehydration, diuretic use, ACEi/ARB — Triple Whammy → AKI risk
  • Asthma — NSAID-sensitive asthma in ~10%; use with caution
  • Pregnancy — avoid in third trimester
Adverse Effects
Common
  • GI: nausea, dyspepsia, abdominal discomfort, diarrhoea (↓ with food or COX-2 selective agents)
  • Fluid retention, peripheral oedema, raised BP
  • ↑ Serum creatinine / renal impairment (dose-related)
Serious / Rare
  • GI ulceration, perforation, bleeding (↓ risk with COX-2 selective or PPI co-prescription)
  • Acute kidney injury (especially in elderly, CKD, heart failure, dehydrated patients)
  • Cardiovascular events (MI, stroke) — especially prolonged use
  • Bronchospasm (aspirin-sensitive asthma)
  • Hypersensitivity reactions, rash
Monitoring
  • Renal function, blood pressure, fluid status — especially in elderly, CKD, or heart failure
  • Signs of GI bleeding (black/tarry stools, haematemesis)
  • Avoid prolonged use without reassessment
Counselling
  • Take with food or milk to reduce stomach upset.
  • Use for the shortest time needed at the lowest effective dose.
  • Report immediately: black or tarry stools, vomiting blood, severe stomach pain.
  • Report: ankle swelling, sudden weight gain, reduced urine output, breathlessness.
  • Avoid combining with aspirin, anticoagulants, or other NSAIDs unless directed.
  • If you also take a diuretic and an ACE inhibitor or ARB — be cautious about using NSAIDs (Triple Whammy → kidney injury risk).

Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

Systemic autoimmune disease of synovial joints. Treat-to-target — aim for remission or low disease activity. Early, aggressive DMARD therapy is crucial to prevent joint destruction.

🔬

Pathophysiology

Core process: Autoimmune activation → persistent synovial inflammation → pannus formation → joint destruction.
Genetic susceptibility (HLA-DR4, HLA-DR1) + environmental triggers (smoking, periodontal infection, microbiome) → autoimmune activation
Anti-citrullinated protein antibodies (ACPA/anti-CCP) and Rheumatoid Factor (RF) formation — may precede symptoms by years
Macrophage + T-cell + B-cell activation → inflammatory cytokine cascade (TNFα, IL-1, IL-6, IL-17) → synovial inflammation
Synovial membrane hyperplasia → pannus formation (invasive granulation tissue) → cartilage and bone erosion
Systemic inflammation → extra-articular manifestations; ↑ CVD risk; fatigue; anaemia of chronic disease
⚠️

Risk Factors

🔒 Non-Modifiable
  • Female sex (3:1 F:M)
  • Age 40–60 (peak onset)
  • Family history / genetics (HLA-DR4)
  • ACPA/RF positive (predict severity)
✏️ Modifiable
  • Smoking — strongest modifiable RF; worsens severity; reduces DMARD response
  • Periodontal disease
  • Obesity
  • Silica dust exposure
🚩 Poor Prognosis Markers
  • High ACPA / RF titres
  • Smoking
  • Early joint erosions on imaging
  • High disease activity at baseline
  • Functional impairment
  • Extra-articular manifestations
🩺

Clinical Features

💡
Typical presentation: RA usually begins as a symmetrical inflammatory polyarthritis affecting small joints of the hands, wrists, and feet — especially MCP, PIP, wrist joints, and MTP joints. Large joints (elbows, shoulders, knees, ankles) and the cervical spine may also be involved. DIP joints and CMC joints are generally spared (key distinction from OA).
Early RA Indicators
🟡 Indicators Suggestive of Early RA
  • Swelling in 5 or more joints
  • Symptoms present for more than 6 weeks
  • Early morning stiffness lasting more than 1 hour
  • Symmetrical involvement
  • Bilateral compression tenderness of MTP joints
  • Positive RF or anti-CCP antibodies
  • Raised CRP or ESR without infection
  • Bony erosions on X-ray of wrists/hands/feet (present in <10% of early disease)
  • Rheumatoid nodules
  • Family history of RA
🔴 Common Symptoms
  • Joint pain
  • Joint swelling, warmth, redness
  • Morning stiffness (>1 hour — hallmark)
  • Fatigue
  • Weakness
  • Malaise (general feeling of being unwell)
  • Diffuse musculoskeletal pain
  • Late deformities: ulnar deviation, swan neck, boutonnière
  • Wrist synovitis → carpal tunnel syndrome
Systemic & Extra-Articular Features
🌡️ Systemic Features (more severe disease)
  • Anorexia and weight loss
  • Low-grade fever
  • Fatigue and malaise
  • Cytokines → circulate systemically:
    • Brain → fever
    • Muscle → catabolism → weakness
    • Liver → ↓ iron → anaemia of chronic disease
    • Vessels → endothelial dysfunction → ↑ CVD risk
🌐 Extra-Articular Manifestations (common)
  • Rheumatoid nodules — subcutaneous at pressure points (elbows, bony prominences); rarely in lungs, sclera, heart
  • Secondary Sjögren syndrome — dry eyes, dry mouth, dry mucous membranes
  • Interstitial lung disease / changes — often asymptomatic; pulmonary nodules, pleural effusion
  • Ocular — episcleritis, scleritis
  • Haematological — anaemia (most common), thrombocytosis, lymphadenopathy
  • Depression — common; associated with pain, fatigue, sleep disturbance
⚠️ Less Common Extra-Articular (longstanding/severe)
  • Vasculitic rash / cutaneous vasculitis
  • Peripheral neuropathy (mononeuritis multiplex)
  • Serositis (pericarditis, pleuritis)
  • Glomerulonephritis
  • Felty syndrome — RA + splenomegaly + neutropenia
  • Amyloidosis (very rare)
🚨 Important Consequences
  • Irreversible joint damage → deformity + disability
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome (wrist synovitis)
  • Atlanto-axial instability (cervical spine RA) → risk of cervical myelopathy → urgent neurosurgical referral if suspected
  • ↑ CVD risk — systemic inflammation → accelerated atherosclerosis (RA also causes a lipid paradox: active inflammation → lower LDL but still ↑ CVD risk)

Diagnosis & Investigations

ACR/EULAR 2010 Classification Criteria (score ≥6/10 → classify as RA):
Joint involvement (0–5) + Serology [RF/ACPA] (0–3) + Acute phase reactants [CRP/ESR] (0–1) + Duration ≥6 weeks (1)
TestRole
RF (Rheumatoid Factor)Positive in ~70–80% RA; not specific (also positive in Sjögren, HCV, elderly). High titres → worse prognosis.
Anti-CCP (ACPA)More specific than RF (~95%). Positive years before symptoms. Predicts erosive, severe disease.
CRP, ESRElevated in active disease. Used for disease activity monitoring.
FBCAnaemia of chronic disease common; leucopenia if on DMARDs.
X-rays hands/feetBaseline; joint space narrowing, periarticular osteopenia, erosions (late). Normal early.
Ultrasound / MRIMore sensitive for early synovitis and erosions than X-ray.
📉

Indicators of Poor Prognosis

⚠️
When any of these indicators are present, treatment should be more intensive and started as early as possible. No single feature is entirely reliable — the combination matters. Refer promptly to a rheumatologist, especially if indicators are present.
🔴 Disease Activity Markers
  • Persistent disease activity despite DMARD therapy
  • Sustained raised inflammatory markers (ESR or CRP)
  • Swelling in more than 20 joints
  • High disease activity at onset
  • Failure of ≥2 DMARDs
🧬 Serological / Imaging
  • High RF titre or positive high anti-CCP antibody
  • Bony erosions evident on X-ray early in disease
🦴 Functional
  • Impaired joint function early in disease
  • Extra-articular features present
  • Small joint involvement (MCP, PIP, wrists)
🎯

Treatment Goals

Primary (Treat-to-Target)
  • Achieve remission (preferred) OR low disease activity
  • Prevent joint erosion and structural damage
  • Maintain function, sleep, psychological health, and QoL
  • Enable participation in work and social activities
  • Detect and manage drug adverse effects
  • Manage comorbidities (CVD, osteoporosis, depression)
Disease Activity Targets (Remission)
  • DAS28 <2.6 = clinical remission
  • DAS28 <3.2 = low disease activity (acceptable if remission not achievable)
  • SDAI / CDAI criteria also used
  • ~20% of patients achieve full drug-free remission — most require lifelong therapy
  • When remission is sustained: consider stepwise dose reduction to lowest effective dose
⚠️
Key treatment principles: Start DMARD therapy as soon as possible (ideally within 3 months of symptom onset). Review and escalate every 3 months if target not met. Bridge with corticosteroids only — not for maintenance. A response to csDMARDs is usually apparent within 12 weeks. Most patients require lifelong therapy.
💡
Multidisciplinary approach — individualised self-management plan:
GP + Rheumatologist ± Physiotherapist ± Occupational Therapist ± Podiatrist ± Psychologist

Non-pharmacological:
  • Smoking cessation — essential; smoking worsens prognosis and reduces drug response
  • Exercise — aerobic + strengthening; benefits outweigh risks at all disease stages; low-impact options if pain limits activity; weight-bearing helps bone health
  • Diet — Mediterranean-style diet most accepted (CVD + weight benefits); fish oil ≥2.7 g omega-3 daily reduces symptom severity vs placebo
  • Weight management — obesity worsens inflammation, pain, and reduces drug efficacy
  • Joint protection education; splints; orthoses; thermotherapy; TENS
  • Psychological support — CBT, mindfulness; depression is common and must be managed
Comorbidity monitoring (both GP and specialist):
  • Cardiovascular disease — systemic inflammation → ↑ atherosclerosis risk. Manage all CV risk factors actively. RA also causes lipid paradox: active inflammation → lower LDL but still ↑ CV events.
  • Osteoporosis — active RA + corticosteroid use + inactivity = significant risk. Monitor BMD; treat appropriately.
  • Depression — common (chronic pain, fatigue, sleep disturbance, functional limitation). Screen and manage proactively.
  • Peptic ulcer disease — NSAID + corticosteroid use → gastroprotection with PPI where indicated.
📊
Disease activity monitoring: Monitor at frequency tailored to severity and treatment stage.
• Inflammatory signs — number of swollen or tender joints
• Inflammatory markers — CRP and ESR (RF and anti-CCP are NOT useful for monitoring disease activity after diagnosis)
• Patient-reported outcomes — pain, physical function, psychological health, sleep, exercise participation
• Residual joint pain without evidence of inflammation → likely joint damage or central sensitisation, not a flare — systemic corticosteroids are NOT recommended for residual non-inflammatory pain
💊

Pharmacological Treatment Ladder

💡
Corticosteroids — Adjunct to DMARD Therapy (Specialist-prescribed)

Systemic corticosteroids have both anti-inflammatory and disease-modifying effects in RA. They are prescribed by specialists for rapid symptom control at presentation, during a disease flare, or while awaiting a response to csDMARD therapy (which typically takes 6–12 weeks). Always use the lowest possible dose for the shortest possible duration.

Oral (usual initial dose): Prednisolone (or prednisone) 5–15 mg PO daily. Patients with RA are usually highly steroid sensitive — doses higher than this are rarely necessary and are associated with more adverse effects. Some patients may be started on low-dose oral CS by their GP while awaiting specialist review; if ineffective, prompt specialist referral is important.

Intramuscular (if indicated for severe symptoms): Methylprednisolone acetate 80–120 mg IM as a single dose. A single IM dose may have a prolonged effect (up to 8 weeks) and repeat doses are often unnecessary.

Although corticosteroids rapidly control inflammation, they are inadequate to prevent joint damage or achieve clinical remission. When remission or low disease activity is achieved, the specialist should supervise dose reduction and potential discontinuation.

Step 3 — b/tsDMARD (PBS authority; rheumatologist or immunologist only)
Biological DMARD (bDMARD) or Targeted Synthetic DMARD (tsDMARD)
📋
PBS Requirements to Access b/tsDMARDs (ALL must be met):
  • Clinical remission cannot be achieved with one or more csDMARDs, AND
  • Significant disease activity persists after trialling at least 2 csDMARDs
  • Prescribed on PBS authority by a rheumatologist or immunologist only
  • All b/tsDMARDs must be co-prescribed with a csDMARD (usually methotrexate)
  • PBS-specific MTX co-prescription requirement: Methotrexate must be co-prescribed when using abatacept, golimumab, or infliximab
💡
Drug choice is based on patient preference regarding route of administration and dosing frequency. If response to one bDMARD is insufficient, an alternative bDMARD may be trialled. Aim to dose-reduce and cease to csDMARD monotherapy if sustained remission achieved. Pre-treatment screening: TB (Mantoux + CXR), Hepatitis B serology, FBC, LFTs, lipids.

Special Situations

SituationRecommended Approach
Pregnancy planningStop MTX ≥3 months before conception; stop leflunomide + washout before conception. Continue HCQ (safe in pregnancy). Certolizumab preferred biologic in pregnancy (limited placental transfer). Prednisolone acceptable in pregnancy.
Surgery (perioperative)csDMARDs: do NOT pause routinely. bDMARDs: do not pause routinely for minor procedures; may pause for high infection-risk surgery (duration varies — discuss with specialist). tsDMARDs: consider pausing before/after major surgery (discuss with specialist). Restart once wound healed and no infection.
InfectionWithhold all DMARDs/bDMARDs/tsDMARDs during serious infection. Restart once infection fully resolved. Latent TB: treat before starting bDMARD/tsDMARD. Annual TB screening if on biologics.
VaccinationInactivated vaccines: annual flu; pneumococcal; Hep B; COVID-19 — give to all. Live vaccines: avoid during bDMARD/tsDMARD. Ideally vaccinate before starting immunosuppressive therapy. Pneumococcal vaccination recommended for all RA patients.
Dose reduction / discontinuationWhen target (clinical remission) is achieved and maintained, consider stepwise dose reduction to lowest effective dose. Dose reduction should only occur under specialist guidance after corticosteroids are discontinued. Abrupt cessation of b/tsDMARDs without prior dose reduction is not recommended.
🚨
Red Flags in RA — Urgent specialist referral required if ANY present:
Severe or escalating joint pain in a patient with RA may represent a flare, or a more serious condition (avascular necrosis, fracture, infection, malignancy, vasculitis).
  • Fever, weight loss, malaise
  • Acute severe pain — particularly acute monoarthritis (different from usual RA pattern)
  • Focal or diffuse muscle weakness
  • History of significant trauma
  • Night pain, neurogenic pain, or claudication
  • Rash or purpura
  • Features of cervical myelopathy (neck pain + upper/lower limb neurological features) → urgent neurosurgical referral
Residual Non-Inflammatory Joint Pain (RA in clinical remission):
Patient-reported pain without evidence of joint inflammation → likely joint damage, peripheral sensitisation, or central sensitisation — NOT a disease flare.

Management:
  • Systemic corticosteroids are NOT recommended (no inflammation to treat)
  • Address biopsychosocial and environmental contributing factors first
  • NSAIDs for mild–moderate pain — same agents and doses as for OA:

Drug Summaries

Oral NSAIDs Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs — Systemic
Doses (RA — Residual Non-Inflammatory Pain)
  • Celecoxib: 100–200 mg PO daily or BD
  • Etoricoxib: 30–60 mg PO daily
  • Ibuprofen IR: 200–400 mg PO 3–4×/day
  • Indometacin: 25–50 mg PO 2–4×/day
  • Ketoprofen MR: 200 mg PO daily
  • Meloxicam: 7.5–15 mg PO daily
  • Naproxen IR: 250–500 mg PO BD
  • Naproxen MR: 750–1000 mg PO daily
  • Piroxicam: 10–20 mg PO daily
  • Diclofenac: 25–50 mg PO 2–3×/day— Use minimum dose for shortest effective duration
Mechanism of Action
Inhibit COX-1 and COX-2 enzymes → ↓ prostaglandin synthesis.
COX-2 inhibition → analgesic and anti-inflammatory effects
COX-1 inhibition → impaired gastric mucosal protection, antiplatelet effects
Both → ↓ renal prostaglandins → ↓ renal blood flow and GFR (worsened by dehydration)
Indications
  • OA — second-line systemic analgesia
  • Gout — acute flare treatment and flare prophylaxis
  • RA — symptomatic pain relief (not disease-modifying)
  • Other inflammatory arthropathies and musculoskeletal pain
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Contraindicated/avoid: active PUD or GI bleeding, history of GI bleeding
  • Contraindicated/avoid: renal impairment (CrCl <25–30 mL/min), dehydration
  • Avoid: severe hepatic impairment, pregnancy, perioperative period
  • Caution: CVD, heart failure, hypertension
  • Caution: elderly, asthma (bronchospasm risk)
  • Caution: coagulation disorders, concurrent anticoagulants
  • Caution: inflammatory bowel disease
  • Caution: women trying to conceive
  • Triple Whammy: NSAID + ACEi/ARB + Diuretic → high AKI risk
Key Drug Interactions:
  • Antihypertensives — ↓ BP control, ↓ renal function
  • Anticoagulants (warfarin, DOACs) — ↑ bleeding risk
  • Methotrexate — ↑ methotrexate toxicity
  • Lithium — ↑ lithium levels/toxicity
  • Corticosteroids — ↑ GI bleed risk
  • Digoxin — NSAIDs ↓ renal function → ↑ digoxin levels
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Nausea, dyspepsia, diarrhoea
  • Headache, dizziness
  • GI ulceration or bleeding
  • Raised liver enzymes
  • Fluid retention, hypertension
Infrequent
  • Heart failure, hyperkalaemia, renal impairment
  • Bronchospasm, rash
Rare / Red Flags
  • Acute renal failure, MI, stroke
  • Severe hepatitis, severe skin reactions, anaphylaxis
Monitoring
  • Assess GI, renal, and CV risk before prescribing
  • Monitor renal function in high-risk patients or prolonged use
  • Check BP in hypertensive patients
  • Use minimum effective dose for shortest necessary duration
Counselling
  • Do NOT take when dehydrated (vomiting, diarrhoea, hot weather, fasting).
  • Seek urgent medical review for: ankle swelling, breathlessness, chest pain (CV events), black/tarry stools or coffee-ground vomit (GI bleed).
  • Do not take with aspirin for pain unless specifically advised.
  • Only one NSAID at a time — do not double up.
  • GI protection: if at high GI risk and NSAID essential, take with food or add a PPI.
Methotrexate + Folic AcidcsDMARD — Folate Antagonist
Dose (RA)
  • Oral: 10–25 mg PO once weekly on the same specified day each weekOral bioavailability plateaus above 15 mg/week — if >15 mg oral is inadequate, switch to SC
  • SC / IM: 10–25 mg subcutaneously or IM once weekly — use if oral >15 mg is inadequate or not toleratedBetter bioavailability at higher doses
  • Always co-prescribe: Folic acid 5–10 mg PO once weekly on a different day from methotrexateOR folic acid 5 mg PO daily on all days except methotrexate day
  • ⚠️ ONCE WEEKLY ONLY — never daily. Potentially fatal dosing error if given daily.
Mechanism of Action
Folate antagonist → inhibits dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR) → ↓ purine and pyrimidine synthesis → ↓ rapidly proliferating cells (immune cells) → ↓ inflammation. Also increases adenosine release → anti-inflammatory effect.
Indications
  • RA — first-line anchor DMARD
  • Psoriasis, psoriatic arthritis
  • Crohn disease, IBD
  • Malignancies (at higher doses)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Pregnancy & breastfeeding — absolutely contraindicated (teratogenic)
  • Significant renal impairment — avoid (MTX accumulates)
  • Significant hepatic impairment — avoid
  • Alcohol — avoid (synergistic hepatotoxicity)
  • Bone marrow suppression — caution
  • NSAIDs — may ↑ MTX toxicity (↓ renal clearance)
  • Trimethoprim — folate antagonist interaction → ↑ MTX toxicity; avoid combination
  • Effective contraception required in BOTH sexes during and ≥3 months after stopping
Adverse Effects
Common
  • GI: nausea, vomiting, mouth ulcers, diarrhoea
  • Fatigue, headache, alopecia
  • ↑ LFTs (dose-related)
Serious / Rare
  • Myelosuppression (pancytopenia) — reduce dose or stop; folate deficiency worsens risk
  • Hepatotoxicity / hepatic fibrosis (cumulative dose) — monitor LFTs; consider fibroscan/biopsy if cumulative dose >1.5g
  • Methotrexate pneumonitis — dry cough, dyspnoea, fever → STOP IMMEDIATELY and refer
  • Teratogenicity (category X)
Monitoring
  • FBC + LFTs + renal function + albumin: at baseline → 2-weekly for first month → monthly
  • Chest X-ray at baseline (pulmonary safety)
  • Reduce or stop if: WBC <3.5 × 10⁹/L, Plt <150 × 10⁹/L, LFTs >2× upper limit of normal
Counselling
  • ONCE WEEKLY only — never more often. Weekly day must be consistent and clearly marked.
  • Always take your folic acid as prescribed (different day from methotrexate).
  • Avoid all alcohol during treatment.
  • Use reliable contraception — both males and females. Do not try to conceive for ≥3 months after stopping.
  • Seek urgent medical attention for: fever, unusual bruising/bleeding, mouth sores, shortness of breath, or new cough.
  • Regular blood tests are mandatory — do not skip them.
LeflunomidecsDMARD — Pyrimidine Synthesis Inhibitor
Dose (RA)
  • Standard dose: 20 mg PO daily
  • If poorly tolerated: Reduce to 10 mg PO daily
💡
Clinical improvement usually starts after ~4 weeks and stabilises within 4–6 months. Active metabolite (teriflunomide) has a very long half-life (2–4 weeks) — adverse effects may be prolonged after stopping. Consider washout procedure if needed.
Mechanism of Action
Active metabolite (teriflunomide) inhibits dihydro-orotate dehydrogenase (DHODH) → ↓ de novo pyrimidine synthesis in leucocytes and other rapidly dividing cells → immunosuppressive, immunomodulating, and antiproliferative effects → ↓ lymphocyte proliferation and inflammation. Also has uricosuric effects.
Indications
  • RA — when methotrexate is contraindicated or not tolerated (first-line alternative)
  • Psoriatic arthritis
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Pregnancy — contraindicated (teratogenic in animals; stop immediately if pregnancy occurs; perform washout procedure)
  • Breastfeeding — contraindicated (excreted in milk)
  • Allergy to leflunomide or teriflunomide — contraindicated
  • History of Stevens-Johnson syndrome, TEN, or erythema multiforme — contraindicated
  • Severe immunodeficiency or bone marrow depression not due to RA — contraindicated
  • Lymphoproliferative disease within last 5 years or myelodysplasia — contraindicated
  • Serious or untreated infection (e.g. sepsis, abscess, hepatitis B, active TB) — contraindicated; stop drug and consider washout if serious infection develops
  • Hepatic impairment or ALT/AST >2× ULN — contraindicated
  • History of interstitial lung disease — ↑ risk of leflunomide-induced ILD
  • Combination with methotrexate — ↑ hepatotoxicity risk; monitor LFTs monthly
  • Other hepatotoxic drugs — ↑ risk of serious hepatic reactions; avoid if possible
  • Effective contraception required in BOTH sexes during and until drug levels undetectable after stopping (consider washout procedure to hasten elimination)
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Diarrhoea, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
  • ↑ Liver enzymes (usually mild and transient at start of treatment)
  • Alopecia (hair loss), rash, itch
  • Hypertension
  • Headache, dizziness, peripheral neuropathy (paraesthesia)
  • Weight loss, weakness
  • Bronchitis, pharyngitis, dyspnoea, pneumonia
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Severe hepatic injury (including liver failure — some fatal; most within first 6 months)
  • Interstitial lung disease (including pneumonitis)
  • Pancytopenia, agranulocytosis, anaemia
  • Stevens-Johnson syndrome, TEN, multi-organ hypersensitivity, vasculitis
  • Serious infection (including reactivation of latent TB and hepatitis B)
Monitoring
  • Baseline: BP, FBC, renal function, LFTs (ALT, AST), hepatitis B and C serology (in high-risk patients)
  • Every 2–4 weeks for first 3 months → every 3 months thereafter: BP, FBC, renal function, LFTs
  • Monthly if combined with methotrexate
  • If ALT/AST >3× ULN → stop leflunomide; use washout procedure
  • If ALT/AST 2–3× ULN → consider reducing to 10 mg; check ALT/AST weekly. If persistently >2× ULN → stop drug; washout procedure
  • Washout procedure (to hasten elimination): Colestyramine 8 g 3 times daily (or activated charcoal 50 g 4 times daily) for 11 days — seek specialist advice for modification
Counselling
  • Contact your doctor urgently if you develop: mouth ulcers or skin problems; cough or difficulty breathing; persistent fever, sore throat; unusual tiredness, bruising, bleeding, or pallor; abdominal pain or yellowing of the skin (jaundice).
  • Report any numbness or tingling in fingers or toes (peripheral neuropathy).
  • Do not drink alcohol — it may increase the risk of liver damage.
  • Reliable contraception is required — both males and females. Do not try to conceive until drug levels are confirmed undetectable after stopping.
  • Some vaccines should not be given during treatment — check with your doctor before receiving any vaccines.
  • Adverse effects may persist for some time after stopping due to the drug's very long half-life.
Sulfasalazine (Enteric-coated)csDMARD — Aminosalicylate + Sulfonamide
Dose (RA)
  • Starting dose: 1 g PO bd — increase up to a maximum of 1.5 g bd
  • Target / maintenance: 2–3 g daily in 2–3 divided doses (usual maintenance)
  • Maximum: 3 g daily in divided doses
Mechanism of Action
Anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressant via both the aminosalicylate (5-ASA) and sulfapyridine components. Exact mechanism unknown. Also impairs folic acid absorption.
Indications
  • RA — as monotherapy (less-severe disease or when other agents contraindicated) or as part of dual/triple csDMARD combination therapy
  • Ulcerative colitis; Crohn disease (limited role)
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (limited role)
  • Safe to continue perioperatively — do not stop for surgery
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Allergy to sulfonamides — contraindicated
  • Allergy to salicylates — contraindicated
  • Blood dyscrasias — ↑ risk of myelosuppression; monitor FBC
  • G6PD deficiency — ↑ risk of haemolysis
  • Renal impairment (eGFR <20 mL/min) — use with caution; monitor regularly
  • Severe hepatic impairment — caution; hepatically cleared
  • Asthma / severe allergy — may be exacerbated
  • Psoriasis — may worsen psoriasis
  • Pregnancy — safe to use; give folic acid 5 mg daily supplement
  • Breastfeeding — safe to use; infant diarrhoea has been reported
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, headache, rash
  • Reversible male infertility (oligospermia) — resolves on stopping
  • Haemolysis (usually not severe)
  • Yellow-orange discolouration of urine, skin, and tears — may permanently stain soft contact lenses
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Interstitial nephritis
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Blood dyscrasias (agranulocytosis, aplastic anaemia, thrombocytopenia)
  • Hepatitis
  • Fibrosing alveolitis, pancreatitis
  • Meningitis, arthralgia
Monitoring
  • Baseline: FBC, LFTs, renal function
  • Every 2–4 weeks for first 3 months → every 3 months for first year → every 6 months thereafter
  • 1 month after any dose change
  • Consider folic acid 5 mg daily supplementation — sulfasalazine impairs folic acid absorption (especially important in pregnancy)
Counselling
  • Take tablets with food to reduce stomach upset.
  • Your urine may turn dark orange; your skin and tears may become yellow-orange. Soft contact lenses may be permanently stained — consider daily disposables.
  • Take your folic acid supplement as prescribed.
  • Report urgently: fever, sore throat, unusual bruising or bleeding, pallor, mouth ulcers (blood disorder signs); yellowing of skin/eyes (hepatitis sign).
  • May take 2–3 months before benefit in RA is seen.
  • Male patients: reversible reduction in sperm count may occur during treatment — discuss with your doctor if planning a family.
Hydroxychloroquine (HCQ)csDMARD — Antimalarial
Dose (RA)
400 mg PO daily
Mechanism of Action
Accumulates in lysosomes → ↑ lysosomal pH → ↓ antigen processing and presentation → ↓ immune activation. Also inhibits Toll-like receptor signalling.
Indications
  • RA — part of triple therapy (MTX + SSZ + HCQ)
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) — cornerstone therapy
  • Sjögren syndrome
  • Safe in pregnancy
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Retinopathy / macular disease — avoid if pre-existing retinal disease
  • QT prolongation / arrhythmia risk
  • Psoriasis — may worsen
  • Haematological disorders
  • Interaction: tamoxifen → ↑ retinopathy risk
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Blurred vision, nausea, diarrhoea, rash, headache, alopecia
Rare / Serious
  • Retinopathy — cumulative dose-dependent; irreversible if not detected early
  • Cardiomyopathy, QT prolongation, arrhythmia
  • Agranulocytosis, severe hypoglycaemia
Monitoring (Retinopathy)
  • Ophthalmology review within 12 months of starting
  • Every 5 years if low risk → annually if high risk (dose >5 mg/kg/day, duration >10 years, renal impairment, macular disease, Asian ancestry)
Counselling
  • Take with food to reduce stomach upset.
  • Report any vision changes immediately — especially difficulty reading, seeing faces, colour changes. Do not wait for scheduled appointment.
  • Annual eye examinations are mandatory — never skip them.
  • Wear sunglasses outdoors (may reduce eye problems).

Patients with RA are usually highly steroid sensitive.
Mechanism of Action
Glucocorticoid receptor agonist → ↓ transcription of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) → broad anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive effects. Also ↓ vascular permeability and immune cell migration.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Active, uncontrolled infection — avoid (masks fever and may worsen infection)
  • Live vaccines — contraindicated during immunosuppressive doses
  • Diabetes — significant hyperglycaemia; monitor BGL closely
  • Hypertension / heart failure — fluid retention worsens; monitor BP
  • Peptic ulcer disease — ↑ ulcer risk; use with PPI
  • Osteoporosis — prolonged use causes bone loss; consider bisphosphonate prophylaxis
  • Psychiatric disorders — may precipitate or worsen psychosis, mania, depression
  • Glaucoma / cataracts — may worsen with prolonged use
  • Do NOT stop abruptly after prolonged use — taper to avoid adrenal insufficiency and disease rebound
Adverse Effects
Common (dose and duration dependent)
  • Weight gain, increased appetite, fluid retention, insomnia
  • Mood changes (euphoria or irritability)
  • Hyperglycaemia (may precipitate or worsen diabetes)
  • ↑ Blood pressure
  • GI: nausea, dyspepsia (take with food)
  • Skin: acne, easy bruising, striae, poor wound healing
  • Increased susceptibility to infection
Prolonged/High-dose — Serious
  • Adrenal suppression → adrenal crisis if stopped abruptly
  • Osteoporosis → fractures (especially vertebral)
  • Cushingoid features (moon face, buffalo hump, central obesity)
  • Avascular necrosis (especially femoral head)
  • Posterior subcapsular cataracts; glaucoma
  • Proximal myopathy
Monitoring
  • Blood glucose — especially in diabetic patients or with high doses
  • Blood pressure and fluid status; weight
  • Signs of infection (fever may be masked by corticosteroids)
  • BMD if prolonged use (≥3 months) — consider bisphosphonate prophylaxis
  • Eye pressure (IOP) if prolonged use
Counselling
  • Take in the morning with food to reduce GI upset and minimise sleep disturbance.
  • Do NOT stop suddenly — taper the dose gradually as directed. Stopping abruptly can cause adrenal crisis.
  • Carry a steroid card or medical alert if on prolonged therapy.
  • Report immediately: fever or signs of infection (the drug can mask fever).
  • Monitor blood glucose more frequently if diabetic.
  • Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D intake for bone protection.
  • Mood changes and insomnia are common — report severe psychiatric symptoms.

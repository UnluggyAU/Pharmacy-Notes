Ulcerative Colitis (UC)

Chronic, relapsing, immune-mediated inflammatory disease of the colon. Continuous mucosal inflammation starting in the rectum and extending proximally. Management depends on disease extent and severity.

🔬

Pathophysiology

Core process: Immune dysregulation (genetic + environmental) → continuous mucosal inflammation starting in the rectum → inflammation-driven symptoms (bleeding from friable mucosa, diarrhoea from impaired colonic absorption, urgency/tenesmus from rectal inflammation).
💡
Key pathological features: Inflammation confined mainly to colonic mucosa (NOT transmural like Crohn); disease is continuous (not patchy); always begins in rectum and extends proximally; crypt abscesses; gland destruction. Granulomas are absent (cf. Crohn).
⚠️
Long-term UC risk: UC for >8 years → ↑ colorectal cancer risk → periodic colonoscopy surveillance required.
⚠️

Risk Factors

🧬 Genetic
  • Family history (genetic influence)
  • Appendicectomy is protective against UC — reduces risk (note: appendicectomy increases Crohn risk)
🌍 Environmental
  • Western diet — highly processed foods, high refined sugar, low fibre
  • Ex-smokers at highest risk for UC onset; nicotine has some protective effect in active smokers
  • Stress — a recognised trigger for relapse
  • NSAIDs may exacerbate IBD
  • Altered microbiome — dysbiosis may predispose to UC; antibiotics may cause relapse by altering gut flora
  • Previous infection — may be a cause; limited evidence
⚠️ Complications That Increase Risk
  • Severe inflammatory IBD (hospitalised patients) → ↑ VTE risk → prophylaxis needed
  • Long-standing UC (>8 years) → ↑ colorectal cancer risk
🩺

Clinical Features

🔴 Core Symptoms
  • Diarrhoea (frequent small stools)
  • Blood in stool (very common) — key distinguishing feature from Crohn
  • Urgency
  • Tenesmus (feeling of needing to pass stool when bowel is empty) — especially with rectal involvement
🗺️ Disease Distribution → Symptom Implications
  • Proctitis: Limited to rectum; urgency and tenesmus often prominent
  • Distal / left-sided colitis: Extends proximally but not beyond splenic flexure
  • Extensive colitis: Extends proximal to splenic flexure
🌐 Extra-Intestinal Manifestations
  • Arthropathy
  • Erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum
  • Uveitis, episcleritis, scleritis
  • Hepatobiliary disease
🔍 Differentiating UC from Crohn Disease
⚠️
Always exclude infection before assuming UC flare — especially important in patients on immunomodulatory therapy (C. difficile, CMV, bacterial).

Diagnosis & Investigations

Diagnosis of UC when: (1) Typical clinical features; (2) Endoscopic findings consistent with UC; (3) Histology consistent with UC; (4) Infectious causes excluded.

Key investigations:
• Colonoscopy with biopsy — gold standard
• Faecal calprotectin — cut-off 50 mcg/g: NPV ~100% (good rule-out); less sensitive for proximal disease
• FBC, CRP/ESR, urea/electrolytes
• Abdominal X-ray on admission (ASUC) — monitor for colonic dilatation
• Stool cultures — exclude C. difficile, CMV, amoebic colitis, bacterial causes
• CMV exclusion by sigmoidoscopy + histology before salvage therapy in ASUC

ASUC requires hospital admission, early specialist management, and early surgical consultation.

🎯

Treatment Goals

Primary
  • Induce remission in active disease
  • Maintain corticosteroid-free remission
  • Achieve mucosal healing (endoscopic remission)
  • Prevent relapse
Why Mucosal Healing Matters
  • ↓ Hospital admissions
  • ↓ Need for surgery
  • ↓ Colorectal cancer risk
🚨
NEVER use corticosteroids for maintenance therapy — not more effective than placebo for preventing relapse, don't induce mucosal healing, and have major long-term adverse effects.
💡
Non-pharmacological: Adequate energy, protein, micronutrients; nutritional supplements if inadequate; enteral nutrition in severe disease (rarely parenteral); restrict insoluble fibre/lactose during exacerbation if symptomatically helpful; avoid foods that worsen symptoms; fibre restriction NOT routinely required. FMT: some benefit in mild-moderate active colitis in trials, but not recommended for routine use. Probiotics: not shown to be effective.
📋
Before starting treatment, consider:
  • Active or latent infection — TB, Hepatitis B, HIV
  • Immunisation requirements — especially live vaccines before starting biologics; Hepatitis B vaccination if at risk
  • History of malignant disease

Acute Severe Ulcerative Colitis (ASUC): Treatment

⚠️
Critical safety points in ASUC:
  • VTE prophylaxis is mandatory — hospitalised severe IBD markedly increases VTE risk
  • Avoid antidiarrhoeals (loperamide), anticholinergics, and minimise opioids — may precipitate toxic megacolon
  • If perforation occurs → emergency colectomy
  • Exclude infection before salvage therapy — CMV by sigmoidoscopy and histology
1
Admit to hospital — early specialist + surgical consultation
IV Corticosteroids + Supportive Care
Hydrocortisone IV100 mg IV every 6 hours for 3–7 days
or Methylprednisolone sodium succinate 60 mg IV daily (single or divided doses)
PLUS: Fluids, electrolyte replacement, blood replacement if required. VTE prophylaxis mandatory.
🔵 Monitor daily: stool count; FBC including CRP; urea/electrolytes; AXR if symptoms change 🔵 Switch to oral CS when disease activity subsides; ensure response maintained before discharge
2
Review Day 3 & Day 7 for adequate response
Assess Response (Oxford Criteria)
Lack of adequate response:
• Day 3: stool frequency ≥8/day, OR ≥3/day with CRP >45 mg/L
• Day 7: ≥3 stools/day with visible blood
→ If criteria met → Salvage therapy OR colectomy
3
Salvage therapy — Exclude CMV first (sigmoidoscopy + histology). Do NOT delay.
Medical Salvage: Infliximab OR Ciclosporin
Infliximab5 mg/kg IV over 2 hours

Maintenance Therapy (UC)

🚨
Corticosteroids are NOT for maintenance — not more effective than placebo for relapse prevention. Usually lifelong therapy is required. De-escalation only in stable disease with mucosal healing evidence on endoscopy — consult gastroenterologist.
1
First-line maintenance for most patients (~50% reduction in relapse rate)
5-ASA Maintenance (Oral ± Rectal)
Oral 5-ASAMesalazine 1–3 g/day | Sulfasalazine 1–2 g/day | Balsalazide 3 g/day | Olsalazine 1–2 g/day
✅ Patients who responded to rectal mesalazine → ideally continue rectal maintenance: 1–4 g rectally 2–3 times per week
2
Severe initial disease or frequent relapses despite 5-ASA maintenance
Add Thiopurine (Steroid-Sparing)
Azathioprine / Mercaptopurine
  • Azathioprine: 2–2.5 mg/kg PO daily (range 1–3 mg/kg/day — adjust to response)
  • Mercaptopurine: 1–1.5 mg/kg PO daily (round to nearest 25 mg half-tablet)
✅ Maintain remission and corticosteroid-sparing effect — usually started in acute disease when CS begins (full effect takes up to 3 months) ❌ Check TPMT and NUDT15 genotypes before starting — poor metabolisers at risk of severe myelosuppression ❌ Allopurinol or febuxostat — both ↑ thiopurine toxicity; see shunting protocol below for intentional low-dose combination ⚠️ Full effect may take 3 months — co-prescribe 5-ASA or CS during initiation 🔵 Monitor: FBC weekly for 2 months → then monthly. LFTs periodically. Check TPMT/NUDT15 at baseline.
⚠️
Thiopurine shunting (6-MMP pathway): Some patients metabolise thiopurines to 6-MMP (instead of active 6-TGN) → adverse effects (hepatitis, nausea) + ↓ efficacy.
Confirm by measuring thiopurine metabolites. If shunting confirmed:
→ Reduce azathioprine/mercaptopurine dose to ⅓ of current dose
→ Add allopurinol 100 mg PO daily
If thiopurines not tolerated → Tioguanine 20 mg PO once or twice daily
3
If remission was induced with biologic / advanced therapy
Continue Biologic for Maintenance
Biological/Advanced Therapies
• Adalimumab  |  Golimumab  |  Infliximab  |  Tofacitinib
• Ustekinumab  |  Vedolizumab
✅ Continue same biologic used for induction if patient responded ⚠️ Combination infliximab + aza/merca gives higher remission rates — evidence not established for adalimumab, golimumab, vedolizumab, tofacitinib combinations
💡
After ileal pouch-anal anastomosis (proctocolectomy): Up to 50% develop pouchitis — treat with metronidazole or ciprofloxacin; repeated courses may be required.

Drug Summaries

Azathioprine / Mercaptopurine / Tioguanine (UC)Immunomodulator — Purine Antimetabolite
Doses (UC Maintenance)
  • Azathioprine: 2–2.5 mg/kg PO dailyRange 1–3 mg/kg/day; adjust to response. Lower end initially, increase over several weeks if tolerated.
  • Mercaptopurine: 1–1.5 mg/kg PO once dailyRound to nearest 25 mg half-tablet. Take on empty stomach (1h before or 2h after food); may take with food if GI upset.
  • Tioguanine (if thiopurines not tolerated): 20 mg PO once or twice daily
Mechanism of Action
Purine antimetabolites. Azathioprine is metabolised via mercaptopurine to thioguanine nucleotides → interfere with purine synthesis → impair lymphocyte proliferation, cellular immunity and antibody responses → ↓ intestinal inflammation and maintain remission.
Indications (UC)
  • Maintain remission — for corticosteroid-dependent or frequently relapsing UC
  • Corticosteroid-sparing effect
  • Usually started during acute disease when corticosteroid treatment begins (full effect takes up to 3 months)
⚠️
Thiopurine shunting (6-MMP pathway): Some patients metabolise thiopurines to 6-MMP (instead of active 6-TGN) → adverse effects (hepatitis, nausea) + ↓ efficacy.
Confirm by measuring thiopurine metabolites. If shunting confirmed:
→ Reduce azathioprine/mercaptopurine dose to ⅓ of current dose
→ Add allopurinol 100 mg PO daily
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Check TPMT and NUDT15 genotypes before starting — poor metabolisers at risk of severe myelosuppression; avoid or reduce dose
  • Allopurinol or febuxostat — both ↓ azathioprine/mercaptopurine metabolism → ↑ toxicity risk; dose reduction required or use shunting protocol
  • Hypersensitivity to azathioprine or mercaptopurine — contraindicated
  • Serious active infection — contraindicated
  • Hepatic impairment — may require lower dose; monitor LFTs carefully
  • Renal impairment (CrCl <20 mL/min) — consider dose reduction
  • Myelosuppressive drugs — ↑ risk of severe myelosuppression
  • Pregnancy — appears relatively safe; limit to conditions where stopping may be harmful (e.g. IBD)
  • Breastfeeding — appears safe; consider monitoring infant
  • Avoid excessive sun exposure — photosensitivity; ↑ risk of non-melanoma skin cancer with long-term use
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Myelosuppression (leucopenia, thrombocytopenia, anaemia) — dose-related
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, anorexia, mouth ulcers
  • Infection (↑ risk due to immunosuppression)
  • Alopecia
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Hepatitis — especially with doses >2.5 mg/kg/day
  • Hypersensitivity syndrome (fever, rash, arthralgia, pancreatitis)
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Hepatic sinusoidal obstruction syndrome (usually after 1–2 years, mainly males)
  • Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis
  • Pancreatitis, pneumonitis, interstitial nephritis
Monitoring
  • Baseline: TPMT and NUDT15 genotyping; FBC; LFTs; renal function
  • FBC weekly for 2 months → then monthly (or more frequently if clinically indicated)
  • LFTs periodically throughout treatment
  • Reduce dose or stop if white cell count is low or falls rapidly
  • Thiopurine metabolite levels (6-TGN / 6-MMP) if suboptimal response or suspected toxicity — guides shunting protocol
Counselling
  • Swallow tablets whole — do not break, chew or crush.
  • Azathioprine: take with food to reduce stomach upset. Mercaptopurine: best taken on an empty stomach (1h before or 2h after food) — avoid dairy products.
  • Tell your doctor immediately if you develop fever, increased bruising, bleeding, or signs of infection.
  • Protect skin from sun — wear protective clothing and SPF30+ sunscreen (↑ skin cancer risk with long-term use).
  • Regular blood tests are essential — do not skip them.
  • Full effect takes weeks to months — do not stop without medical advice.
5-Aminosalicylates (5-ASA): Mesalazine, Sulfasalazine, Balsalazide, OlsalazineAminosalicylate — Anti-inflammatory
Doses (Induction / Maintenance)

  • MAINTENANCE (oral)Mesalazine: 1–3 g/day | Sulfasalazine: 1–2 g/dayBalsalazide: 3 g/day | Olsalazine: 1–2 g/day

Mechanism of Action
Exact mechanism unknown. 5-ASA (mesalazine) is the active component. Topical anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive effects in the gut mucosa. Thought to modulate inflammatory mediators especially leukotrienes and inhibit TNF. MOA appears primarily topical (local).

Prodrugs: Sulfasalazine = 5-ASA + sulfapyridine (sulfapyridine → additional side effects). Olsalazine = 2 × 5-ASA linked. Balsalazide → releases 5-ASA in colon.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Allergy to salicylates (aspirin) — contraindicated
  • Renal impairment (CrCl <20 mL/min) — use with caution; monitor renal function
  • Hepatic impairment — caution
  • Mesalazine: different brands have different release characteristics → NOT interchangeable. Salofalk contains sucrose (hereditary fructose intolerance concern).
  • Sulfasalazine: contains sulfonamide → ↓ folate absorption → folic acid supplementation; can cause reversible male infertility; additional FBC/LFT/RFT monitoring required
  • Olsalazine: take with food (↓ diarrhoea)
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Nausea, rash, headache, diarrhoea — more common with higher doses
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Interstitial nephritis
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Blood dyscrasias
  • Pancreatitis (reversible)
  • Hepatitis
Monitoring
  • Check renal and liver function at baseline → repeat every 3 months in the first year → every 6 months thereafter
  • Sulfasalazine: additional FBC, LFT, RFT monitoring required — same schedule as above plus 1 month after dose change
  • Caution in renal impairment (CrCl <20 mL/min) — monitor carefully
  • Breastfeeding: monitor infant for diarrhoea
Counselling
  • Mesalazine: swallow tablets/granules whole. Check product-specific instructions — Salofalk tablets 1h before food; Pentasa regardless of food. Brands are NOT interchangeable — do not substitute without pharmacist/doctor advice.
  • Olsalazine: take with food to reduce diarrhoea.
  • Sulfasalazine: take with food. Urine may turn orange; skin/tears may become yellow-orange; soft contact lenses may stain permanently.
  • Rectal preparations: best administered at bedtime lying on left side. Suppositories for isolated proctitis only.
  • Regular blood and urine tests are needed to monitor kidney and liver function.
Rectal Corticosteroids (Budesonide foam, Hydrocortisone foam, Prednisolone enema/suppository)Corticosteroid — Rectal / Local
Doses
  • Budesonide 2 mg/applicatorful rectal foam (Budenofalk): 1 applicatorful rectally once daily for up to 6–8 weeksIdeally use after a bowel motion. Lowest systemic bioavailability of rectal preparations.
  • Hydrocortisone acetate 10% foam: 1 applicatorful rectally once or twice daily
  • Prednisolone enema 20 mg/100 mL: 1 enema rectally at night until remission achievedAdults and children >12 years. Reaches to splenic flexure.
  • Prednisolone suppository 5 mg: twice daily until remission achievedAdults and children >2 years. For isolated proctitis only.
Mechanism of Action & Notes
Local rectal anti-inflammatory effect via glucocorticoid receptor activation → ↓ mucosal cytokine production → ↓ colonic inflammation. Less systemic absorption than oral corticosteroids — reduced systemic ADE risk but not zero (budesonide foam has lowest systemic bioavailability of the rectal preparations). Continue until symptoms resolve then taper over several weeks. Generally better tolerated at bedtime.
Adverse Effects (Local & Systemic)
Common — Local
  • Rectal/anal discomfort, bloating, urgency during administration
Systemic (with prolonged use / high doses)
  • Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis suppression — less than oral, but possible with prolonged high-dose use
  • Hyperglycaemia (less common than systemic CS)
  • Insomnia, mood changes
Precautions
  • Rectal infection or recent rectal surgery — use with caution
  • Diabetes — monitor BGL if using large doses or prolonged courses
  • Do not stop abruptly after prolonged use — taper dose
Counselling
  • Best administered at bedtime, lying on the left side — helps retain the preparation.
  • Suppositories: for isolated proctitis only (limited reach). Foam: reaches sigmoid colon. Enema: reaches up to splenic flexure.
  • Try to retain the preparation for as long as possible.
  • Report: significant rectal bleeding, persistent fever, or symptoms worsening despite treatment.
Tacrolimus (Rectal)Calcineurin Inhibitor — Rectal (unlicensed/compounded)
Dose
2 mg rectally each evening for ≥4 weeks until clinical response → reduce frequency per response
Notes
Calcineurin inhibitor — blocks T-cell activation and cytokine production. Topical rectal use for unresponsive distal UC. Not TGA-registered for this use — available through hospital pharmacy or compounding pharmacies. Similar toxicity concerns to systemic ciclosporin (nephrotoxicity, hypertension, neurotoxicity, QT prolongation risk) — though topical exposure is lower.
Ciclosporin IV — ASUC SalvageCalcineurin Inhibitor — Immunosuppressant
Dose (Severe UC — IV)
  • IV continuous infusion: 2 mg/kg/day as a continuous IV infusionSwitch to oral ciclosporin when appropriate (oral dose ≈ 2–3× the IV dose). Adjust according to response and blood concentration monitoring.
Mechanism of Action
Calcineurin inhibitor — forms complex with cyclophilin → blocks calcineurin in activated T cells → prevents production of interleukin-2 and other cytokines → ↓ T cell proliferation and differentiation → rapid immunosuppression of intestinal inflammatory cascade.
Indications (UC)
  • Salvage therapy for ASUC not responding adequately to IV corticosteroids after 3–5 days
  • Bridge therapy while awaiting effect of thiopurines (or infliximab if the latter is used instead)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Uncontrolled hypertension — contraindicated for non-transplant indications
  • Malignancy — contraindicated for non-transplant indications
  • Renal impairment — contraindicated unless disease is damaging renal function and other treatment has failed
  • Active serious infection — high infection risk on combined immunosuppression; screen for TB, Hep B before use
  • Nephrotoxic agents (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast) — additive nephrotoxicity; avoid
  • Potassium / K⁺-sparing diuretics / ACEi / ARBs — ↑ hyperkalaemia risk
  • Many significant drug interactions via CYP3A4 — check before starting or stopping any medication
  • Contains ethanol in formulation
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Nephrotoxicity — dose-related (acute, reversible with dose reduction); chronic nephrotoxicity with long-term use (may be irreversible)
  • Hypertension
  • Neurotoxicity — tremor and headache most frequent; paraesthesia, confusion, seizures
  • Hyperkalaemia, hypomagnesaemia
  • Hyperglycaemia, hypercholesterolaemia
  • Opportunistic infection
  • Gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism (more common in children)
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Haemolytic uraemic syndrome
  • Reversible posterior leucoencephalopathy syndrome (RPLS)
Monitoring
  • Serum creatinine and BP — monitor closely; reduce dose if either increases significantly
  • Electrolytes (especially potassium, magnesium); blood glucose; lipids; FBC; LFTs
  • Blood ciclosporin concentration — trough levels or AUC; target varies; seek specialist guidance
  • Monitor every 2 weeks for first few months until stable → then every 1–3 months
  • Grapefruit juice and many drugs interact — check all concurrent medications
Counselling (when transitioned to oral)
  • Swallow capsules whole; take 12 hours apart at the same times each day.
  • Clean teeth and gums regularly (gingival hyperplasia risk).
  • Avoid sun exposure, wear protective clothing and use sunscreen.
  • Tell your doctor and pharmacist about all medications — many drugs interact with ciclosporin including grapefruit juice.
  • Regular blood tests and BP checks are mandatory.
Advanced Therapies (UC) — TNFα Inhibitors, Vedolizumab, Ustekinumab, JAK Inhibitors, S1P ModulatorsBiological / Advanced Therapy — Moderate-Severe UC
UC-Specific Drug Notes & Roles
Infliximab (TNFα — IV): 5 mg/kg at 0, 2, 6 weeks → every 8 weeks. Induction + maintenance for moderate-severe UC; salvage therapy in ASUC (if IV CS inadequate after 3 days). Combination with azathioprine/mercaptopurine reduces anti-drug antibody formation. Stop if no response by 14 weeks.

Adalimumab (TNFα — SC): Induction + maintenance for moderate-severe UC with inadequate response to conventional therapy or TNFα antagonists.
Golimumab (TNFα — SC): Induction + maintenance for moderate-severe UC; used rarely compared to infliximab/adalimumab.

Vedolizumab (gut-selective integrin antagonist — IV/SC): Induction + maintenance; moderate-severe UC. Slower onset than TNFα antagonists but gut-selective — fewer serious systemic infections. Consider when systemic immunosuppression is a concern or after TNFα failure.

Ustekinumab (IL-12/23 — IV load then SC): Induction + maintenance; moderate-severe UC. Consider in those with inadequate response to infliximab.

Tofacitinib (JAK inhibitor — oral): Induction + maintenance; moderate-severe UC with inadequate response to TNFα antagonists. VTE risk — caution in patients >65, smoking, CVD.
Upadacitinib (JAK inhibitor — oral): Induction + maintenance; moderate-severe UC with inadequate response to conventional therapy or TNFα antagonists.

Ozanimod / Etrasimod (S1P receptor modulators — oral): Induction + maintenance; moderate-severe UC. Comparative trials are limited.
Shared Precautions & Contraindications
  • Active or serious infection — contraindicated
  • Active TB — contraindicated; screen (Mantoux + CXR) and treat latent TB before starting
  • Active Hepatitis B — contraindicated; screen before starting (reactivation risk)
  • Live vaccines — avoid during treatment; vaccinate before starting where possible
  • Infliximab: moderate-severe heart failure, demyelinating disease (MS) — avoid
  • JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, upadacitinib): VTE risk, smoking, cardiovascular disease, elderly >65 — caution; also ↑ infection and malignancy risk
  • Anti-drug antibodies (particularly with infliximab) can reduce response and increase infusion reactions — consider monitoring drug/antibody levels
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Infections (URTI, opportunistic infections)
  • Injection site or infusion reactions (rash, flushing, headache, chills)
  • Headache, arthralgia, fatigue
Rare / Serious
  • Serious infections (TB reactivation, opportunistic infections — Pneumocystis, fungal)
  • Malignancy — lymphoma (particularly with TNF-α antagonists)
  • Infliximab: severe infusion reactions; lupus-like syndrome
  • Vedolizumab: hypersensitivity (GI symptoms, headache, dizziness, itch) — if anaphylaxis occurs, further use contraindicated
  • Tofacitinib (JAK inhibitor): VTE (DVT, PE), cytopenia, herpes zoster, hyperlipidaemia
Monitoring
  • Screen for TB and Hepatitis B before starting (all biologics)
  • FBC, LFTs, renal function at baseline and regularly
  • Infliximab: stop if no response within 14 weeks; monitor drug levels and anti-drug antibodies if suboptimal response
  • Tofacitinib: FBC, LFTs, lipids at baseline → monitor regularly; watch for VTE symptoms
  • Annual TB screening and skin cancer checks during ongoing treatment
Counselling
  • Report fever or signs of infection urgently — infections can be serious on these medications and may progress rapidly.
  • Check with your doctor before getting any vaccines — live vaccines must be avoided during treatment.
  • Have recommended vaccinations (flu, pneumococcal, Hep B) before starting biologic therapy if possible.
  • Infliximab infusions: tell the nurse immediately if you develop breathlessness, chest tightness, rash, or chills during or after infusion.
  • Vedolizumab: report any allergic reaction during or after infusion — if severe reaction (anaphylaxis) occurs, further treatment is contraindicated.
  • Tofacitinib: watch for signs of blood clots (leg swelling, pain, sudden breathlessness) — seek urgent medical help. Also watch for shingles (painful blistering rash).
  • Tell all healthcare providers you are on biologic therapy before any procedure or new medication.
  • Regular skin cancer checks are recommended — protect skin from sun.

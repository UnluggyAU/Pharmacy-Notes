Dementia

A progressive, life-limiting syndrome of cognitive and functional decline. Management is predominantly non-pharmacological. Drug therapy has a limited but important role — benefits are modest, harms are real, and all prescribing decisions require careful benefit–harm analysis.

📊

Epidemiology & Burden

🇦🇺 Australian Statistics (2025)
  • ~433,300 Australians living with dementia
  • 29,000 have younger-onset dementia (age 30–64)
  • 1 in 10 Australians aged ≥65 has dementia
  • ⅔ of people with dementia live in the community
  • 50% of people in residential aged care have dementia
  • 2nd leading cause of death in Australia; leading cause of death for Australian women
  • ~$3.7 billion in health and aged care spending directly attributed to dementia (2020–21)
  • More common in females; predicted to increase as population ages
⚠️ Important Distinctions
  • Dementia is NOT a normal part of ageing — it is a progressive, life-limiting disease
  • Most people with dementia are older, but not all older people get dementia
  • Diagnosis requires exclusion of reversible causes — especially delirium and depression (depressive pseudodementia)
  • Up to 44% of Australian aged care residents are on an antipsychotic, despite estimates that only ~10% of this prescribing is appropriate
  • The Royal Commission into Aged Care highlighted the critical need for antipsychotic deprescribing

Pathophysiology by Subtype

🧠 Alzheimer's Disease (AD) — 50–75% of cases
  • β-amyloid plaques: APP cleaved by β-secretase + γ-secretase (not α-secretase) → insoluble amyloid-β aggregates outside neurons → disrupts inter-neuronal signalling, triggers neuroinflammation, amyloid angiopathy (vessel weakening → haemorrhage risk)
  • Neurofibrillary tangles: Plaque buildup → kinase activation → Tau hyperphosphorylation → Tau misfolded → microtubules unsupported → intracellular Tau tangles → impaired neuronal transport → cell death
  • Cholinergic deficit: Loss of cholinergic neurons (hippocampus, entorhinal cortex) → ↓ acetylcholine → memory and cognition impaired → basis for AChEI therapy
  • Glutamate excitotoxicity: Excess NMDA-receptor activation → neuronal damage → basis for memantine
  • Genetics: Sporadic (95%): APOE ε4 (chr 19) — heterozygous 2–3×, homozygous 8–12× ↑ risk. Familial (5–10%): PSEN1, PSEN2, or APP mutations; Trisomy 21 — extra APP gene → ↑ amyloid
  • Brain changes: Hippocampal atrophy → cortical thinning → gyral narrowing → sulcal widening → ventricular enlargement
🩸 Vascular Dementia — 20–30% of cases
  • Mechanism: Atherosclerosis → reduced cerebral blood flow OR embolic occlusion of small perforating arteries → ischaemic strokes → liquefactive necrosis of brain tissue
  • Progression: Cumulative loss of neurons from multiple infarctions → stepwise or gradual cognitive decline (multi-infarct dementia)
  • Location-dependency: Symptoms depend on infarct location — temporal lobe: memory; frontal: personality; left parietal: aphasia; brainstem: gait/falls
  • Risk factors: Hypertension, diabetes, hypercholesterolaemia, obesity, smoking, atrial fibrillation, prior stroke
  • Imaging: CT or MRI — multiple cortical/subcortical infarcts, lacunar infarcts, white matter changes (leukoaraiosis)
  • Management focus: Treat vascular risk factors to prevent further strokes; no specific pharmacotherapy for cognition
⚡ Lewy Body Dementia (LBD) — 5–15% of cases
  • Mechanism: Alpha-synuclein misfolds → aggregates into Lewy bodies (eosinophilic intraneuronal inclusions) depositing in cortex and substantia nigra
  • Dopaminergic loss: Lewy bodies in substantia nigra → dopaminergic neuron death → parkinsonism (postural instability, bradykinesia; tremor less prominent than Parkinson's)
  • Cholinergic loss: Cortical Lewy bodies → cholinergic neuron loss → dementia and visual hallucinations → basis for AChEI therapy in LBD
  • DLB vs PDD distinction: LBD — dementia precedes or occurs within 12 months of parkinsonism. Parkinson's disease dementia (PDD) — parkinsonism established >12 months before dementia onset
  • Overlap: Also associated with amyloid plaque pathology (overlap with AD); alpha-synuclein pathology also seen in Parkinson's disease — likely a spectrum
🗣️ Frontotemporal Dementia (FTD) — up to 10% of cases
  • Mechanism: Intracellular inclusions of abnormal protein (hyperphosphorylated Tau or TDP-43) in frontal and temporal lobes → neuronal dysfunction and apoptosis; parietal and occipital lobes largely spared
  • Pick bodies (Pick's disease): 3R Tau isoforms only (unlike AD tangles which contain both 3R and 4R). A subtype of FTD — a tauopathy
  • Behavioural variant (most common): Frontal lobe atrophy → personality changes, disinhibition, impulsivity, repetitive behaviours, apathy, hypersexuality, hyperorality
  • Language variants: Temporal lobe atrophy → progressive aphasia (semantic variant: word meaning loss; non-fluent variant: effortful speech)
  • Age: Younger onset (40–75 years) than AD. Memory relatively preserved early; visuospatial function usually preserved (unlike AD)
  • Treatment: No pharmacotherapy — do NOT use AChEIs or memantine; management is behavioural and supportive only

Diagnosis & Assessment

💡
Dementia is diagnosed when cognitive decline affects a patient's ability to function independently AND reversible causes have been excluded. It is a clinical diagnosis — no single test confirms it.
🩺 Diagnostic Components
  • History of progressive cognitive decline affecting independent function — from patient AND a reliable collateral source (significant other)
  • Full medical history including drug and alcohol intake — many drugs cause reversible cognitive impairment (anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, opioids)
  • Physical and neurological examination
  • Investigations to exclude reversible causes (see below)
  • Cognitive assessment with a validated tool
🔬 Investigations — Exclude Reversible Causes
  • FBC — anaemia, infection
  • UEC + calcium — metabolic disturbances, organ failure
  • LFTs — investigate if abnormal
  • Blood glucose — hyper/hypoglycaemia
  • Thyroid function tests — hypo- or hyperthyroidism
  • Vitamin B12 and folate — deficiency
  • If at risk: syphilis serology (neurosyphilis), HIV Ag/Ab
  • Brain imaging (CT or MRI) — exclude space-occupying lesions, normal-pressure hydrocephalus; determine subtype. PET if diagnosis uncertain.
📊 Cognitive Assessment Tools (Australia)
  • MMSE / SMMSE — Moderately correlates with functional impairment in AD. PBS uses this for eligibility. Score 21–24 = mild; 10–20 = moderate; <10 = severe AD. Limited for non-AD types (does not adequately assess executive function).
  • MoCA — Community, acute, primary and aged-care. Better for executive function than MMSE.
  • ACE-III — Can distinguish between certain dementia types
  • FAB (Frontal Assessment Battery) — Distinguishes FTD from AD
  • GPCOG — Primary care; includes informant component
  • RUDAS — Culturally and linguistically diverse backgrounds
  • KiCA — Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples in rural/remote areas
⚠️
Severity is not MMSE alone. In practice: impact of functional impairment AND cognitive impairment AND BPSD AND previous cognitive function. A patient with MMSE 24 requiring institutional care due to severe BPSD = severe dementia. A patient with MMSE 13 but no functional impairment = mild dementia.

Behavioural & Psychological Symptoms of Dementia (BPSD)

💡
Over 90% of patients with dementia will experience BPSD. These symptoms significantly affect the patient and their carers and are a major precipitant of residential aged care entry. BPSD are often temporary and are usually caused by unmet needs.
🔎 Common BPSD Examples
  • Psychotic: Delusions (distressing beliefs), hallucinations
  • Agitation: Repeating questions, arguing, hoarding, pacing, disruptive vocalisations, rejection of care
  • Aggression: Physical or verbal
  • Mood: Depression, anxiety, apathy/indifference, irritability
  • Disinhibition: Socially or sexually inappropriate behaviour
  • Motor: Wandering, rummaging, repetitive purposeless activities
  • Night-time behaviours: Waking and getting up at night
⚠️ Factors That Precipitate BPSD — Address FIRST
  • Unmet needs: Pain, constipation, dehydration, fatigue, hunger, incontinence, urinary retention, loneliness, boredom, frustration, lack of glasses/hearing aids
  • Delirium — if acute onset, always exclude delirium first (infection, drugs, metabolic)
  • Drug-induced: Anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, opioids, and many others can worsen cognition and behaviour
  • Environmental factors: Overstimulation, understimulation, unsafe or uncomfortable environment, lack of routine
  • Carer stress — identify and address carer distress
  • Pre-existing psychiatric disorder
🚨
There is NO drug treatment for wandering, calling out, shadowing, exit-seeking, disrobing, or inappropriate voiding. Antipsychotics can only sedate — they do not address the cause of these behaviours. They are inappropriate for these indications.
Support resources: Dementia Behaviour Management Advisory Service (DBMAS) — 24-hour helpline 1800 699 799 | National Dementia Helpline 1800 100 500 | Specialist Dementia Care Program (SDCP) for severe BPSD

Risk Reduction

🚨
No drug, herbal or dietary supplement has been shown to prevent dementia. Some have shown higher rates of adverse effects than placebo. Do NOT prescribe drugs, herbal or dietary supplements to prevent dementia. Avoid vitamin E — associated with increased mortality.
✅ 12 Modifiable Risk Factors (Lancet Commission 2020)
  • Air pollution (including second-hand smoke)
  • Hazardous alcohol consumption
  • Depression
  • Diabetes
  • Poor education
  • Head injury
  • Hearing impairment (use hearing aids)
  • Hypertension
  • Obesity
  • Physical inactivity
  • Smoking
  • Social isolation
💊 Drugs That May Increase Dementia Risk — Deprescribe
  • Benzodiazepines — use in later life associated with ↑ dementia risk; deprescribe if possible
  • Drugs with anticholinergic properties — linked to increased dementia risk; review all anticholinergic medications (especially: tricyclic antidepressants, oxybutynin, first-generation antihistamines, older antipsychotics)
🛡️ Protective Lifestyle Practices
  • Regular physical activity
  • Remain cognitively and socially active
  • Healthy diet; maintain healthy weight
  • Practice good sleep hygiene
  • Protect head from injury (helmet use)
  • Prevent hearing loss (ear protection in noise)
  • Avoid smoking and second-hand smoke
  • Reduce alcohol intake

Goals & Principles of Management

🚨
Pharmacological therapy has a limited role. There is no cure for dementia. Drug treatments for cognitive symptoms are palliative — they can modestly improve or stabilise symptoms temporarily but do not treat the underlying disease or delay progression. Management is primarily supportive and non-pharmacological.
Primary Goals
  • Maintain quality of life
  • Maximise functional independence
  • Enhance cognition, mood, and behaviour
  • Foster a safe environment
  • Promote social engagement
  • Support families and carers
Prescribing Principles
  • Confirm valid indication for every drug
  • Use lowest effective dose; shortest duration
  • Start low; titrate slowly
  • Regularly review benefit–harm ratio
  • Avoid polypharmacy
  • Drug must align with patient's goals of care
  • Deprescribe proactively
Review & Rationalise
  • Stop drugs causing cognitive impairment (anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, psychotropics)
  • Medication aids (e.g. Webster packs) to support adherence
  • Review preventive medicines vs short life expectancy (e.g. statins need 2–5 years to benefit)
  • Address driving — most lose capacity within a few years
Planning
  • Advance care planning — before capacity is lost
  • Appoint substitute decision-maker
  • Plan for residential aged care transition
  • Carer support — educate, provide respite, connect with services
🏃

Non-Pharmacological Management

🚨
Non-pharmacological management is the primary treatment for BPSD. It is more effective than drugs for most BPSD. A personalised, multicomponent behavioural care plan is the foundation. Physical restraint is NEVER appropriate — it worsens distress and disorientation.
🧠 Cognitive & Psychological Approaches
  • CBT: Adjustment to diagnosis, forward planning, treating depression in early dementia
  • Psychotherapy / psycho-educational interventions: For carers; improves carer wellbeing and resilience
  • Behavioural management therapy: Targeting specific challenging behaviours (wandering, agitation, repetitive questioning)
  • Memory training and external memory aids: Maximise independence in early dementia
  • Dementia support groups: Networks, support services
🌿 Environmental & Activity-Based Approaches
  • Environmental modification: Non-stressful, constant, familiar environment is optimal; address overstimulation or understimulation
  • Montessori activities: Meaningful activities supporting independence through environmental cueing
  • Physical activity: Exercise, walking groups, gentle activity
  • Alternative therapies: Light massage and aromatherapy, music and dance therapy, animal-assisted therapy, multi-sensory therapy
  • Routine: Consistent daily routines reduce agitation
  • Respect cultural, religious, and social norms — behaviours must be understood in life history context

Pharmacological Treatment — Cognitive Symptoms

🚨
AChEIs and memantine: TGA-approved for Alzheimer's dementia and LBD only. Do NOT use for frontotemporal dementia — no evidence and may cause harm. Seek expert advice for vascular and other subtypes. These drugs are not curative and do not slow disease progression — they can modestly improve or stabilise symptoms temporarily in some patients.

Pharmacological Treatment — Agitation, Aggression & Psychosis (BPSD)

🚨
Only consider pharmacotherapy for BPSD if ALL of the following are met: (1) Non-pharmacological management has been adequately tried and has NOT alleviated symptoms, AND (2) The patient is distressed OR considered a threat to themselves or others. Use lowest effective dose for shortest time. Combine with — not instead of — non-pharmacological management. Seek specialist advice before starting if possible.
🚨
LBD — Critical Drug Safety: Typical antipsychotics (e.g. haloperidol) are CONTRAINDICATED in LBD — they cause severe sensitivity reactions, paradoxically worsen psychosis, agitation, and motor symptoms, and ↑ mortality. Benzodiazepines may also cause paradoxical sedation and agitation in LBD — avoid. These warnings apply throughout all BPSD management in LBD.

❌ Avoid benzodiazepines for BPSD — limited evidence; associated with cognitive decline, urinary incontinence, falls, hip fractures, dependence, and ↑ all-cause mortality ❌ Paradoxical sedation and agitation in LBD — avoid entirely in LBD ⚠️ If antipsychotic AND antidepressant cannot be used: short-acting benzodiazepine without active metabolites (e.g. oxazepam) may be considered for a maximum of 2 weeks only 🔵 Note: a single dose of a benzodiazepine may be used to calm a patient for an investigation (e.g. X-ray) — this is separate from regular prescribing ❌ Sodium valproate — Do NOT use for agitation/aggression/psychosis of dementia. No evidence of benefit; higher rate of serious adverse effects. Cochrane review: further research may not be justified.

Depression in Dementia

⚠️
Evidence for antidepressants in major depression with dementia is weak — Cochrane review found limited or no efficacy. Antidepressants are associated with dry mouth, dizziness, hyponatraemia, falls, and fractures. Non-pharmacological interventions and psychological therapies are the primary treatment. Avoid TCAs — significant anticholinergic effects impair cognition and ↑ delirium risk.

Modifying & Stopping Pharmacological Therapy

🚨
Stopping AChEIs or memantine can cause irreversible or rapid cognitive/functional decline and severe BPSD. This does not preclude stopping — but stopping must be done thoughtfully via shared decision-making, with a monitoring plan. Do not stop abruptly if avoidable. Before attributing deterioration to drug withdrawal, always exclude delirium and other causes.

🔄 Switching Between AChEIs (First Year)
  • Cannot tolerate one AChEI → may tolerate another: stop the problem AChEI; start new one only after adverse effect resolves
  • Does not respond to one AChEI → may respond to another: direct switch — stop original and start new one the next day
  • If switching criteria not met but switch is considered → seek specialist advice
  • Consider whether memantine is suitable alternative to a stopped AChEI
⬇️ How to Stop AChEI or Memantine Safely
  • Halve the dose every 4 weeks until lowest available dose for 4 weeks → then stop
  • If formulations don't allow halving → step down through available formulations
  • Monitor cognition, function, and BPSD at minimum every 4 weeks during reduction
  • More frequent monitoring if high dose or rapid reduction needed
  • Patient and carer should watch for deterioration between assessments
  • Adjust speed based on reason (e.g. severe adverse effect → stop immediately)
⚡ Interpreting Deterioration During Withdrawal
  • <1 week after dose reduction: Severe worsening (agitation, aggression, hallucinations, ↓ consciousness) → immediately resume previous minimum effective dose (likely withdrawal reaction)
  • 1–6 weeks after reduction: Worsening likely = symptom re-emergence → consider resuming previous minimum effective dose
  • >6 weeks, <3 months: May be symptom re-emergence OR disease progression → clinical judgement required
  • >3 months: Worsening usually = natural dementia progression
  • Always exclude delirium before attributing deterioration to drug withdrawal
📋 Deprescribing Antipsychotics (Long-term ≥12 weeks)
  • Most patients can stop antipsychotic long-term without BPSD worsening
  • Patients not severely symptomatic initially may actually have less agitation when antipsychotic stopped
  • Reduction schedule: Reduce by 25–50% every 1–2 weeks → stop at lowest practical dose after 1–2 weeks
  • Slower reduction for high initial dose or initially severe symptoms
  • Assess target symptoms and withdrawal symptoms every 1–2 weeks during reduction
  • If significant worsening or severe withdrawal → restart at minimum effective dose; re-trial deprescribing after 3 months
  • Optimise non-pharmacological management during and after deprescribing (critical if symptoms re-emerge)

Drug Summaries

DonepezilAcetylcholinesterase Inhibitor — AD & LBD
Doses (Adult)
  • 5 mg PO at night for minimum 4 weeks → increase to 10 mg at night if tolerated
  • 10 mg marginally more effective but higher adverse effect risk — titrate based on tolerability
  • If insomnia or vivid dreams → consider morning dosing instead
  • Once-daily dosing facilitates adherence
Mechanism
Selectively inhibits acetylcholinesterase in the brain → ↓ breakdown of acetylcholine → ↑ cholinergic neurotransmission → partially compensates for the cholinergic deficit in AD and LBD. Does not treat the underlying disease or slow progression.
Indications
  • Alzheimer's disease (all severity — mild to severe)
  • Dementia with Lewy bodies (including for BPSD — drug of choice)
Evidence
  • Deterioration of cognition delayed by 6 months in 25–50% of patients with mild-to-moderate AD; by 1 year in 12–20% of patients
  • No reliable predictors of individual response — assess after 3 months at max tolerated dose
  • AChEIs do NOT reduce risk of developing dementia in mild cognitive impairment
Precautions & Contraindications
  • ECG mandatory before starting — contraindicated in second- or third-degree heart block, sick sinus syndrome without pacemaker
  • Active peptic ulcer or GI/ureteric obstruction — contraindicated
  • History of peptic ulcer, seizures, bradyarrhythmias, Parkinson's disease, asthma, obstructive pulmonary disease — risk of aggravation
  • Anticholinergic drugs — antagonise therapeutic effect; avoid combination; review all anticholinergics before starting
  • QT risk factors — QT prolongation and torsades de pointes reported; avoid if uncorrectable QT risk factors; use caution with other QT-prolonging drugs
  • Drugs causing bradycardia (e.g. beta-blockers, amiodarone, digoxin) — ↑ risk of bradycardia and hypotension; use with caution; monitor heart rate
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding — avoid
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%) — class effects
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, anorexia, abdominal pain, dyspepsia — most common; dose-related
  • Weight loss — monitor weight regularly
  • Insomnia, vivid dreams — consider morning dosing
  • Headache, fatigue, drowsiness, dizziness
  • Muscle cramps, tremor, urinary incontinence, increased sweating
  • Hypertension, fainting — falls risk
Infrequent/Rare
  • Bradycardia, heart block — perform ECG; monitor HR
  • Seizure, GI haemorrhage (peptic ulcer exacerbation)
  • Agitation, hallucination, confusion (can paradoxically worsen)
  • Prolonged QT interval, torsades de pointes
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (rare)
  • Increased libido, inappropriate sexual behaviour (most published cases with donepezil — inform carers)
Counselling
  • Take at night; if insomnia or vivid dreams occur, speak to your doctor about switching to morning dosing.
  • May cause dizziness or drowsiness — avoid operating machinery until you know how it affects you.
  • If GI adverse effects occur (nausea, diarrhoea), inform your doctor — omitting 1–2 doses and temporarily reducing the dose can help; adverse effects are dose-related and often improve with time.
  • If treatment is interrupted for several days → restart at the lowest dose (5 mg) — do not restart at the previous higher dose, as this markedly increases the risk of severe vomiting.
  • Important for carers: Donepezil can rarely cause increased libido or inappropriate sexual behaviour — inform carers so this can be reported promptly and managed.
  • This medicine does not cure or reverse dementia — it may temporarily slow decline or improve symptoms in some people. The decision to continue will be reviewed regularly.
Galantamine Modified-ReleaseAcetylcholinesterase Inhibitor — AD Only
Doses (Adult)
  • 8 mg PO mane (modified-release) for minimum 4 weeks
  • → 16 mg mane for minimum 4 weeks (if tolerated)
  • → May increase to 24 mg mane if individually needed (no group-data evidence of benefit over 16 mg)
  • Take with food
  • Moderate hepatic impairment: 8 mg once every other morning for 1 week, then 8 mg mane for ≥4 weeks; max 16 mg/day
Indications
  • Mild-to-moderate Alzheimer's disease
  • NOT approved for LBD
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Severe hepatic impairment — contraindicated. Moderate hepatic impairment — reduce dose (see above).
  • CrCl <10 mL/min — contraindicated by manufacturer
  • All class precautions apply (ECG, peptic ulcer, bradyarrhythmia, etc. — see Donepezil)
Adverse Effects
Common (class — see Donepezil)
  • GI adverse effects, weight loss, insomnia, dizziness, etc.
Galantamine-specific
  • Hypersensitivity reactions
  • Serious skin reactions (SJS, AGEP, erythema multiforme) — rare
Evidence Note
  • Two placebo-controlled studies in patients with mild cognitive impairment (not dementia) found galantamine was ineffective and associated with increased mortality — NOT for mild cognitive impairment
Counselling
  • Take with food each morning — reduces nausea and stomach upset significantly.
  • If treatment is interrupted for several days → restart at the lowest dose (8 mg) — do NOT restart at the previous higher dose, as this increases the risk of severe vomiting.
  • Report any skin rash immediately — rare but serious skin reactions have been reported.
  • This medicine does not cure dementia — it may help slow decline or improve symptoms in some people. The decision to continue will be regularly reviewed with the treating team.
  • Important: This medicine is NOT appropriate for mild cognitive impairment before dementia has developed — evidence shows no benefit and possible harm in that population.
Rivastigmine — Patch & OralAcetylcholinesterase Inhibitor — AD & LBD
Doses (Adult)
  • Transdermal patch (preferred): 4.6 mg/24h patch × 4 weeks → 9.5 mg/24h × ≥4 weeks → 13.3 mg/24h if needed/tolerated
  • Oral (less preferred — ↑ GI adverse effects): 1.5 mg PO BD → ↑ by 3 mg/day every 2 weeks → max 6 mg BD. Take with morning and evening meals.
  • Converting from oral to patch: Use patch of closest strength to established oral daily dose; start patch the day after the last oral dose
Indications
  • Alzheimer's disease
  • Dementia with Lewy bodies — drug of choice for cognitive symptoms and BPSD
  • Parkinson's disease dementia
Patch vs Oral
  • Patch significantly better tolerated — less nausea and vomiting vs oral
  • Patch: once-daily; easier titration; suitable for patients intolerant of oral therapy
  • Patch: rotate application sites meticulously — back, upper arm, or chest; avoid same site within 2 weeks
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Severe hepatic impairment — contraindicated (lack of data)
  • All class precautions apply (ECG, peptic ulcer, bradyarrhythmia, anticholinergics, QT — see Donepezil)
Adverse Effects
Common (class — see Donepezil)
  • GI adverse effects (less with patch), weight loss, insomnia, vivid dreams, dizziness, tremor, urinary incontinence, sweating
Patch-specific
  • Application site skin reactions — erythema, rash, irritation; rotate sites meticulously
  • Allergic contact dermatitis — rare; skin hypersensitivity
Counselling (Patch)
  • Apply to clean, dry, hairless, non-irritated skin on the back, upper arm, or chest.
  • When changing the patch: gently remove the old one and apply a new one to a DIFFERENT area — do not re-use the same area within 2 weeks. Recording the rotation schedule can help.
  • Fold used patch in half (sticky side inward), place in original sachet, and dispose safely — out of reach of children (residual drug can cause toxicity).
  • If treatment is interrupted for several days → restart at the lowest patch strength (4.6 mg/24h) — do NOT restart at the previous higher strength.
  • External heat (electric blankets, heat pads, hot baths, saunas) can increase absorption of the patch — avoid during wear; monitor for increased adverse effects if a fever develops.
  • Write the date and time of application on the patch with permanent marker each time you apply it — this helps track when to change it.
MemantineNMDA Antagonist — Moderate-to-Severe AD
Doses (Adult)
  • 5 mg PO mane → ↑ by 5 mg each week (if tolerated) → maintenance 20 mg once daily
  • GFR 5–29 mL/min: maximum daily dose 10 mg
  • GFR <5 mL/min: avoid
  • Must be started by a specialist
Mechanism
Antagonises N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) glutamate receptors → reduces excessive glutamate-mediated neuronal stimulation (excitotoxicity) → thought to slow neuronal degradation in moderate-severe AD. Glutamate excess is associated with AD progression.
Indications
  • Moderate-to-severe Alzheimer's disease (MMSE ≤20)
  • Monotherapy if AChEI cannot be taken; OR add to AChEI if inadequate response
  • NOT for mild AD; NOT for FTD; equivocal evidence in LBD
Precautions & Contraindications
  • History of seizures — contraindicated
  • Renal impairment — dose reduction required (GFR 5–29: max 10 mg/day); avoid if GFR <5
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Confusion, dizziness, drowsiness, headache
  • Insomnia, agitation, hallucinations (can paradoxically worsen)
  • Dyspnoea, hypersensitivity
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Vomiting, anxiety, hypertonia, VTE
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Seizures, renal failure, cholestatic hepatitis, heart failure, bradycardia
Counselling
  • May make you feel drowsy or dizzy — avoid operating machinery; take particular care on standing.
  • Report significant new confusion, hallucinations, or any seizure-like episodes immediately — these are important side effects that require prompt review.
  • Regular review of behavioural and functional status is important — treatment continues only if these stabilise or improve. Do not continue indefinitely without review.
  • This medicine must be started under specialist guidance — it is not appropriate for mild memory problems, only for moderate-to-severe Alzheimer's disease.
  • Renal function matters: If kidney function changes, the dose of this medicine may need adjustment — inform your doctor of any kidney-related changes.
RisperidoneAtypical Antipsychotic — PBS-approved BPSD (AD only, ≤12 weeks)
🚨
Use of antipsychotics in older people with dementia is associated with ↑ risk of stroke and death. Risk appears greatest early in treatment and with higher doses. Risk is higher in vascular or mixed dementia. Only use after non-pharmacological interventions have failed; psychosis or aggression in AD only; maximum 12 weeks.
Doses (BPSD in Moderate-Severe AD)
  • 0.25 mg PO BD → ↑ by 0.25 mg BD every ≥2 days
  • Usual effective dose: 1 mg/day (range 1–2 mg/day) in 1 or 2 doses
  • Maximum: 2 mg/day
  • Elderly, renal, or hepatic impairment: start 0.5 mg once or twice daily; increase by 0.5 mg every second day; max 4 mg/day (for schizophrenia) but for BPSD: max 2 mg/day
  • Treat for no more than 12 weeks
PBS-Approved Indication for Dementia
  • Behavioural symptoms characterised by psychosis AND/OR aggression in moderate-severe AD
  • Only AFTER non-pharmacological interventions have failed
  • Maximum 12-week treatment period under PBS restrictions
  • Risperidone is the ONLY antipsychotic PBS-approved for BPSD
Precautions & Contraindications
  • LBD — do NOT use (risk of severe sensitivity reactions, paradoxical worsening, ↑ mortality)
  • Allergy to risperidone or paliperidone — contraindicated
  • High stroke risk (vascular disease, AF, prior stroke) — carefully assess benefit–harm; seek expert advice; avoid if possible
  • Elderly with dementia — significantly ↑ cerebrovascular adverse events vs placebo; risk greater in vascular/mixed dementia than AD alone
  • Renal impairment — dose reduction; avoid once-monthly injection if CrCl <60 mL/min
  • Hepatic impairment — use half usual starting and incremental doses
  • Parkinson's disease — antipsychotics may worsen; low-dose quetiapine or clozapine preferred if needed
  • Prolactinoma — ↑ prolactin levels (risperidone has most prolactin effect of atypicals)
  • Diabetes — ↑ blood glucose; monitor
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%) — class + drug specific
  • Sedation, anxiety, agitation, EPSE (especially at higher doses)
  • Orthostatic hypotension — ↑ falls; get up gradually
  • Weight gain, constipation, nausea, dry mouth, urinary retention
  • Hyperprolactinaemia (most of atypicals) — galactorrhoea, gynaecomastia
  • Insomnia, headache, akathisia
Rare / Serious
  • Stroke, TIA — especially vascular/mixed dementia; ↑ risk with antipsychotics in elderly
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome — fever, rigidity, autonomic instability
  • Sudden death — ↑ mortality in elderly with dementia on antipsychotics
  • Tardive dyskinesia — involuntary orofacial/limb movements with long-term use
  • QT prolongation (intermediate risk vs other antipsychotics)
Monitoring
  • Review target behaviour weekly for first 2 weeks. Stop if no benefit within 2 weeks.
  • If benefit → continue and review every 4–6 weeks. Monitor for adverse effects.
  • Do NOT continue beyond 12 weeks routinely — BPSD are often temporary.
  • Baseline and regular: weight, BMI, waist, blood glucose, lipids, BP, ECG (if indicated).
Counselling
  • This medicine is being used for a limited period (up to 12 weeks) to manage a specific behaviour — it is not for long-term use in dementia. Its use will be regularly reviewed and stopped once behaviour settles.
  • May cause dizziness on standing — get up slowly from sitting or lying; sit or lie down if you feel dizzy.
  • Report any uncontrolled movements of the face, mouth, tongue, or limbs immediately — these can be a sign of a serious movement-related side effect (tardive dyskinesia).
  • Do not stop suddenly without advice — dose should be reduced gradually after longer use.
  • Risk awareness (for family/carer): This medicine carries a small increased risk of stroke and death in older people with dementia. The treating team has assessed that the benefit outweighs this risk for this patient. Report any sudden weakness, speech difficulty, or facial drooping immediately (emergency signs).
  • Avoid alcohol — increases sedation.
OlanzapineAtypical Antipsychotic — BPSD (off-label; NOT PBS for dementia)
Doses (Elderly — BPSD)
  • 2.5 mg PO daily → ↑ by 2.5 mg every ≥7 days
  • Maximum for BPSD: 10 mg daily
  • Start at 5 mg for general adult if orthostatic hypotension risk (5 mg for elderly)
  • EPSE and hypotension relatively uncommon at lower doses in elderly
Specific Cautions vs Risperidone
  • More metabolic effects than risperidone (hyperglycaemia, weight gain, dyslipidaemia) — monitor glucose and weight more closely
  • Smoking ↑ olanzapine clearance — dose may need adjustment if patient starts or stops smoking
  • More anticholinergic than risperidone — ↑ constipation, dry mouth, urinary retention risk
Precautions
  • LBD — severe sensitivity risk; avoid
  • ↑ Stroke and death in elderly with dementia — same class warning as risperidone
  • Diabetes — ↑ blood glucose, type 2 diabetes risk; monitor blood glucose regularly
  • Hepatic impairment — start at 5 mg; use conservative doses
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Sedation, orthostatic hypotension, weight gain, hyperglycaemia, constipation, dry mouth
Infrequent/Rare
  • Stroke, death, NMS, tardive dyskinesia, hepatic failure, rhabdomyolysis, VTE
Counselling
  • This medicine is for short-term use (up to 12 weeks) to manage specific behaviours — its use will be regularly reviewed.
  • Smoking: Tell your doctor if you start or stop smoking — smoking significantly affects how this medicine works and the dose may need to change.
  • May cause dizziness on standing — get up slowly; avoid falls.
  • Blood glucose and weight should be checked regularly — this medicine can raise blood sugar levels, particularly in patients with diabetes or risk factors for diabetes.
  • Swallow tablets whole — do not crush or chew.

QuetiapineAtypical Antipsychotic — Drug of Choice if Antipsychotic Required in LBD
Doses (LBD BPSD or Elderly Dementia — if antipsychotic required)
  • 12.5–25 mg PO immediate-release once or twice daily
  • Increase by 12.5–25 mg every ≥2 days as tolerated
  • Maximum: 75 mg twice daily
  • Conservative starting dose and very slow titration in LBD
  • Use immediate-release (not controlled-release) for BPSD in dementia
Why Quetiapine in LBD
  • Lower EPSE (extrapyramidal side effect) potential than other antipsychotics
  • May be less likely to cause the severe sensitivity reactions seen with typical antipsychotics and other atypicals in LBD
  • Still carries risk — use lowest possible dose for shortest possible time
  • NOT for routine use — only if rivastigmine/donepezil inadequate for BPSD
Precautions
  • LBD — extreme caution even with quetiapine; lowest dose; shortest time; monitor very closely
  • Hepatic impairment — use conservative doses
  • Diabetes — ↑ blood glucose (clozapine/olanzapine/quetiapine most associated with metabolic effects)
  • Controlled-release tablets: swallow whole, without food — NOT for BPSD management
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Somnolence, tachycardia, orthostatic hypotension, weight gain, dry mouth
Infrequent/Rare
  • Stroke, death, NMS, QT prolongation, cardiomyopathy, hepatic failure
  • Hypothyroidism (monitor thyroid function if history of thyroid disease)
Counselling
  • This medicine is being used for short-term management of specific behaviours — regular review will occur and it will be stopped once symptoms settle.
  • May cause significant drowsiness and dizziness on standing — take particular care with falls; get up slowly.
  • Controlled-release tablets (if used for other indications): swallow whole without food — do NOT cut, crush, or chew. For dementia BPSD, use immediate-release formulation only.
  • Avoid alcohol — increases sedation substantially.
  • Important for carers (LBD patients): Report any worsening of confusion, hallucinations, or movement difficulties immediately — this medicine can occasionally worsen these symptoms in LBD even at low doses.

SSRIs — Citalopram, Escitalopram, SertralineAntidepressant — Depression in Dementia & Second-line BPSD
Doses (Adult — Dementia Context)
  • Citalopram (BPSD — strongest evidence; and depression):Adult: 20 mg mane; max 40 mg/day. Elderly >65 years: start 10 mg mane; max 20 mg/day. Hepatic impairment / ↓ CYP2C19: 10 mg mane; max 20 mg/day.
  • Escitalopram (depression):Adult: 10 mg mane; max 20 mg/day. Elderly or hepatic impairment: 5 mg mane; max 10 mg/day.
  • Sertraline (depression):Adult: 50 mg mane; max 200 mg/day. Hepatic impairment: lower initial dose; titrate slowly.
Mechanism
Selectively inhibit presynaptic reuptake of serotonin → ↑ serotonergic neurotransmission. Relevant to dementia via effects on mood, agitation, and neuropsychiatric symptoms.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • MAOIs — contraindicated (serotonin toxicity); wait ≥14 days after stopping MAOI; wait 2–4 days after stopping SSRI (14 days for fluoxetine) before starting MAOI
  • Citalopram/escitalopram — QT prolongation risk at higher doses; contraindicated if uncorrectable QT risk factors; citalopram elderly max 20 mg/day; escitalopram elderly max 10 mg/day
  • Escitalopram — allergy to escitalopram or citalopram: contraindicated
  • High bleeding risk (age >80, prior GI bleed, NSAIDs/aspirin) — SSRIs ↓ platelet aggregation; ↑ haemorrhage risk; monitor or avoid
  • Hyponatraemia risk — check sodium at baseline and early in treatment (especially in elderly)
  • Hepatic impairment — reduce dose for all SSRIs
  • Bipolar disorder — all antidepressants may precipitate mania
  • Serotonin toxicity risk with other serotonergic drugs — monitor carefully
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Nausea, diarrhoea, dry mouth, insomnia, drowsiness, dizziness, headache, sweating
  • Falls risk — orthostatic hypotension, dizziness
  • Hyponatraemia (SIADH) — especially in elderly; usually early in treatment
  • Sexual dysfunction, weakness, anxiety, agitation
Infrequent
  • Abnormal platelet aggregation, bleeding (GI, intracranial)
  • QT prolongation (citalopram/escitalopram at higher doses)
Rare
  • Hepatic failure, blood dyscrasias, angle-closure glaucoma (especially paroxetine)
Monitoring
  • Check sodium at baseline and early in treatment (especially elderly)
  • Formal falls risk assessment before starting
  • ECG at baseline if citalopram or escitalopram at higher doses (QT risk)
  • Review for suicidal ideation early in treatment (especially young people)
Counselling
  • Take in the morning — SSRIs may cause insomnia if taken at night.
  • Do not stop suddenly — always taper gradually as directed to avoid discontinuation symptoms (dizziness, nausea, "electric shock" sensations).
  • May cause dizziness on standing — get up slowly; sit or lie if dizzy; ↑ falls risk especially in elderly.
  • Report any unusual bleeding (bruising, blood in stool, urine), confusion, or signs of low sodium (headache, weakness, nausea) — particularly important in elderly.
  • Avoid other medicines (especially herbal products including St John's Wort) without checking with a pharmacist — serious serotonin toxicity interactions possible.
  • Citalopram/Escitalopram (important dose safety): Do not exceed the dose limits — citalopram 40 mg/day (20 mg/day if over 65); escitalopram 20 mg/day (10 mg/day if over 65). Higher doses significantly increase the risk of dangerous heart rhythm problems.
  • It may take 2–4 weeks to notice any improvement — be patient and continue taking as prescribed. Effectiveness will be formally reviewed at around 2–4 months.

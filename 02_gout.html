Gout

Inflammatory arthritis caused by monosodium urate crystal deposition. The most common cause of inflammatory arthritis globally. Requires TWO separate treatment approaches: acute flare management and long-term urate lowering.

🔬

Pathophysiology

🔑
Key concept: Gout develops in 4 stages — hyperuricaemia → crystal deposition → acute inflammatory response → chronic disease (tophi, joint damage).
Urate overproduction OR underexcretion → Hyperuricaemia (♂ >0.42 mmol/L; ♀ >0.36 mmol/L)
• Overproduction: high cell turnover (psoriasis, haematological malignancy, chemo/radiation)
• Underexcretion (most common): high insulin (obesity/metabolic syndrome) → ↓ renal urate excretion; diuretics; low-dose aspirin; ciclosporin; CKD; dehydration; catabolic states (sepsis)
Monosodium urate (MSU) crystal formation and deposition in joints and soft tissues when urate supersaturates synovial fluid (especially in peripheral joints — cooler temperature favours crystallisation)
Crystal-triggered immune activation: MSU crystals activate TLR2/TLR4 on macrophages/monocytes → inflammasome activation → ↑ IL-1β → endothelial activation → neutrophil recruitment → intense neutrophil-driven inflammation → acute gout flare (severe pain, heat, redness, swelling)
Flare resolution: Neutrophils form neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs) that bind MSU crystals → degrades pro-inflammatory signals → flare resolves spontaneously (usually 1–2 weeks). Crystal deposits remain despite flare resolution.
Chronic gout: Continued crystal build-up → persistent inflammation → tophus formation (urate crystal deposits surrounded by immune cells) → bone erosion, cartilage damage, chronic pain, structural joint damage, urate nephropathy, kidney stones
⚠️
Important: Hyperuricaemia alone ≠ gout. Many people with raised urate remain asymptomatic. Serum urate may be normal during an acute flare — do not use to rule out gout during active attack.
⚠️

Risk Factors

🔒 Non-Modifiable
  • Male sex or post-menopausal female
  • Family history
  • Increasing age
  • Aboriginal / Torres Strait Islander status
✏️ Modifiable / Acquired
  • High purine intake: red meat, organ meats, seafood
  • Alcohol (especially beer/spirits)
  • Fructose-sweetened beverages
  • Dehydration
  • Obesity / high BMI
  • Smoking
  • Catabolic state (e.g. sepsis, post-hospitalisation)
💊 Drugs (Reduce Urate Excretion)
  • Thiazide diuretics
  • Loop diuretics
  • Low-dose aspirin (<150 mg)
  • Ciclosporin
🏥 Associated Conditions
  • Hypertension
  • CKD (urate excretion ↓)
  • Dyslipidaemia
  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Obesity / metabolic syndrome
  • Overall ↑ CVD risk
  • High cell turnover: psoriasis, haematological malignancies, chemotherapy
  • Autoimmune conditions
🩺

Clinical Features

⚡ Acute Gout Flare
  • Sudden onset, often at night or early morning
  • Severe pain — most intense in first 1–2 hours
  • Swelling, redness (erythema), heat, tenderness
  • Usually monoarticular (can be polyarticular in women)
  • Attack often self-limiting, resolves in 1–2 weeks
  • May present with fever, malaise, raised inflammatory markers
  • Serum urate may be normal during acute flare
🗺️ Joints Typically Affected
  • 1st metatarsophalangeal joint (big toe — podagra) — most classic
  • Foot, ankle, knee
  • Wrist, elbow, fingers (less common)
  • Initial attacks typically lower extremity
📅 Chronic / Recurrent Gout
  • More frequent attacks, shorter inter-attack interval
  • Often polyarticular
  • Tophi (soft tissue deposits — often ears, elbows, fingers, Achilles)
  • Chronic low-grade joint inflammation
  • Joint deformity
  • Bone erosion (distinctive "overhanging edge" on X-ray)
🔴 Complications
  • Tophi with risk of ulceration and infection
  • Destructive arthritis
  • Kidney stones (urate nephrolithiasis)
  • Urate nephropathy
  • CKD progression
🔍 Differentiating Gout from Other Arthritides
  • vs OA: Gout is episodic/acute; OA is gradual/chronic. Gout → severe redness/heat typical; OA less so. Confirm: polarised microscopy showing negatively birefringent MSU crystals.
  • vs Septic arthritis: Both can have monoarticular hot red joint. Gout — crystals on microscopy; septic arthritis — bacteria on culture. ALWAYS send synovial fluid for microscopy AND culture (septic arthritis is an emergency — do NOT miss).
  • vs Pseudogout (CPPD): Pseudogout — positively birefringent calcium pyrophosphate crystals; usually affects knee/wrist in older patients.
  • vs RA: RA — symmetric polyarthritis; morning stiffness >1hr; positive RF/anti-CCP; different joint distribution; RA doesn't usually affect 1st MTP.
🔍

Diagnosis & Investigations

🥇
Gold standard: Joint aspiration → Identification of monosodium urate crystals (needle-shaped, negatively birefringent) under polarised microscopy in synovial/bursal fluid or tophus material. Diagnostic certainty is important — gout often requires lifelong ULT therapy.
If aspiration not feasible, clinical diagnosis supported by:
  • Monoarticular foot/ankle involvement, especially 1st MTP (podagra)
  • Rapid onset severe pain/swelling peaking within 24 hours
  • Prior similar acute episodes (previous gout attacks)
  • Erythema overlying joint
  • Tophi visible
  • Strong family history of gout
  • CVD + hyperuricaemia in males or post-menopausal females
Key Investigations:

Synovial fluid microscopy & cultureDefinitive diagnosis (crystals); exclude septic arthritis
Serum urateSupport hyperuricaemia — but may be NORMAL during acute flare (do not use to exclude gout acutely). Useful for baseline and monitoring ULT

Treatment Goals

⚡ Acute Gout Goals
  • Rapid symptom relief
  • Suppress acute inflammation
  • Restore function
🔄 Chronic / ULT Goals
  • Lower serum urate to dissolve MSU crystals
  • Prevent recurrent flares
  • Resolve tophi
  • Prevent structural joint damage and kidney complications
Urate Targets (treat-to-target approach):
Patient GroupInitial TargetLong-term Target
Tophi, chronic arthropathy, or frequent attacks<0.30 mmol/L (until total crystal dissolution)<0.36 mmol/L
Non-tophaceous gout<0.36 mmol/L
💡
Non-pharmacological: Maintain healthy weight; regular exercise; adequate hydration; limit beer/spirits; reduce fructose-sweetened drinks; moderate purine-rich foods (red meat, seafood); smoking cessation. Do NOT stop low-dose aspirin prescribed for CVD. Avoid analgesic-dose aspirin (>150 mg) during acute flare — can shift urate and worsen/prolong flare.
⚡

Acute Gout: Pharmacological Treatment

🚨
Critical rule: Do NOT stop or adjust urate-lowering therapy during an acute attack — sudden urate level changes can worsen or prolong the flare.
💡
Evidence shows NSAIDs = oral corticosteroids for efficacy in acute gout. Choice is driven by comorbidities, contraindications, timing, and prior response. All three options (NSAIDs, corticosteroids, colchicine) are considered first-line — select based on patient-specific factors.

Chronic Gout: Urate-Lowering Therapy (ULT)

⚠️
Critical principle: Starting or increasing ULT commonly triggers gout flares (crystal mobilisation during urate lowering). Always add flare prophylaxis when initiating or increasing ULT.
💡
ULT indication is stronger for patients with: ≥2 flares/year; subcutaneous tophi; radiographic damage; concurrent significant CKD or kidney stones; first attack with very high serum urate.

Drug Summaries

Prednisolone / Prednisone Systemic Corticosteroid
Dose (Gout)
  • Acute flare: 15–30 mg PO daily until symptoms subside (usually 3–5 days)
  • Prophylaxis (second-line): 5 mg PO daily until symptoms subside
  • Intra-articular injection: up to 2 affected sites (gout flare)
Mechanism of Action
Regulates gene expression → glucocorticoid effects (suppresses inflammation, immune responses, gluconeogenesis) + mineralocorticoid effects (Na/water retention, K+ loss). In gout: rapidly suppresses intense crystal-triggered neutrophilic inflammation.
Indications
  • Acute gout (when NSAIDs contraindicated/inappropriate)
  • Flare prophylaxis (second-line, low dose)
  • RA — bridging therapy
  • OA — intra-articular injection
  • IBD — induction of remission
  • Various autoimmune and inflammatory conditions
Precautions & Contraindications
No absolute systemic contraindications — benefits must outweigh risks. Use cautiously in:
  • Diabetes — hyperglycaemia (check BGL regularly)
  • Active infection — may mask/worsen (check for TB)
  • PUD — ↑ GI risk (add PPI if high risk)
  • Hypertension, heart failure — fluid/Na retention
  • Psychiatric illness — mood effects
  • Osteoporosis — ↑ fracture risk with prolonged use
  • Glaucoma, myasthenia gravis, phaeochromocytoma
  • Pregnancy — use lowest effective dose
  • Intra-articular: contraindicated in infective arthritis
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Fluid retention, oedema
  • Hyperglycaemia, hypertension, hypokalaemia
  • Dyspepsia, weight gain
  • Mood effects (euphoria, anxiety, insomnia)
  • Infection risk ↑
  • Adrenal suppression (with prolonged use)
  • Osteoporosis (with prolonged use)
Rare
  • Peptic ulceration (especially with NSAIDs)
  • Tendon rupture
  • Severe psychiatric effects (psychosis)
  • Injection-related: post-injection flare, skin atrophy, hypopigmentation
Monitoring
  • BGL (especially diabetics — monitor for up to 7 days after intra-articular)
  • BP, fluid retention
  • Signs of infection
  • Mood changes
  • Consider BMD if prolonged or repeated courses (>3 months) — assess osteoporosis risk
  • Review need for taper if prolonged treatment (avoid abrupt cessation → adrenal crisis risk)
Counselling
  • Take with food to reduce stomach upset.
  • Do not stop suddenly unless told by doctor — may need gradual dose reduction.
  • Tell doctor immediately if signs of infection develop (fever, chills, worsening symptoms).
  • This medicine may affect mood and sleep.
  • Tell all treating health professionals (including surgeons, dentists) you are taking corticosteroids.
NSAIDs (Oral) — Gout Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs
Doses (Gout — Acute Flare & Prophylaxis)
  • Celecoxib: 100–200 mg PO daily or BD until symptoms subside
  • Etoricoxib: 30–60 mg PO daily until symptoms subside
  • Ibuprofen IR: 200–400 mg PO 3–4×/day until symptoms subside
  • Indometacin: 25–50 mg PO 2–4×/day until symptoms subside
  • Ketoprofen MR: 200 mg PO daily until symptoms subside
  • Meloxicam: 7.5–15 mg PO daily until symptoms subside
  • Naproxen IR: 250–500 mg PO BD until symptoms subside
  • Naproxen MR: 750–1000 mg PO daily until symptoms subside
  • Piroxicam: 10–20 mg PO daily until symptoms subside
  • Diclofenac: 25–50 mg PO 2–3×/day until symptoms subside
⚠️
Gout-specific notes: All NSAIDs are equally effective for acute gout. Do NOT combine with oral corticosteroids (↑ GI bleed risk, no additional benefit). Use minimal dose and duration for prophylaxis.
Mechanism of Action
Inhibit cyclo-oxygenase (COX) enzymes → ↓ prostaglandin synthesis → ↓ inflammation, pain, and fever. COX-2 selective agents (celecoxib, etoricoxib, meloxicam) have lower GI adverse effects but similar renal and cardiovascular risks.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Peptic ulcer disease / GI bleeding history — contraindicated or use with PPI cover
  • Significant renal impairment (eGFR <30) — avoid
  • Severe heart failure — avoid
  • Allergy to aspirin or NSAIDs — contraindicated
  • Cardiovascular disease — ↑ risk of MI, stroke; use lowest dose for shortest duration
  • Hypertension — NSAIDs cause fluid retention and raise BP; monitor
  • Elderly — ↑ all risks; use lowest effective dose for shortest time
  • Anticoagulants (warfarin, DOACs) — ↑ bleeding risk; avoid or monitor closely
  • Renal impairment, dehydration, diuretic use, ACEi/ARB — Triple Whammy → AKI risk
  • Asthma — NSAID-sensitive asthma in ~10%; use with caution
  • Pregnancy — avoid in third trimester
Adverse Effects
Common
  • GI: nausea, dyspepsia, abdominal discomfort, diarrhoea (↓ with food or COX-2 selective agents)
  • Fluid retention, peripheral oedema, raised BP
  • ↑ Serum creatinine / renal impairment (dose-related)
Serious / Rare
  • GI ulceration, perforation, bleeding (↓ risk with COX-2 selective or PPI co-prescription)
  • Acute kidney injury (especially in elderly, CKD, heart failure, dehydrated patients)
  • Cardiovascular events (MI, stroke) — especially prolonged use
  • Bronchospasm (aspirin-sensitive asthma)
  • Hypersensitivity reactions, rash
Monitoring
  • Renal function, blood pressure, fluid status — especially in elderly, CKD, or heart failure
  • Signs of GI bleeding (black/tarry stools, haematemesis)
  • Avoid prolonged use without reassessment
Counselling
  • Take with food or milk to reduce stomach upset.
  • Use for the shortest time needed at the lowest effective dose.
  • Report immediately: black or tarry stools, vomiting blood, severe stomach pain.
  • Report: ankle swelling, sudden weight gain, reduced urine output, breathlessness.
  • Avoid combining with aspirin, anticoagulants, or other NSAIDs unless directed.
  • If you also take a diuretic and an ACE inhibitor or ARB — be cautious about using NSAIDs (Triple Whammy → kidney injury risk).
Colchicine Anti-inflammatory Agent (Tubulin Inhibitor)
Doses (Gout)
  • ACUTE FLARE1 mg PO initially → 0.5 mg 1 hour later(total 1.5 mg; one-day course ONLY)Do NOT repeat within 3 daysDo NOT exceed 1.5 mg per acute courseMust be started within 24h of flare onset
  • PROPHYLAXIS (with ULT)500 micrograms PO daily for ≥6 months(Reduce dose in renal impairment or diarrhoea)
Mechanism of Action
Reduces inflammation by inhibiting neutrophil migration, chemotaxis, adhesion, and phagocytosis. In gout: ↓ the crystal-triggered inflammatory response driven by neutrophils in the joint.
Indications
  • Acute gout (within 24h of onset; prior successful use)
  • Flare prophylaxis during ULT initiation (first-line)
  • Familial Mediterranean fever
  • Acute or recurrent pericarditis
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Contraindicated: blood dyscrasias
  • Do NOT use for acute flare if CrCl <80 mL/min AND already on prophylactic colchicine
  • Renal impairment: ↓ elimination → ↑ toxicity; increase time between courses if CrCl <30 mL/min
  • Hepatic impairment: ↓ elimination → dose reduce; avoid acute dosing on top of prophylactic dose if severe
  • Severe GI disease
  • Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (clarithromycin, ketoconazole, ritonavir) → ↑ colchicine plasma levels → toxicity
  • Strong P-gp inhibitors → ↑ colchicine levels
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Diarrhoea, nausea, abdominal discomfort, vomiting (dose-dependent)
  • Pharyngolaryngeal pain
Infrequent
  • GI haemorrhage
  • Rash
Rare / Life-threatening
  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Myopathy, rhabdomyolysis
  • Myelosuppression: agranulocytosis, thrombocytopenia, leucopenia
  • Arrhythmias, respiratory failure (colchicine toxicity)
  • Hepatitis
  • Angioedema
Monitoring
  • Long-term prophylactic use: FBC at baseline, then at 1 month, 6 months, then annually
  • Creatine kinase (CK) at baseline for long-term use; repeat if myopathy symptoms (muscle pain/weakness)
  • Renal function — guides dose adjustment
  • Monitor for GI symptoms — dose-limiting toxicity
Counselling
  • Colchicine is highly toxic if taken incorrectly. For acute gout: take only the prescribed doses (1 mg then 0.5 mg 1 hour later). Do NOT take more. Do NOT repeat the course within 3 days.
  • Start as early as possible in an attack — less effective if >24 hours after onset.
  • Avoid grapefruit juice (inhibits CYP3A4 → increases colchicine levels).
  • Stop and seek urgent medical attention for: severe diarrhoea, vomiting, muscle pain, weakness, numbness, unusual bruising, or signs of infection.
  • Tell your doctor/pharmacist about ALL other medications — many drugs interact with colchicine.
Allopurinol Xanthine Oxidase Inhibitor — ULT First-Line
Dose (Gout ULT)
  • Start: 50–100 mg PO dailyTitrate: ↑ by 50 mg q2 weeks OR 100 mg q4 weeksTarget: serum urate (see table above)Maximum: 900 mg/day
  • Renal dosingeGFR >60: start 100 mg/dayeGFR 30–60: start 50 mg/dayeGFR 15–30: 50 mg every other dayeGFR <15: 50 mg twice weekly(Approx formula: allopurinol dose mg ≈ 1.5 × eGFR)
Mechanism of Action
Inhibits xanthine oxidase → ↓ conversion of hypoxanthine and xanthine to uric acid → ↓ uric acid production → ↓ serum urate. Sustained lowering allows existing crystal deposits to gradually dissolve, preventing future flares.
Indications
  • First-line ULT for chronic gout prevention
  • Hyperuricaemia in gout
  • Tumour lysis syndrome prophylaxis
  • Recurrent uric acid kidney stones
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Hypersensitivity to allopurinol — contraindicated
  • Azathioprine or mercaptopurine — contraindicated unless dose drastically reduced and FBC closely monitored (↑ myelosuppression risk)
  • HLA-B*5801 allele (SE Asian ancestry) — higher risk of severe hypersensitivity (SJS/TEN); consider screening
  • Renal impairment — slow titration; reduce initial dose (formula above)
  • Flares likely in first months — do not stop ULT
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Acute gout flare (early in treatment — mobilisation of crystals)
  • Raised liver enzymes
  • Oedema, rash
Infrequent
  • Arthralgia, dizziness, drowsiness, taste disturbance, abdominal pain
Rare / Serious
  • Allopurinol hypersensitivity syndrome (AHS) — fever, rash, organ failure; highest risk first 3 months and with renal impairment
  • Stevens–Johnson syndrome / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis
  • Multi-organ hypersensitivity, anaphylaxis
  • Hepatotoxicity, blood dyscrasias
  • Nephrolithiasis
Monitoring
  • Serum urate: every 2–5 weeks during dose titration, then every 6 months maintenance
  • Renal function: regularly (risk factor for gout; affects dosing)
  • LFTs: at baseline and periodically
  • Watch closely for hypersensitivity (rash, fever, mucosal involvement) — especially first 3 months
Counselling
  • You may experience more gout attacks in the first few months — this is expected and does not mean the treatment is failing. Keep taking it unless advised otherwise.
  • This is a long-term (usually lifelong) medication. Do not stop without medical advice.
  • Stay well hydrated — adequate fluid intake helps prevent urate build-up.
  • Can be split into 2 doses if GI side effects are an issue.
  • Stop immediately and seek urgent medical attention if: rash, fever, swollen lips/mouth, sore throat, yellowing of skin/eyes, or worsening kidney function. These may indicate serious hypersensitivity — time-critical.
  • Regular blood tests to confirm dose is adequate and check for side effects.
Febuxostat Xanthine Oxidase Inhibitor — ULT Second-Line
Dose (Gout ULT)
  • 40 mg PO daily for 2–4 weeks→ ↑ by 40 mg q2–4 weeks to targetMaximum: 120 mg/day
  • Renal: CrCl <30 mL/min → max 40 mg/dayMild-moderate hepatic impairment → max 80 mg/day
Mechanism of Action
Selective, potent non-purine inhibitor of xanthine oxidase → ↓ uric acid synthesis. Structurally different from allopurinol — alternative mechanism allows use when allopurinol is not tolerated.
Indications
  • Second-line ULT when allopurinol is unsuitable/not tolerated
  • Chronic symptomatic hyperuricaemia with gout or tophi
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Contraindicated: major cardiovascular disease (ischaemic heart disease, stroke) — ↑ CV mortality risk in trials
  • Caution: hepatic impairment — dose limit
  • Caution: moderate-severe renal impairment — dose limit 40 mg/day
  • Azathioprine or mercaptopurine — avoid combination (drastically reduce dose + monitor FBC if essential)
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Acute gout flare (early treatment)
  • Raised liver enzymes, oedema, rash
Rare
  • Hypersensitivity, SJS/TEN, multi-organ hypersensitivity
  • Hepatotoxicity, blood dyscrasias, nephrolithiasis
  • Cardiovascular events
Monitoring
  • Serum urate: q2–5 weeks during titration, then 6-monthly
  • Renal function, liver biochemistry, FBC
  • Attack frequency, tophi size
  • Cardiovascular status — monitor for CV events
Counselling
  • More gout attacks early in treatment are expected — this does not mean treatment failure.
  • Stop and seek urgent help if: rash, fever, swollen lips/mouth, sore throat, chest pain, or signs of liver problems (yellow skin/eyes, dark urine).
  • Stay well hydrated.
  • Regular urate and blood tests are needed to confirm dose is adequate.
  • Use with caution if you have heart disease — discuss risk/benefit with your doctor.
Probenecid Uricosuric Agent — ULT Second-Line/Add-On
Dose (Gout ULT)
  • 250 mg PO BD for 1 week
  • → 500 mg PO BD
  • → ↑ by 500 mg q4 weeks to target
  • Maximum: 2 g/day (in divided doses)
Mechanism of Action
Blocks renal tubular reabsorption of uric acid → ↑ urinary uric acid excretion → ↓ serum urate. Also blocks tubular excretion of some acidic drugs (e.g. penicillins) → historically used as adjunct to antibiotics.
Indications
  • Gout — second-line ULT or add-on to allopurinol
  • Adjunct to beta-lactam antibiotic therapy (historical use)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Contraindicated: history of uric acid kidney stones (↑ stone risk)
  • Contraindicated: blood dyscrasias
  • Contraindicated: allergy to probenecid
  • Avoid starting during acute gout flare
  • CrCl <50 mL/min — uricosuric effect reduced; largely ineffective
  • CrCl <10 mL/min — avoid (ineffective)
  • Elite athletes — prohibited in sport
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Rash, nausea, vomiting
Infrequent
  • Headache, dizziness, flushing, sore gums, alopecia, urinary frequency
  • Uric acid kidney stones (ensure adequate hydration)
Rare
  • Aplastic anaemia, leucopenia, thrombocytopenia
  • Hepatic necrosis, anaphylaxis, SJS
  • Nephrotic syndrome
Monitoring
  • Renal function regularly (risk factor for gout; guides dosing)
  • FBC regularly
  • Ensure adequate urine output (to reduce kidney stone risk)
  • Consider urine alkalinisation if stones are a concern
  • Continue at same dose during acute flares once established (do not stop)
Counselling
  • Take with food if it upsets your stomach.
  • Drink plenty of fluids (at least 2 litres/day) to reduce kidney stone risk.
  • More gout flares may occur early in treatment — this is expected and does not mean it is not working.
  • If you have an acute gout attack, do NOT stop probenecid — continue at the same dose.
  • Ongoing blood tests and urate monitoring are needed to assess dose adequacy.

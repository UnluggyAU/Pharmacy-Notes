Hypothyroidism

Deficiency of thyroid hormone leading to a reduced metabolic rate. Primary disease is most common (~95% of adult cases). Treated with lifelong levothyroxine replacement in most cases.

⚙️

Thyroid Physiology

💡
The hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis is the central regulatory mechanism. Understanding this axis is essential for interpreting blood tests, understanding disease, and monitoring treatment.
🧠 HPT Axis — Regulation
  • Hypothalamus → releases TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone)
  • TRH → stimulates anterior pituitary → releases TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone)
  • TSH → stimulates thyroid gland → synthesises and releases T3 and T4
  • Rising T3/T4 → negative feedback on pituitary and hypothalamus → ↓ TRH and TSH
  • Low T3/T4 → ↑ TRH and TSH (loss of negative feedback)
🔬 Hormone Synthesis (Follicular Cells)
  • Dietary iodine trapped from circulation, oxidised by thyroid peroxidase (TPO) and H₂O₂
  • Iodine attached to tyrosine residues in thyroglobulin → forms mono-iodotyrosine (MIT) and di-iodotyrosine (DIT)
  • Coupling via TPO: DIT + DIT → T4 (thyroxine); DIT + MIT → T3
  • Hormones stored in thyroid gland colloid; released on TSH stimulation
  • Thyroglobulin resorbed; amino acids and iodine recycled
🩸 Circulation & Peripheral Conversion
  • Thyroid secretes T4:T3 ratio of ~10:1 (~80–100 µg T4 and ~10 µg T3 per day)
  • Only 10% of circulating T3 is from direct thyroid secretion; 90% from peripheral conversion of T4 → T3 by 5-deiodinase (liver, kidney, brain)
  • T4 is a prohormone — converted peripherally to active T3 or inactive reverse T3 (rT3)
  • T4: 99.98% protein-bound (mainly TBG); only 0.02% free (active); half-life 6–7 days
  • T3: 99.8% protein-bound; 0.2% free; half-life 24–36 hours; more potent than T4; acts directly on target cell nuclear receptors
⚡ Thyroid Hormone Functions
  • ↑ Metabolic rate and oxygen consumption
  • ↑ Protein synthesis
  • ↑ Heart rate and cardiac output
  • Heat production and thermoregulation
  • ↑ Nervous system activity, mood, and cognition
  • Essential for fetal CNS and skeletal development (thyroid active from week 12 gestation)
  • Maternal TSH and free T4 do NOT cross the placenta — iodide does

⚠️
Key diagnostic rule: In primary hypothyroidism, the TSH is always elevated (↑) because the pituitary responds to low T4 by increasing TSH production. In secondary/tertiary, all three are low. Never interpret TSH alone — always consider clinical context and free T4.
⚠️
Important for central hypothyroidism (secondary/tertiary): Do NOT use TSH to guide dose adjustment — it will always be low/normal regardless of T4 status. Use free T4 (target upper half of normal range) and clinical symptoms instead.

Causes & Risk Factors

🔑 Primary Causes
  • Autoimmune (Hashimoto's thyroiditis) — most common in iodine-replete areas (>95% of adult primary cases); TPO antibodies almost always positive
  • Iodine deficiency — most common cause globally
  • Post-thyroidectomy or post-radioiodine therapy
  • Drug-induced (see below)
  • Subacute or painless thyroiditis (transient)
💊 Drug-Induced Hypothyroidism
  • Amiodarone — hypothyroidism in ~10% of treated patients
  • Lithium — hypothyroidism in ~10% of treated patients
  • Interferon alfa, interleukin-2 — autoimmune thyroiditis
  • Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (sunitinib, sorafenib)
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ipilimumab, nivolumab, pembrolizumab, atezolizumab)
  • Iodine excess — can paradoxically cause hypothyroidism (Wolff-Chaikoff effect)
👤 Patient Risk Factors
  • Female sex
  • Age >55 years
  • History of autoimmune disorders (type 1 diabetes, coeliac disease)
  • Conditions ↑ autoimmune risk (Down syndrome, Turner syndrome)
  • History of Graves disease (can develop hypothyroidism in remission)
  • Positive thyroid peroxidase antibody — ↑ risk progression of subclinical to overt disease
🩺

Clinical Features

💡
Symptoms are non-specific and gradual in onset — often attributed incorrectly to ageing or depression. Most clinically useful signs: myotonic (slow-relaxing) tendon reflexes, bradycardia, hair loss, cool and dry skin.
⚡ Cardiovascular
  • Bradycardia
  • ↓ Cardiac output
  • Cardiac enlargement
  • Pericardial effusion
  • ↑ Total and LDL cholesterol
🧠 Neuropsychiatric
  • Fatigue, lethargy, persistent drowsiness
  • Poor memory, ↓ cognitive function
  • Depression
  • Slow speech
  • Delayed deep-tendon reflexes (myotonic)
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome
  • Psychiatric disturbance
🌡️ Metabolic & GI
  • Weight gain despite ↓ appetite
  • Cold intolerance
  • Constipation
  • Abdominal distension, ascites
  • Macrocytic anaemia
  • ↑ LDL cholesterol, hypertriglyceridaemia
🧴 Skin, Hair & Nails
  • Dry, cold, thickened, flaking skin
  • Reduced sweating
  • Yellowish complexion
  • Puffy face and eyes (myxoedema)
  • Coarse, sparse, dry hair; hair loss
  • Loss of outer third of eyebrows
  • Brittle nails
🫁 Pulmonary & Other
  • Pleural effusion, shortness of breath
  • Goitre (in iodine deficiency or Hashimoto's)
  • Periorbital oedema
  • Muscle weakness and pain
  • Reduced hearing
  • Irregular / heavy periods; subfertility
  • ↓ Libido
🚨 Myxoedema Coma
  • Rare but potentially fatal — severe untreated hypothyroidism
  • Precipitants: hypothermia, infection, trauma, CNS depressants (opioids, benzodiazepines, phenothiazines, beta-blockers, anaesthetics)
  • Features: profound hypothermia, bradycardia, hypotension, hypoventilation, confusion, coma
  • Medical emergency — arrange immediate transfer; see treatment section

Additional investigations:
  • TPO antibody — diagnostic of Hashimoto's; predicts progression from subclinical to overt hypothyroidism; guides treatment decision in subclinical disease
  • Thyroglobulin antibody — may be elevated in Hashimoto's; used in thyroid cancer monitoring
  • FBC — mild macrocytic anaemia common; check for pernicious anaemia (may coexist)
  • Lipids — ↑ total cholesterol and LDL common; often improves with adequate thyroid replacement
  • Routine testing not recommended in asymptomatic general population — test only in those with symptoms or risk factors
⚠️ Drugs that alter diagnostic test results:
  • Biotin (vitamin B7) — can cause false-positive results suggesting Graves disease — stop at least 2 days before testing
  • Dopamine, glucocorticoids — lower serum TSH concentration (may mask hypothyroidism)
  • Phenytoin, carbamazepine, furosemide, heparin, aspirin, some NSAIDs — can alter serum T4 concentration depending on analytical method

Treatment Goals & TSH Targets

💡
Goal: achieve euthyroidism and relieve hypothyroid symptoms. TSH target varies with age — aggressive targets in the elderly increase risk of overtreatment (↑ AF, bone loss).

Ongoing Dose Titration & Adherence Strategies
Subtle dose adjustments
🔵 Prescribe different strength tablets on different days of the week (e.g. 100 µg on weekdays, 150 µg on weekends) to achieve total weekly requirements without needing fractional tablets 🔵 Not all brands are bioequivalent — if switching brands, recheck TFTs after 4–8 weeks and adjust dose if necessary ⚠️ Poor adherence: if daily dosing not feasible, a SINGLE WEEKLY DOSE (total weekly dose given at once) can safely be used. Address all precipitating factors.
⚠️
Critical safety — adrenal insufficiency: In hypopituitarism and adrenal insufficiency, ALWAYS treat glucocorticoid deficiency BEFORE starting levothyroxine. Thyroid hormones increase hepatic metabolism of glucocorticoids. Starting levothyroxine first can precipitate an adrenal crisis in patients with coexisting undiagnosed adrenal insufficiency.
⚠️
Combination T4 + T3 therapy (levothyroxine + liothyronine): Evidence does NOT support routine use over levothyroxine monotherapy. Available T3 preparations have higher T3:T4 ratios than physiological and short half-lives, risking serum T3 fluctuations. Not recommended in pregnancy. If considered, must be managed by a specialist.

Special Situations

🤰 Hypothyroidism in Pregnancy
  • Untreated maternal hypothyroidism → ↑ risk of miscarriage, premature birth, low birth weight, impaired fetal neurocognitive development
  • Thyroid hormone production increases up to 50% during healthy pregnancy
  • Pre-existing on levothyroxine: usually require 25–30% dose increase at 4–6 weeks gestation
  • Pre-conception: optimise dose to achieve TSH <2.5 mU/L before conception
  • Test TSH every 4–6 weeks in first trimester; again at ~30 weeks if stable
  • After delivery: reduce to pre-pregnancy dose; check TSH ~6 weeks postpartum
  • Combination T4+T3 therapy is CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy
  • Levothyroxine is SAFE to use in pregnancy and breastfeeding
  • Maternal TSH and free T4 do NOT cross placenta — iodide does
👶 Congenital Hypothyroidism
  • ~1 in 2500–3500 neonates; identified by neonatal screening
  • Start replacement within 24–48 hours of diagnosis; ideally before 2 weeks of age — prevents intellectual disability
  • Starting dose: 10–15 µg/kg/day (higher weight-based dose than adults)
  • Target: TSH lower half of normal; T4 upper half of normal
  • Assess thyroid function weekly for first month; every 2–3 months for first 5 years; then every 3–6 months until fully grown
  • Seek paediatric endocrinologist advice for all cases
💊 Drug-Induced Hypothyroidism
  • Lithium, amiodarone — hypothyroidism in ~10% each; may be reversible if drug stopped
  • Immune checkpoint inhibitors — often irreversible
  • Decision: continue drug + add levothyroxine vs stop drug (consider harms of each)
  • If levothyroxine started but causative drug later stopped: retest TSH 4–8 weeks after stopping levothyroxine; restart if TSH rises
🔪 Perioperative Management
  • Hypothyroidism should be adequately treated before elective surgery
  • If euthyroid on therapy: levothyroxine can be safely withheld for up to 7 days (long half-life)
  • If oral not possible after 7 days: switch to IV levothyroxine 75% of usual oral dose daily (IV bioavailability is higher)
  • Restart oral as soon as patient can take fluids

Drug Summaries

Levothyroxine (T4)Thyroid Hormone Analogue — Synthetic T4
Doses (Adult)
  • Initial full replacement: 1.6 µg/kg PO once daily (to nearest 25 µg)Use lean body weight at extremes of body weight. Adjust every 4–8 weeks based on TSH.
  • Initial partial replacement (elderly/CVD/subclinical): 25–50 µg PO once dailyStart at 12.5 µg in very frail or severe IHD. Increase by 25 µg every 6–8 weeks.
  • Usual maintenance range: 50–200 µg once daily
  • Myxoedema coma (IV): 300 µg loading dose by slow IV injection over 30 min → 1.2 µg/kg IV daily; or 75% of known oral dose IV daily
  • Perioperative (if oral not possible >7 days): 75% of usual oral dose IV daily
  • Weekly dosing (poor adherence): 11.2 µg/kg/week (= 1.6 µg/kg/day × 7) to nearest 100 µg
Doses (Paediatric)
  • Congenital hypothyroidism: 10–15 µg/kg/day (up to 15 µg/kg in athyreosis)
  • 1–12 years: 2–6 µg/kg/day, adjusted every 2–4 weeks by 25 µg
  • Seek paediatric endocrinologist advice. Crushed tablets given in small amount of water for infants.
Mechanism of Action
Levothyroxine is a synthetic form of T4 (thyroxine). It must be converted to active T3 by peripheral 5-deiodinase in liver, kidney, and brain before exerting its effects on target tissues. Acts on nuclear thyroid hormone receptors → regulates gene expression → ↑ metabolic rate, protein synthesis, heart function, thermogenesis, and nervous system activity. The 6–7 day half-life provides stable circulating T4 levels — makes it ideal for long-term replacement. Requires 4–8 weeks for full therapeutic effect after dose change.
Indications
  • Hypothyroidism (primary, secondary, tertiary, congenital)
  • Block-replace regimen in hyperthyroidism (combined with antithyroid drug)
  • TSH-suppressive therapy in thyroid cancer and euthyroid goitre
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Untreated hyperthyroidism — contraindicated
  • Adrenal insufficiency — must correct glucocorticoid deficiency BEFORE starting levothyroxine (risk of adrenal crisis)
  • Cardiovascular disease — risk of worsening ischaemia and arrhythmias; start low, titrate slowly; do not use full replacement strategy
  • Diabetes — may require adjustment of insulin or antidiabetic doses when starting; thyroid hormones alter glucose metabolism
  • Elderly — require slower dose adjustment; ↑ risk of cardiovascular adverse effects; use lower TSH targets
  • Pregnancy — safe to use; dose increase of ~25–40% likely required in first trimester; check TFTs each trimester; avoid combination T4+T3 in pregnancy
  • Children — levothyroxine preferred; try to avoid liothyronine (developing brain preferentially uses levothyroxine)
  • Store correctly — some brands require refrigeration; incorrect storage impairs potency
Adverse Effects
At therapeutic dose — usually from excessive dosage
  • Symptoms of hyperthyroidism: tachycardia, arrhythmia, palpitations, excitability, insomnia, flushing, sweating, diarrhoea, weight loss
  • Worsening of ischaemic heart disease symptoms — even at reduced doses in predisposed patients
  • Decreased bone density and fractures — particularly in overtreated postmenopausal women and those with suppressed TSH
  • Atrial fibrillation — with long-term overtreatment (suppressed TSH)
In children (excessive doses)
  • Craniosynostosis and advancement of bone age
  • Benign intracranial hypertension (headache, vomiting, papilloedema) — rarely in first weeks of treatment

Monitoring
  • Primary hypothyroidism: measure serum TSH every 4–8 weeks after dose change until stable, then 3 months, 6 months, then annually
  • Secondary/tertiary hypothyroidism: measure free T4 (NOT TSH) — take sample BEFORE daily dose; target upper half of normal range
  • Pregnancy: TFTs every 4–6 weeks in first trimester; at ~30 weeks if stable; at 6 weeks postpartum
  • Paediatrics: weekly TFTs for first month, then reduce frequency as TSH normalises; assess intellectual and physical development every 2–3 months for first 5 years
Counselling
  • Take in the same way every day with respect to food — best absorbed fasting (30–60 minutes before breakfast, or at bedtime at least 3 hours after evening meal).
  • Do NOT take at the same time as calcium supplements, iron supplements, antacids, PPIs, or cholestyramine — separate by at least 4 hours.
  • Tell your doctor if you experience palpitations, excessive sweating, weight loss, tremor, or insomnia — these may indicate overtreatment.
  • Check how to store your specific brand — some require refrigeration; incorrect storage reduces effectiveness.
  • Do not switch between brands without telling your doctor — different brands may not be bioequivalent and a dose adjustment may be needed.
  • Treatment is usually lifelong — do not stop without medical advice.
  • Infants: crush tablets and dissolve in a small amount (about one teaspoon) of water or non-soy milk for administration.
Liothyronine (T3)Thyroid Hormone Analogue — Synthetic T3 — Emergency / Specialist Use
Doses (Adult)
  • Myxoedema coma (IV): 20 µg slow IV injection BD (over 30 min) — add after 24 hours if no improvement on levothyroxine alone
  • Oral (specialist use only): Usual range 20–60 µg daily in 2–3 divided doses
  • 20–25 µg liothyronine ≈ 100 µg levothyroxine in activity
  • Seek endocrinologist advice for all liothyronine use
Mechanism of Action
Liothyronine is synthetic T3 — the biologically active form of thyroid hormone. Unlike levothyroxine (T4), it does NOT require peripheral conversion by 5-deiodinase. It acts directly on thyroid hormone nuclear receptors. Much shorter half-life (24–36 hours) than T4 (6–7 days) → more rapid onset but less stable serum levels. More potent than T4 at equivalent doses.
Indications
  • Myxoedema coma — when T4→T3 conversion may be impaired
  • Short-term IV use when oral levothyroxine cannot be taken
  • Specialist use: when rapid thyroid hormone effect required
  • Short-term withdrawal before thyroid cancer surveillance scanning (shortens symptomatic hypothyroid period vs levothyroxine withdrawal)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Untreated hyperthyroidism — contraindicated
  • Adrenal insufficiency — correct glucocorticoid deficiency first
  • Cardiovascular disease — high risk of worsening ischaemia and arrhythmias; liothyronine is more potent and acts faster; use with extreme caution or avoid
  • Pregnancy — avoid liothyronine; developing brain preferentially uses T4 (levothyroxine); combination T4+T3 contraindicated in pregnancy
  • Children — try to avoid; developing brain preferentially uses levothyroxine (T4)
  • Elderly — ↑ risk of cardiovascular adverse effects with any thyroid hormone replacement; extreme caution
  • Diabetes — may alter insulin/antidiabetic drug requirements
Adverse Effects
Usually from excessive dosage
  • Tachycardia, palpitations, arrhythmia — more pronounced than with levothyroxine due to higher potency and shorter half-life
  • Excitability, anxiety, insomnia, flushing, sweating, diarrhoea, weight loss (symptoms of hyperthyroidism)
  • Serum T3 fluctuations throughout the day — especially with once-daily dosing
  • Worsening ischaemic heart disease symptoms — can occur even at reduced doses
  • Osteoporosis / accelerated bone loss with chronic overtreatment
Why Liothyronine is NOT the Standard Treatment
  • Higher potency → narrower therapeutic index → more difficult to dose accurately
  • Short half-life (24–36 hours) → fluctuating T3 levels throughout day (risk of peaks causing hyperthyroid symptoms)
  • Available preparations contain much higher T3:T4 ratio than the body normally produces
  • Thyroid extracts and compounded preparations are not regulated for potency or consistency
  • Evidence does NOT support combination T3+T4 therapy over levothyroxine monotherapy for routine use
Counselling (if prescribed oral)
  • Take in divided doses as directed — do NOT take once daily (causes T3 peaks and troughs).
  • Report any palpitations, sweating, tremor, or insomnia immediately.
  • This medicine is much more potent than levothyroxine — never increase dose without specialist advice.

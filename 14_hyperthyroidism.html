Hyperthyroidism & Thyrotoxicosis

Thyrotoxicosis = excess thyroid hormones in body tissue regardless of cause. Most cases are due to hyperthyroid disorders. Managed with antithyroid drugs, radioiodine, or thyroidectomy. Always guided by specialist.

⚙️

Pathophysiology

💡
Thyrotoxicosis refers to the clinical syndrome resulting from excess T3 and T4 in body tissues, regardless of the cause. Hyperthyroidism specifically refers to cases where the thyroid itself is producing excess hormone.
🧠 Normal HPT Axis (Recap)
  • Hypothalamus → TRH → anterior pituitary → TSH → thyroid → T3/T4
  • Rising T3/T4 → negative feedback → ↓ TRH and TSH
  • In hyperthyroidism: excess T3/T4 suppresses TSH via negative feedback → TSH is LOW (or undetectable) in primary hyperthyroidism
🔬 Primary Hyperthyroidism Mechanisms
  • Graves disease: TSH receptor autoantibodies (TRAb) mimic TSH → stimulate thyroid continuously → excess T3/T4 independent of HPT feedback
  • Toxic multinodular goitre: Nodules become autonomously functioning → continuous hormone secretion independent of TSH
  • Toxic adenoma: Single autonomously functioning nodule (TSH receptor or G-protein gene mutation)
⚡ Secondary Hyperthyroidism
  • Pituitary produces excess TSH (e.g. TSH-secreting adenoma) → stimulates thyroid → excess T3/T4
  • Result: TSH, T3, and T4 are ALL high on blood test (unlike primary where TSH is low)
  • Rare — accounts for <1% of hyperthyroid cases
🩸 Thyrotoxicosis Without Hyperthyroidism
  • Thyroiditis (subacute, postpartum, sporadic) — damage to thyroid follicles → preformed hormone leaks into circulation; the thyroid is NOT overproducing, so antithyroid drugs are NOT effective
  • Exogenous thyroid hormone — excess ingestion; low thyroglobulin distinguishes from true hyperthyroidism
  • Amiodarone-induced (type 2) — direct destructive thyroiditis; treated with glucocorticoids, not antithyroid drugs

Clinical Features

⚠️
Thyroid hormones affect almost every organ system — atypical presentations are common, particularly in older people. Long-term untreated thyrotoxicosis → cardiovascular disease (especially AF) and accelerated bone mass loss.
❤️ Cardiovascular
  • Tachycardia, palpitations
  • Atrial fibrillation
  • High-output cardiac failure
  • ↑ Pulse pressure
  • Hypertension
🧠 Neuropsychiatric
  • Anxiety, irritability, nervousness
  • Tremor (shaky hands)
  • Insomnia
  • Heat intolerance
  • Emotional lability
⚡ Metabolic & GI
  • Weight loss despite ↑ appetite
  • Heat intolerance, sweating
  • Frequent loose stools
  • ↑ Metabolic rate
🦴 Musculoskeletal
  • Muscle weakness (proximal myopathy)
  • Fatigue
  • Accelerated bone loss
  • Hair loss/thinning
👩 Reproductive
  • Disrupted menstrual cycle
  • Sexual dysfunction
  • ↑ First-trimester miscarriage risk
  • Hyperemesis gravidarum
🚨 Thyroid Storm
  • Pyrexia, severe tachycardia
  • Vomiting, dehydration
  • Delirium, coma
  • Organ dysfunction
  • Life-threatening emergency — immediate hospitalisation

🔬 Diagnostic Approach
  • Symptomatic overt thyrotoxicosis: start beta-blocker immediately for symptom relief while awaiting further testing
  • Subclinical or minimally overt, no symptoms: retest TSH and T4 after 6–8 weeks to confirm persistence (subacute thyroiditis is transient)
  • Test TSH-receptor antibody (TRAb) — significantly elevated → diagnostic of Graves disease (95% sensitivity)
  • If TRAb absent → radionuclide thyroid scan to differentiate remaining causes
  • Ultrasound ONLY useful if patient is taking amiodarone, or has known/palpable nodules — NOT recommended in other patients
  • Radionuclide scan CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy
⚠️ Important Pitfalls
  • Low/suppressed TSH in first trimester of pregnancy is often normal — due to hCG stimulating TSH receptors; does NOT require treatment
  • Iodine exposure (contrast media) can exacerbate hyperthyroidism in nodular disease — iodine-induced thyrotoxicosis may be self-limiting, do NOT treat unless persistent
  • Amiodarone: do NOT use amiodarone to treat AF in thyrotoxicosis — iodine content impairs antithyroid drug response and blocks radioiodine uptake
  • Thyrotoxicosis increases drug clearance — review ALL drug doses when treating; warfarin sensitivity increased (lower dose needed)
🎯

Treatment Goals

Primary
  • Achieve euthyroidism
  • Relieve symptoms of thyrotoxicosis
  • Prevent long-term complications (AF, bone loss)
Dose Adjustment Target
  • TSH and T3/T4 within normal range
  • Initially base dose on T3 and T4 — TSH may remain suppressed for several months after euthyroidism restored
Specialist Involvement
  • All patients — specialist guidance recommended
  • Agranulocytosis risk from antithyroid drugs requires careful patient education
  • Definitive treatment choice is individualised

Antithyroid Drug Therapy — Key Principles

⚡ Dose Titration Principles
  • Initial dose based on: symptom severity, gland size, biochemical severity, urgency
  • Titrate down after 4–6 weeks based on T3 and T4 (NOT TSH — remains suppressed months after euthyroidism)
  • If T3/T4 drop by half → halve dose
  • Hyperthyroidism can persist despite normal T4 if T3 still elevated — consider dose increase if still symptomatic
  • Reassess every 4–6 weeks; adjust to maintain T3/T4 in normal range
  • Do not stop drug abruptly once TFTs normalise
🆚 Carbimazole vs PTU
  • Carbimazole: First-line in most situations; can be given once daily as maintenance
  • PTU: Second-line only; preferred in: first trimester of pregnancy, thyroid storm (inhibits T4→T3 conversion), carbimazole intolerance
  • PTU: severe hepatotoxicity risk — potentially fatal; contraindicated in children; more frequent dosing required (shorter half-life)
  • In first trimester: switch carbimazole → PTU (lower risk of severe congenital abnormalities); switch back to carbimazole in second trimester
  • Do not use carbimazole or PTU in any patient with history of agranulocytosis from either drug
🚨 Agranulocytosis
  • Most likely in first 3 months of therapy
  • Rapid onset — routine monitoring of FBC has limited clinical usefulness (can develop between checks)
  • Patients MUST be counselled to immediately STOP drug and seek urgent medical assessment if: fever, acute malaise, mouth ulcers, or severe pharyngitis
  • Do NOT rechallenge with either drug after agranulocytosis — consider radioiodine or thyroidectomy
🔄 Block-Replace Regimen
  • High-dose antithyroid drug to fully block thyroid + levothyroxine to prevent hypothyroidism
  • Does NOT improve remission rates in Graves disease
  • ↑ Adverse effect risk compared to titration regimen
  • Reserved for rare specific situations (e.g. patient who changes easily from hypo to hyper); specialist use only
  • CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy (risk of fetal hypothyroidism)

Special Situations

🚨 Thyroid Storm
  • Life-threatening emergency — immediate hospitalisation by ambulance
  • Treat underlying precipitant (infection, trauma, surgery)
  • Control tachycardia: propranolol 40–80 mg PO QID; or esmolol IV (loading 250–500 µg/kg then 50–100 µg/kg/min infusion); or metoprolol 5 mg IV q5 minutes (up to 15 mg)
  • Block synthesis: propylthiouracil 200 mg PO q4–6h (preferred — also blocks T4→T3); OR carbimazole 20 mg q6–8h
  • Block hormone release: Lugol's solution 0.5 mL TDS (iodide acutely inhibits hormone release)
  • Block T4→T3 conversion: dexamethasone 4 mg PO/IV q12h
  • Antiarrhythmics if arrhythmia; fluid resuscitation
🤰 Hyperthyroidism in Pregnancy
  • Specialist management essential
  • Goal: maintain free T4 in upper half of trimester-specific range; lowest effective antithyroid dose; never block-replace
  • First trimester: use PTU (carbimazole linked to aplasia cutis and embryopathy)
  • Second trimester: switch BACK to carbimazole (PTU hepatotoxicity risk; congenital abnormality risk has passed)
  • Many Graves patients improve in pregnancy — progressive dose reduction often possible; disease often relapses postpartum
  • Radioiodine: CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy
  • Gestational hyperthyroidism (due to hCG in first trimester): usually self-limiting; does not usually require treatment
👶 Neonatal Hyperthyroidism
  • Risk: ~1–2% in infants of mothers with current or prior Graves disease
  • Caused by transplacental passage of maternal TRAb
  • Monitor maternal TRAb in all pregnant women with Graves history — in first trimester; if elevated, repeat at ~20 and ~32 weeks
  • All at-risk neonates: measure TSH, free T3, free T4 at delivery and at day 2–7 of life
  • If mother was on antithyroid drug in pregnancy: retest at day 10–14 (drug may delay presentation)
  • Neonatal hyperthyroidism resolves spontaneously in weeks; short-term beta-blocker + antithyroid drug may be needed
👁️ Graves Eye Disease
  • Not directly correlated with degree of hyperthyroidism — can progress unpredictably
  • Achieving euthyroidism is important initial goal
  • Active Graves eye disease: radioiodine is CONTRAINDICATED (can worsen eye disease)
  • Mild symptoms: lubricant eye drops (hypromellose or polyvinyl alcohol)
  • Severe disease: IV methylprednisolone (first-line) or high-dose oral prednisolone; requires both ophthalmologist and endocrinologist involvement
  • Smoking strongly associated with more severe eye disease — counsel all Graves patients to stop smoking
  • Selenium supplementation may improve mild Graves eye disease (RCT evidence)
💊 Amiodarone-Induced Thyrotoxicosis
  • Specialist management required
  • Can occur up to 12–18 months after STOPPING amiodarone (long half-life)
  • Type 1 (underlying thyroid disease + excess iodine) → antithyroid drugs
  • Type 2 (destructive thyroiditis) → high-dose glucocorticoids; taper based on clinical and biochemical response
  • Often difficult to distinguish → empirical dual therapy (both antithyroid + glucocorticoid)
  • If not responding at 4–6 weeks → thyroidectomy may be required
  • Decision to stop/continue amiodarone: in consultation with cardiologist

Drug Summaries

CarbimazoleAntithyroid Drug — Thionamide — First-Line
Doses (Adult)
  • Significant symptoms or biochemically severe (T3/T4 >2.5× ULN): 30–45 mg/day in 2–3 divided doses (up to 60 mg/day in severe cases)
  • Mild symptoms or mild-moderate biochemical disease: 10–20 mg/day in 2–3 divided doses
  • Maintenance (once euthyroid): 5–15 mg/day (range 2.5–40 mg/day) — can be given once daily
  • Block-replace regimen: Continue initial high dose + add levothyroxine 100–150 µg when T4 in normal range
  • Thyroid storm: 60–80 mg/day in divided doses, gradually reduced
  • Children: 0.2–0.3 mg/kg PO three times daily → titrate to maintenance 2.5–5 mg up to TDS
Mechanism of Action
Blocks thyroid hormone synthesis by inhibiting thyroid peroxidase → prevents oxidation of iodide and subsequent iodination of tyrosine residues in thyroglobulin → ↓ formation of MIT, DIT, T3, and T4. Does NOT block release of pre-formed hormone or peripheral conversion of T4→T3 (unlike propylthiouracil at high doses).
Indications
  • Graves disease (first-line)
  • Short-term preparation before thyroid surgery or radioiodine
  • Thyroid storm
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Prior agranulocytosis from carbimazole OR propylthiouracil — contraindicated in both; seek alternative (radioiodine, thyroidectomy)
  • History of acute pancreatitis with carbimazole — contraindicated
  • First trimester of pregnancy — switch to propylthiouracil (carbimazole associated with aplasia cutis and embryopathy)
  • Women planning to conceive or not using adequate contraception — avoid carbimazole; switch to PTU before conception
  • Breastfeeding — use lowest effective dose; monitor infant; carbimazole generally preferred over PTU (lower hepatotoxicity risk)
  • High iodine exposure (contrast, amiodarone, kelp) — may be resistant; higher doses required
  • Liver disease — use with caution; monitor LFTs
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Itching and rash — most common; may respond to antihistamines; can often switch between carbimazole and PTU without recurrence (except if agranulocytosis)
  • Nausea, vomiting, gastric discomfort, headache, arthralgia
  • Mild leucopenia (monitor)
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Agranulocytosis — most likely in first 3 months; STOP IMMEDIATELY if fever/malaise/pharyngitis; do NOT rechallenge
  • Cholestatic jaundice (rare with carbimazole — less hepatotoxic than PTU)
  • Acute pancreatitis — contraindication to rechallenge
  • Lupus-like syndrome, vasculitis, myositis
Monitoring
  • TFTs every 4–6 weeks, adjusting dose to maintain T3/T4 in normal range. Base initial adjustments on T3/T4 not TSH (TSH remains suppressed for months)
  • FBC if symptoms of agranulocytosis (routine monitoring of FBC has limited clinical usefulness due to rapid onset)
  • TRAb level — if normal before stopping antithyroid drug, ↑ chance of sustained remission
  • Pregnancy: TFTs and free T4 every 4–6 weeks; use lowest effective dose
Counselling
  • MOST IMPORTANT: Stop the medicine IMMEDIATELY and go to your doctor or emergency department if you develop fever, sore throat, mouth ulcers, or feel very unwell — these may be signs of a serious but rare blood condition called agranulocytosis.
  • Take doses as directed — do not skip or adjust doses without medical advice.
  • Rash and itch are common — report but do not stop immediately without advice.
  • Women: tell your doctor before you become pregnant or if you are trying to conceive.
Propylthiouracil (PTU)Antithyroid Drug — Thionamide — Second-Line / Specific Indications
Doses (Adult)
  • Significant symptoms / severe: 300–450 mg/day in 2–4 divided doses
  • Mild/moderate: 100–200 mg/day in 2–4 divided doses
  • Maintenance: 25–300 mg/day in divided doses
  • Thyroid storm: 600–1200 mg/day in divided doses, gradually reduced; rectal administration also effective if oral not possible
  • Propylthiouracil has a shorter plasma half-life than carbimazole → must continue in divided doses (cannot give once daily as maintenance, unlike carbimazole)
Mechanism of Action
Inhibits thyroid peroxidase → ↓ thyroid hormone synthesis (same as carbimazole). Additionally, at high doses: inhibits peripheral 5-deiodinase → ↓ conversion of T4 to T3 in peripheral tissues. This additional mechanism is particularly valuable in thyroid storm.
Specific Indications (where PTU is preferred over carbimazole)
  • First trimester of pregnancy — associated with less severe congenital abnormalities than carbimazole
  • Thyroid storm — high doses also block T4→T3 conversion
  • Carbimazole intolerance (not agranulocytosis)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Prior agranulocytosis from carbimazole OR propylthiouracil — contraindicated in both
  • Children — contraindicated (higher risk of severe hepatotoxicity than in adults)
  • Hepatic impairment — caution; PTU can cause severe hepatotoxicity
  • Breastfeeding — carbimazole preferred over PTU (lower hepatotoxicity risk)
  • Measure LFTs at baseline and when clinically indicated — stop if significant abnormalities
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Itching, rash, leucopenia, nausea, vomiting, headache, arthralgia
  • Asymptomatic ↑ aminotransferases in first 2 months — usually resolves with continued treatment
Rare (<0.1%) — Serious
  • Serious hepatotoxicity (hepatocellular hepatitis) — potentially fatal; requires liver transplantation; STOP immediately if jaundice, abdominal pain, dark urine, or malaise
  • Agranulocytosis — same risk as carbimazole; STOP immediately if fever/malaise/pharyngitis
  • ANCA-positive vasculitis — rare; seek specialist advice
  • Lupus-like syndrome, myositis, bleeding
Counselling
  • MOST IMPORTANT: Stop immediately and seek urgent medical assessment if fever, sore throat, mouth ulcers (agranulocytosis) OR jaundice, abdominal pain, dark urine, or significant nausea/malaise (hepatotoxicity) develop.
  • Take in divided doses as prescribed — do NOT take as a single daily dose (shorter half-life than carbimazole).
  • Women in first trimester of pregnancy should be switched to PTU — tell all treating clinicians.
  • In second trimester of pregnancy: switch back to carbimazole as directed.
Beta-Blockers — Propranolol & AtenololSymptomatic Control Only — Not Definitive Treatment
⚠️
Beta-blockers do NOT reduce thyroid hormone levels. They only relieve adrenergic symptoms (palpitations, tremor, sweating, anxiety) while antithyroid drugs gradually achieve euthyroidism. Discontinue once euthyroid.
Doses (Adult)
  • Atenolol: 25 mg PO once daily → increase as needed up to 50 mg/day (titrate to heart rate)
  • Propranolol (oral): 10–40 mg PO 3–4 times daily (tachyarrhythmias / hyperthyroidism)
  • Propranolol — Thyroid storm: 40–80 mg PO QID; OR esmolol IV 250–500 µg/kg loading → 50–100 µg/kg/min infusion; OR metoprolol 5 mg IV q5 minutes (max 15 mg)
  • Titration guide: heart rate (target ~80–90 bpm at rest)
  • Diltiazem (if beta-blockers contraindicated): 60 mg PO QID
Propranolol vs Atenolol in Thyrotoxicosis
  • Propranolol: Non-selective β-blocker; preferred in thyroid storm — high doses also inhibit 5-deiodinase → ↓ T4→T3 peripheral conversion
  • Atenolol: β1-selective; once-daily dosing convenience; preferred in mild-moderate symptoms
  • Most beta-blockers provide adequate symptom relief — dose should be titrated to heart rate response
Precautions & Contraindications (Beta-Blockers)
  • Bradycardia (<45–50 bpm), second- or third-degree AV block, sick sinus syndrome (without pacemaker) — contraindicated
  • Severe or poorly controlled asthma — contraindicated (may precipitate bronchospasm)
  • Cardiogenic shock, severe hypotension — contraindicated
  • Uncontrolled heart failure — caution; may initially worsen; use propranolol with caution; if already on beta-blocker for HF, only consider dose increase if safe
  • Diabetes — may mask tachycardia of hypoglycaemia; may increase hypoglycaemia frequency; selective agents (atenolol) preferred
  • Peripheral arterial disease, Raynaud's — caution; may worsen peripheral circulation
  • Phaeochromocytoma — do NOT give beta-blocker without prior alpha-blocker (may precipitate hypertensive crisis)
  • Pregnancy: beta-blockers may cause fetal bradycardia; atenolol should be avoided in early pregnancy; propranolol generally preferred in pregnancy
  • Breastfeeding: propranolol preferred (more protein-bound; less transfer to breast milk than atenolol)
  • Do NOT stop suddenly — taper over at least 2 weeks (risk of rebound hypertension, angina exacerbation, MI, arrhythmia)
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Bradycardia, hypotension, orthostatic hypotension
  • Fatigue, dizziness, cold extremities, nausea, diarrhoea
  • Bronchospasm, dyspnoea — especially in asthma/COPD
  • Alteration of glucose and lipid metabolism
Infrequent/Rare
  • Hallucinations, insomnia, nightmares, heart block
  • Exacerbation of psoriasis, impotence, alopecia
Counselling
  • This medicine does not treat the thyroid condition — it only relieves symptoms until your thyroid treatment takes effect. It will be stopped once your thyroid levels are normal.
  • May cause dizziness or tiredness, especially at start — take care with driving.
  • You may feel dizzy on standing — get up gradually.
  • Do NOT stop this medicine suddenly without medical advice — risk of rebound palpitations or cardiac effects.
  • If you have asthma, report any worsening of breathing immediately.

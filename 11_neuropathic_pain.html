Neuropathic Pain

Pain arising from lesion or disease affecting the somatosensory nervous system — characterised by altered sensation, sensitisation, and neuralgia. Adjuvants are the mainstay of drug therapy.

🔬

Pathophysiology

⚡ Peripheral Mechanisms
  • Nerve injury → changes in ion channel expression and function
  • ↑ excitability of peripheral nociceptors (peripheral sensitisation)
  • Ectopic discharge from damaged nerve fibres
  • Altered sodium channel activity → spontaneous firing
  • Loss of inhibitory interneurons at the site of injury
🧠 Central Mechanisms
  • Central sensitisation — spinal cord becomes hyperexcitable
  • Wind-up: repeated C-fibre stimulation → progressively amplified pain responses
  • Loss of descending inhibitory control
  • Glial cell activation → inflammatory mediator release in CNS
  • Structural changes in the CNS with chronic neuropathic pain
🧩 Sensitisation Phenomena
  • Allodynia: pain from normally non-painful stimulus (e.g. light touch, clothing)
  • Hyperalgesia: exaggerated pain response to a normally painful stimulus
  • Dysaesthesia: spontaneous or evoked unpleasant abnormal sensations
  • Spontaneous pain: occurs without any triggering stimulus
⚠️

Causes & Clinical Examples

CategoryExamples
Peripheral nervous systemPostherpetic neuralgia (shingles), diabetic peripheral neuropathy, chemotherapy-induced neuropathy, HIV neuropathy, compressive neuropathy (e.g. carpal tunnel), complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)

🩺

Clinical Features

🔥 Pain Descriptors
  • Burning, electric shocks, stabbing, shooting
  • Pins and needles, tingling, crawling
  • Hot-cold sensations
  • Often described as "different" from ordinary pain
🌡️ Sensory Abnormalities
  • Allodynia — pain from light touch, clothing, temperature
  • Hyperalgesia — exaggerated pain from normally painful stimuli
  • Hypoaesthesia — reduced sensation in the painful area
  • Motor or sensory deficits in the painful area
🔎 Autonomic Features
  • Skin colour changes
  • Temperature asymmetry
  • Altered sweating
  • Trophic changes (skin, hair, nails)
  • Oedema (especially CRPS)
🔍

Diagnosis & Assessment

Neuropathic pain is a clinical diagnosis based on history and examination.

Assessment findings suggesting neuropathic component:
  • Pain described as electric shocks, burning, or pins and needles
  • Allodynia or hyperalgesia identified on examination
  • Motor or sensory deficits in the painful area
  • Area of pain consistent with the innervation of the affected neural structure

Screening tools: PainDETECT questionnaire can help identify pain likely to have a neuropathic component.

Imaging: MRI can sometimes identify cause, but should only be performed if it will change management — do not image solely to confirm diagnosis.

Surgery: Generally ineffective for neuropathic pain (exception: trigeminal neuralgia and radicular pain from nerve root compression). In the medium to long term, surgery may worsen symptoms.
🎯

Treatment Goals

Primary
  • Reduce pain intensity to a tolerable level
  • Restore function and quality of life
  • Improve sleep
Realistic Expectations
  • Complete pain elimination is rarely achievable
  • A ≥30–50% reduction in pain is considered meaningful
  • Function and quality of life improvements are co-equal targets
Non-Pharmacological (All Patients)
  • Physical activity and pacing
  • Psychological techniques (CBT, mindfulness)
  • Social reconnection and support
  • Sleep improvement
⚠️
Analgesics are only partially effective in neuropathic pain — adjuvants are the mainstay of drug therapy, not opioids. Drug therapy should not be used in children without specialist advice.

💡
Invasive procedures (nerve blocks, radiofrequency ablation, spinal cord stimulators) may be considered in carefully selected patients after specialist review — not first-line.

Drug Summaries

Gabapentinoids — Gabapentin & PregabalinAdjuvant Analgesic — α2δ Calcium Channel Ligand
⚠️
Misuse and dependence increasingly reported. Gabapentinoids are misused for euphoric and sedative effects. History of substance misuse or psychiatric disorders — ↑ risk of misuse; avoid if possible. Most patients will NOT achieve clinically useful pain relief — reassess after 4 weeks and every 3–6 months ongoing.
Doses (Adult — Neuropathic Pain)
  • Gabapentin — initial: 100–300 mg PO once daily at nightTitrate every 3–7 days: nocte → BD → TDS. Maximum: 3600 mg/24h. Frail/>70 years: max 900 mg/24h.
  • Pregabalin — initial: 25–75 mg PO at nightIncrease to BD after 3–7 days. Titrate slowly. Maximum: 600 mg/24h. Frail/>70 years: max 300 mg/24h. Uneven dosing (larger evening dose) may improve sleep and reduce daytime sedation.
  • Switch between gabapentin and pregabalin if one ineffective — individual response may differ
Renal Dose Adjustment
  • Gabapentin: CrCl 50–80: 0.6–1.8 g/day | CrCl 30–50: 300–900 mg/day | CrCl 15–30: ≤600 mg/day | CrCl <15: ≤300 mg/day
  • Pregabalin: CrCl 30–60: initially 75 mg/day, max 300 mg | CrCl 15–30: 25–50 mg/day, max 150 mg | CrCl <15: 25 mg/day, max 75 mg
Mechanism of Action
Bind to α2-δ protein subunit of high threshold voltage-dependent calcium channels → ↓ calcium influx → ↓ release of excitatory neurotransmitters (glutamate, substance P, noradrenaline) from hyperexcited nociceptors. Note: structurally related to GABA but do NOT significantly affect GABA or its receptors. Also modulate descending inhibitory pain pathways.
Evidence Notes
  • Effective for diabetic neuropathy and postherpetic neuralgia; pregabalin in post-traumatic neuropathic pain
  • Little evidence for other neuropathic pain types
  • Pregabalin NOT effective for sciatica (RCT evidence)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Opioids or other CNS depressants — ↑ risk of CNS and respiratory depression; deaths reported; avoid combination or monitor closely
  • History of substance misuse or psychiatric disorders — ↑ risk of gabapentinoid misuse and dependence; avoid if possible
  • Pregabalin — pregnancy: avoid if possible; may increase risk of major congenital malformations; ensure effective contraception
  • Renal impairment — dose reduction required for both (renally excreted); see table
  • Pregabalin — heart failure: use with caution (rare reports of worsening)
  • Absence seizures — risk of aggravation
  • Elderly/frail — ↑ falls and cognitive impairment; lower doses, slower titration
  • Do not stop abruptly — taper over ≥1 week (withdrawal symptoms; ↑ seizure risk)
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%) — Class
  • Drowsiness, sedation, fatigue, dizziness, vertigo, ataxia, dysarthria
  • Confusion, amnesia, euphoria, tremor, hypoaesthesia
  • Peripheral oedema, dry mouth, weight gain, blurred vision, diplopia
Common (>1%) — Pregabalin specific
  • Irritability, depression, lethargy, insomnia, impaired balance, constipation, muscle cramps, arthralgia
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Agitation, rash, sexual dysfunction
  • Pregabalin: hallucinations, cognitive impairment, urinary incontinence, tachycardia, first-degree heart block, hypotension
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Respiratory depression — with opioids/CNS depressants
  • Severe hypersensitivity — anaphylaxis, angioedema, SJS
  • Rhabdomyolysis, renal failure
  • Pregabalin: heart failure, pancreatitis
Deprescribing
  • <3 months: reduce by 25–30% per week
  • >3 months: reduce by 25–30% every 2 weeks
Counselling
  • Take the largest dose at night — reduces daytime sedation; drowsiness usually improves after 1–2 weeks.
  • Do not stop suddenly — taper gradually as directed (withdrawal symptoms; seizure risk in epilepsy).
  • Avoid alcohol and sedating medications — additive CNS depression risk.
  • Take care with driving and machinery, especially at initiation and after dose changes.
  • Pregabalin: use effective contraception. Do not crush or chew capsules.
  • Allow 4 weeks to assess benefit. If no meaningful improvement, the dose will be tapered and stopped.
TCAs — Amitriptyline & NortriptylineAdjuvant Analgesic — Tricyclic Antidepressant
Doses (Adult — Neuropathic Pain)
  • Amitriptyline: 10–25 mg PO at night initially → ↑ by 10–25 mg every 7 days as toleratedDiscontinue if no pain relief by 50 mg. If benefit → may titrate up to 150 mg at night. Titrate in increments every 2–3 days as needed.
  • Nortriptyline: 10–25 mg PO at night initially → titrate weekly → up to 150 mg at night
  • Older/frail: lower starting dose, slower titration. Nortriptyline preferred in elderly.
  • Note: analgesic doses much lower than antidepressant doses; antidepressant doses NOT required for analgesia
Mechanism of Action
Inhibit reuptake of noradrenaline and serotonin → ↑ descending inhibitory pain pathways. Also block cholinergic, histaminergic, and alpha1-adrenergic receptors (source of most adverse effects). Sodium channel blocking and NMDA antagonism contribute to analgesia. Antihistaminergic effect → ↑ sleep.
Nortriptyline vs Amitriptyline
  • Nortriptyline is the active metabolite of amitriptyline
  • Fewer anticholinergic effects, less sedation, less hypotension — preferred in elderly
  • Serum drug concentration can guide dosage (best-defined concentration-effect relationship of all TCAs)
  • Both equally effective for neuropathic pain at analgesic doses
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Second- or third-degree heart block, severe ischaemic heart disease, cardiac instability (recent MI) — contraindicated
  • MAOIs — contraindicated (serotonin syndrome)
  • High overdose/suicide risk — prescribe small quantities; overdose is potentially fatal
  • QT prolongation risk — TCAs prolong QT; avoid if uncorrectable QT risk factors. ECG monitoring at higher doses or in at-risk patients.
  • Epilepsy — ↓ seizure threshold (dose-dependent); caution, low doses, slow titration
  • BPH, narrow-angle glaucoma — anticholinergic effects may worsen; urinary retention risk
  • Elderly — ↑ falls, confusion, anticholinergic effects; prefer nortriptyline
  • Bipolar disorder — may trigger manic episode
  • Hepatic impairment — halve dose in severe impairment
  • Check BP (supine and standing) before and after starting and after each dose change
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Sedation, drowsiness — usually diminishes after ~7 days; take at night
  • Dry mouth, constipation, urinary hesitancy, blurred vision, mydriasis (anticholinergic)
  • Orthostatic hypotension, sinus tachycardia — ↑ falls risk; especially elderly
  • Weight gain, tremor, dizziness, sweating, agitation, insomnia, anxiety, confusion
  • Sexual dysfunction, anticholinergic delirium (elderly/Parkinson's)
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Slowed cardiac conduction, T-wave changes, arrhythmias (higher doses)
  • Nausea, hyperglycaemia, allergic skin reactions, manic episodes
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Blood dyscrasias, hepatitis, paralytic ileus
  • Prolonged QT, seizures, hyponatraemia (SIADH), increased intraocular pressure
Monitoring
  • BP (supine and standing) before starting, after starting, and after each dose change
  • ECG if cardiac risk factors — monitor for heart block or QT prolongation
  • Monitor suicidal ideation early in treatment, particularly in young people
  • Therapeutic response usually delayed ~2 weeks; adverse effects may appear early
Counselling
  • Take at night — drowsiness is expected and useful for sleep; usually lessens after ~7 days.
  • You may feel dizzy on standing — get up gradually; sit or lie down if dizzy.
  • Dry mouth, constipation, and blurred vision are common but usually lessen after about 7 days.
  • Do not stop suddenly — reduce gradually to avoid withdrawal effects.
  • Avoid alcohol — increases CNS depression.
  • May take 2–4 weeks to see full analgesic effect — do not assume treatment failure early.
SNRIs — Duloxetine & Venlafaxine XRAdjuvant Analgesic — Serotonin-Noradrenaline Reuptake Inhibitor
Doses (Adult)
  • Duloxetine: 30 mg PO mane initially (30 mg for first week ↓ nausea; suitable starting dose for elderly)Increase by 30 mg every 7 days as tolerated. Maximum: 120 mg/day. CrCl <30: 30 mg/day. Hepatic impairment: contraindicated.
  • Venlafaxine XR: 75 mg PO mane initially → increase to 150 mg if required; up to 225 mg in some patientsCrCl <30 or dialysis: halve dose. Hepatic impairment: halve initial dose, titrate carefully. Venlafaxine is toxic in overdose — prescribe carefully in at-risk patients.
Mechanism
Inhibit reuptake of serotonin AND noradrenaline → enhanced descending inhibitory pain pathways. Noradrenaline reuptake inhibition particularly important for analgesia. No anticholinergic adverse effects (advantage over TCAs). Dual analgesic + antidepressant benefit.
Duloxetine vs Venlafaxine
  • Duloxetine: established evidence in diabetic neuropathy; prefer if comorbid depression or obesity
  • Duloxetine: ↓ BP elevation risk vs venlafaxine; hepatic impairment — contraindicated
  • Venlafaxine: dose-dependent BP ↑ — monitor BP; toxic in overdose (prescribe cautiously in suicide risk patients)
  • Both: taper on cessation to avoid discontinuation syndrome ("brain zaps", dizziness, flu-like)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • MAOIs — contraindicated (serotonin toxicity); wait ≥14 days after stopping MAOI; wait 1–2 days after stopping SNRI before starting MAOI
  • Duloxetine — hepatic impairment: contraindicated
  • Duloxetine — strong CYP1A2 inhibitors (e.g. fluvoxamine): contraindicated
  • Uncontrolled hypertension — SNRIs ↑ BP; control BP before starting venlafaxine; monitor regularly
  • High bleeding risk or concurrent NSAIDs/aspirin — ↓ platelet aggregation; ↑ bleeding risk
  • Renal impairment — duloxetine: 30 mg/day if CrCl <30; venlafaxine: halve dose
  • Bipolar disorder — may provoke mania; angle-closure glaucoma risk
  • Risk of hyponatraemia — especially in elderly; check Na at baseline and early treatment
  • Do not stop suddenly — taper over weeks to months
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Nausea — most common; usually transient; take with food; 30 mg start ↓ risk
  • Dry mouth, constipation, yawning, sweating, decreased appetite, rash
  • Dizziness, headache, somnolence, insomnia, blurred vision, tremor, weakness
  • ↑ BP — venlafaxine (dose-dependent); duloxetine (less common)
  • Sexual dysfunction (impotence, ↓ libido)
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Orthostatic hypotension, palpitations, tachycardia
  • Abnormal LFTs, hyponatraemia (SIADH), urinary retention (duloxetine)
  • ECG changes (venlafaxine)
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Duloxetine: liver failure, GI bleeding, SJS, cutaneous vasculitis
  • Venlafaxine: serious bleeding, acute angle-closure glaucoma, hepatitis, myositis
  • Both: seizures, takotsubo cardiomyopathy, hyperprolactinaemia
Monitoring
  • BP before starting and regularly throughout treatment (especially venlafaxine)
  • Sodium at baseline and early in treatment — especially in elderly or at risk of hyponatraemia
  • Monitor suicidal ideation early in treatment, particularly in young people
Counselling
  • Take with food in the morning to reduce nausea — nausea usually improves after 1–2 weeks.
  • Do not open, crush or chew capsules — swallow whole.
  • Do not stop suddenly — always reduce gradually as directed (discontinuation syndrome).
  • Report unusual bleeding, significant mood or BP changes.
  • Be careful with driving and operating machinery until you know how this medicine affects you.
  • Allow 2–4 weeks to assess full analgesic effect.
Lidocaine 5% PatchTopical Local Anaesthetic — Localised Neuropathic Pain
Dose (Adult)
  • Up to 3 patches over the most painful area
  • Wear for up to 12 hours, then patch-free interval
  • Extended wear if pain returns during patch-free interval and skin remains intact — avoid continuous use
Mechanism
Blocks sodium channels in peripheral nociceptors → ↓ ectopic neuronal discharge → ↓ peripheral pain signal generation. Acts locally with minimal systemic absorption — avoids systemic adverse effects.
Best Indications
  • Postherpetic neuralgia (localised)
  • Nerve entrapment syndromes
  • Localised allodynia or superficial neuropathic pain
  • When systemic adjuvants are not tolerated or contraindicated
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Local skin reactions — erythema, rash, burning, pruritus at application site
Rare
  • Systemic lidocaine toxicity — if applied to broken/inflamed skin or misused (↑ absorption)
Counselling
  • Apply only to intact, dry, non-irritated skin over the most painful area.
  • Remove after 12 hours — allow a patch-free period before reapplying.
  • Wash hands after handling. Do not cut patches.
  • Do not apply to broken or inflamed skin.
Opioids in Neuropathic PainReserve — When Adjuvants Insufficient
🚨
Opioids are generally reserved for neuropathic pain that has not responded adequately to adjuvants, or for severe pain requiring rapid relief. May be effective at higher doses if adverse effects can be tolerated. Seek specialist advice before initiation.
Key Principles
  • Trial adjuvants adequately before considering opioids
  • Use lowest effective dose; modified-release preferred for chronic use
  • Review regularly — benefit must be maintained (≥30% pain reduction + improved function)
  • Short-acting opioids may be considered for episodic severe neuropathic pain flares
  • Monitor for opioid misuse, tolerance, and dependence
  • Avoid combining with benzodiazepines — markedly ↑ respiratory depression death risk
Tramadol — Special Note
  • Weak opioid agonist + noradrenaline/serotonin reuptake inhibitor — dual mechanism may offer benefit in neuropathic pain
  • Lowers seizure threshold — caution in epilepsy
  • CYP2D6 genetic variation affects conversion to active metabolite — variable response
CarbamazepineSodium Channel Blocker — Trigeminal Neuralgia / Neuropathic Pain
Dose (Trigeminal Neuralgia)
  • Initially 50–100 mg PO BD → increase gradually if necessary to 400–800 mg/day in 2–4 dosesUp to 1.2 g/day may be required. Increase dose slowly to allow for enzyme autoinduction (takes 2–4 weeks for steady-state).
  • Elderly/debilitated: start 50 mg BD, increase slowly
  • Controlled-release tablets reduce peak concentration-related adverse effects (dizziness, blurred vision) — give BD
Mechanism of Action
Prevents repetitive neuronal discharges by blocking voltage-dependent and use-dependent sodium channels → ↓ ectopic discharge in trigeminal nerve fibres → ↓ neuralgic pain episodes. First-line for trigeminal neuralgia.
Concentration Monitoring
  • Therapeutic range: 4–12 mg/L (17–50 micromol/L) — measured as trough (pre-dose)
  • Check CBC before treatment and periodically — stop if significant bone marrow depression
  • Monitor liver enzymes
Precautions & Contraindications
  • AV conduction abnormalities — contraindicated
  • History of bone marrow depression — contraindicated
  • Porphyria — contraindicated
  • HLA-B*1502 allele (Asian ancestry, especially Han Chinese, Filipino, Thai, Malay, Vietnamese) — strongly ↑ risk of SJS/TEN; screen before starting if possible; avoid if positive
  • HLA-A*3101 allele (Japanese, Korean, northern European) — ↑ risk of skin reactions and multi-organ hypersensitivity; consider screening
  • Prior hypersensitivity to carbamazepine, phenytoin, phenobarbital, or oxcarbazepine — high cross-sensitivity; avoid
  • Many significant drug interactions — potent CYP3A4 inducer; increases metabolism of many drugs including oral contraceptives; check all medications and grapefruit juice
  • Absence and myoclonic seizures — lack of efficacy; ↑ seizure risk
  • Hepatic impairment — avoid in severe impairment
  • Women: ensure effective contraception (e.g. IUD) during treatment and 1 month after
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Drowsiness, ataxia, dizziness, blurred vision, diplopia, headache — all dose-related
  • GI: abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, anorexia, diarrhoea, constipation, dry mouth
  • Rash — usually transient; may be serious
  • Asymptomatic hyponatraemia, leucopenia, thrombocytopenia, ↑ liver enzymes
Rare / Serious
  • Severe skin reactions — SJS, TEN, exfoliative dermatitis (mostly within first few months; HLA-B*1502 increases risk)
  • Multi-organ hypersensitivity syndrome (DRESS) — fever, rash, lymphadenopathy, eosinophilia, organ involvement; usually 2–6 weeks after starting; stop drug immediately
  • Agranulocytosis, aplastic anaemia — stop if significant bone marrow depression
  • Hepatotoxicity, jaundice, arrhythmia, SIADH
Monitoring
  • CBC before treatment and periodically — stop if significant bone marrow depression
  • LFTs — periodically
  • Serum carbamazepine levels: trough 4–12 mg/L
  • Skin: monitor for rash at every visit — most transient but some life-threatening
  • Sodium — hyponatraemia is common; monitor periodically
  • Consider BMD monitoring and Vitamin D/calcium supplementation with long-term use
Counselling
  • Take with food to prevent stomach upset.
  • Controlled-release tablets: swallow whole — do not crush or chew.
  • This medicine interacts with grapefruit juice and many other drugs — avoid grapefruit juice and tell all doctors and pharmacists you are taking carbamazepine before any new medication.
  • Tell your doctor immediately if: rash, sore throat, fever, mouth ulcers, bruising, or bleeding occur.
  • May cause drowsiness and dizziness especially at the start — do not drive or operate machinery until you know how you react.
  • Do not stop suddenly without medical advice.
  • Women: use effective contraception — carbamazepine reduces effectiveness of the pill; use a barrier method or IUD.

Neuropathic Pain

Pain arising from lesion or disease affecting the somatosensory nervous system — characterised by altered sensation, sensitisation, and neuralgia. Adjuvants are the mainstay of drug therapy.

🔬

Pathophysiology

⚡ Peripheral Mechanisms
  • Nerve injury → changes in ion channel expression and function
  • ↑ excitability of peripheral nociceptors (peripheral sensitisation)
  • Ectopic discharge from damaged nerve fibres
  • Altered sodium channel activity → spontaneous firing
  • Loss of inhibitory interneurons at the site of injury
🧠 Central Mechanisms
  • Central sensitisation — spinal cord becomes hyperexcitable
  • Wind-up: repeated C-fibre stimulation → progressively amplified pain responses
  • Loss of descending inhibitory control
  • Glial cell activation → inflammatory mediator release in CNS
  • Structural changes in the CNS with chronic neuropathic pain
🧩 Sensitisation Phenomena
  • Allodynia: pain from normally non-painful stimulus (e.g. light touch, clothing)
  • Hyperalgesia: exaggerated pain response to a normally painful stimulus
  • Dysaesthesia: spontaneous or evoked unpleasant abnormal sensations
  • Spontaneous pain: occurs without any triggering stimulus
⚠️

Causes & Clinical Examples

CategoryExamples

🩺

Clinical Features

🔥 Pain Descriptors
  • Burning, electric shocks, stabbing, shooting
  • Pins and needles, tingling, crawling
  • Hot-cold sensations
  • Often described as "different" from ordinary pain
🌡️ Sensory Abnormalities
  • Allodynia — pain from light touch, clothing, temperature
  • Hyperalgesia — exaggerated pain from normally painful stimuli
  • Hypoaesthesia — reduced sensation in the painful area
  • Motor or sensory deficits in the painful area
🔎 Autonomic Features
  • Skin colour changes
  • Temperature asymmetry
  • Altered sweating
  • Trophic changes (skin, hair, nails)
  • Oedema (especially CRPS)
🔍

Diagnosis & Assessment

Neuropathic pain is a clinical diagnosis based on history and examination.

Assessment findings suggesting neuropathic component:
  • Pain described as electric shocks, burning, or pins and needles
  • Allodynia or hyperalgesia identified on examination
  • Motor or sensory deficits in the painful area
  • Area of pain consistent with the innervation of the affected neural structure

Screening tools: PainDETECT questionnaire can help identify pain likely to have a neuropathic component.

Imaging: MRI can sometimes identify cause, but should only be performed if it will change management — do not image solely to confirm diagnosis.

Surgery: Generally ineffective for neuropathic pain (exception: trigeminal neuralgia and radicular pain from nerve root compression). In the medium to long term, surgery may worsen symptoms.
🎯

Treatment Goals

Primary
  • Reduce pain intensity to a tolerable level
  • Restore function and quality of life
  • Improve sleep
Realistic Expectations
  • Complete pain elimination is rarely achievable
  • A ≥30–50% reduction in pain is considered meaningful
  • Function and quality of life improvements are co-equal targets
Non-Pharmacological (All Patients)
  • Physical activity and pacing
  • Psychological techniques (CBT, mindfulness)
  • Social reconnection and support
  • Sleep improvement
⚠️
Analgesics are only partially effective in neuropathic pain — adjuvants are the mainstay of drug therapy, not opioids. Drug therapy should not be used in children without specialist advice.

💡
Invasive procedures (nerve blocks, radiofrequency ablation, spinal cord stimulators) may be considered in carefully selected patients after specialist review — not first-line.

Drug Summaries

Gabapentinoids — Gabapentin & PregabalinAdjuvant Analgesic — α2δ Calcium Channel Ligand
Doses (Adult — Neuropathic Pain)
  • Gabapentin — initial: 100–300 mg PO once daily (nocte preferred)Titrate every 3–7 days: once daily → BD → TDS. Maximum: 3600 mg/24h. Frail/>70 years: max 900 mg/24h.
  • Pregabalin — initial: 25–75 mg PO in early eveningIncrease to BD after 3–7 days; titrate slowly. Maximum: 600 mg/24h. Frail/>70 years: max 300 mg/24h.
  • Largest dose at night for both agents. Switch between agents if one ineffective — individual response may differ.
Mechanism of Action
Bind presynaptic α2-δ subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels → ↓ calcium influx → ↓ release of excitatory neurotransmitters (glutamate, substance P, noradrenaline) from hyperexcited nociceptors → ↓ pain signal amplification. Also modulate descending inhibitory pain pathways.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • CNS depressants, opioids, alcohol — additive sedation and respiratory depression risk
  • Renal impairment — both drugs are renally excreted; dose reduction required; check eGFR
  • Elderly/frail — ↑ falls and cognitive impairment risk; lower doses, slower titration
  • Dependence and misuse potential — assess risk; do not stop abruptly
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Dizziness, sedation, fatigue — especially at initiation and during titration
  • Peripheral oedema
  • Blurred vision, diplopia
  • Nausea, weight gain (more common with pregabalin)
  • Ataxia, incoordination
Deprescribing
  • <3 months: reduce by 25–30% per week
  • >3 months: reduce by 25–30% every 2 weeks
Counselling
  • Take the largest dose at night — reduces daytime sedation; this is expected and usually improves over time.
  • Do not stop suddenly — taper gradually to avoid withdrawal (seizure risk, anxiety, insomnia).
  • May take several weeks to see full effect.
  • Avoid alcohol and other sedating medications.
  • Take care with driving at initiation and after dose changes.
TCAs — Amitriptyline & NortriptylineAdjuvant Analgesic — Tricyclic Antidepressant
Doses (Adult — Analgesic Use)
  • Amitriptyline: 5–12.5 mg PO nocte initially → ↑ every 7 daysDiscontinue if no benefit by 50 mg. If benefit → titrate up to 75–150 mg nocte.
  • Nortriptyline: 5–12.5 mg PO nocte initially → titrate weekly → up to 150 mg nocte
  • Older/frail patients: lower starting dose, slower titration
  • Note: analgesic doses are much lower than antidepressant doses
Mechanism of Action
Inhibit reuptake of noradrenaline and serotonin → enhance descending inhibitory pain pathways. Also have sodium channel blocking and NMDA antagonist activity contributing to analgesia. Note: antidepressant doses are not required for analgesia.
Nortriptyline vs Amitriptyline
  • Nortriptyline is the active metabolite of amitriptyline
  • Nortriptyline has fewer anticholinergic effects — preferred in elderly patients
  • Both equally effective for neuropathic pain at analgesic doses
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Cardiac arrhythmia, recent MI — contraindicated
  • MAOIs — contraindicated (serotonin syndrome)
  • Elderly — ↑ falls risk, confusion, anticholinergic adverse effects; use nortriptyline (lower anticholinergic load)
  • BPH, narrow-angle glaucoma — may worsen (anticholinergic)
  • QT prolongation — monitor ECG at higher doses or in at-risk patients
  • Epilepsy — lower seizure threshold; caution
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Sedation — take at night; generally diminishes over 1–2 weeks
  • Dry mouth, constipation, urinary hesitancy (anticholinergic)
  • Blurred vision
  • Weight gain, increased appetite
  • Orthostatic hypotension — ↑ falls risk especially in elderly
Rare / Serious
  • Cardiac arrhythmia, QT prolongation — at higher doses
Counselling
  • Take at night — drowsiness is expected and actually helpful for sleep.
  • Avoid driving or hazardous tasks until you know how this medication affects you.
  • Dry mouth and constipation are common — stay hydrated, eat fibre, use saliva substitutes if needed.
  • Do not stop suddenly — reduce gradually.
  • May take 2–4 weeks to notice full analgesic effect.
SNRIs — Duloxetine & Venlafaxine XRAdjuvant Analgesic — Serotonin-Noradrenaline Reuptake Inhibitor
Doses (Adult)
  • Duloxetine: 30 mg PO mane initially → ↑ by 30 mg every 7 days → up to 120 mg mane
  • Venlafaxine XR: 37.5–75 mg PO mane initially → ↑ every 7 days → up to 225 mg mane
Mechanism
Inhibit reuptake of serotonin AND noradrenaline → enhanced descending inhibitory pain pathways. Noradrenaline reuptake inhibition is particularly important for analgesic effect. No anticholinergic adverse effects.
Advantages Over TCAs
  • No anticholinergic adverse effects
  • Safer in cardiac disease
  • Dual benefit: pain + comorbid depression/anxiety
  • Preferred in obesity (less weight gain)
Precautions & Contraindications
  • MAOIs — contraindicated (serotonin syndrome)
  • Hepatic impairment — duloxetine is hepatically metabolised; use with caution
  • Renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min) — reduce dose or avoid
  • Hypertension — venlafaxine ↑ BP dose-dependently; monitor BP regularly
  • Bleeding risk — ↓ platelet aggregation; caution with anticoagulants/NSAIDs
  • Do not stop suddenly — taper to avoid discontinuation syndrome (dizziness, "brain zaps", flu-like)
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Nausea — usually transient (1–2 weeks); take with food
  • Insomnia, dry mouth, sweating, headache
  • ↑ BP (especially venlafaxine) — monitor
  • Sexual dysfunction
Counselling
  • Take in the morning with food to minimise nausea.
  • Do not stop suddenly — always reduce gradually as directed.
  • Report unusual bleeding, significant BP changes, or mood changes.
  • Allow 2–4 weeks to assess full analgesic effect.
Lidocaine 5% PatchTopical Local Anaesthetic — Localised Neuropathic Pain
Dose (Adult)
  • Up to 3 patches over the most painful area
  • Wear for up to 12 hours, then patch-free interval
  • Extended wear if pain returns during patch-free interval and skin remains intact — avoid continuous use
Mechanism
Blocks sodium channels in peripheral nociceptors → ↓ ectopic neuronal discharge → ↓ peripheral pain signal generation. Acts locally with minimal systemic absorption — avoids systemic adverse effects.
Best Indications
  • Postherpetic neuralgia (localised)
  • Nerve entrapment syndromes
  • Localised allodynia or superficial neuropathic pain
  • When systemic adjuvants are not tolerated or contraindicated
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Local skin reactions — erythema, rash, burning, pruritus at application site
Rare
  • Systemic lidocaine toxicity — if applied to broken/inflamed skin or misused (↑ absorption)
Counselling
  • Apply only to intact, dry, non-irritated skin over the most painful area.
  • Remove after 12 hours — allow a patch-free period before reapplying.
  • Wash hands after handling. Do not cut patches.
  • Do not apply to broken or inflamed skin.
Opioids in Neuropathic PainReserve — When Adjuvants Insufficient
🚨
Opioids are generally reserved for neuropathic pain that has not responded adequately to adjuvants, or for severe pain requiring rapid relief. May be effective at higher doses if adverse effects can be tolerated. Seek specialist advice before initiation.
Key Principles
  • Trial adjuvants adequately before considering opioids
  • Use lowest effective dose; modified-release preferred for chronic use
  • Review regularly — benefit must be maintained (≥30% pain reduction + improved function)
  • Short-acting opioids may be considered for episodic severe neuropathic pain flares
  • Monitor for opioid misuse, tolerance, and dependence
  • Avoid combining with benzodiazepines — markedly ↑ respiratory depression death risk
Tramadol — Special Note
  • Weak opioid agonist + noradrenaline/serotonin reuptake inhibitor — dual mechanism may offer benefit in neuropathic pain
  • Lowers seizure threshold — caution in epilepsy
  • CYP2D6 genetic variation affects conversion to active metabolite — variable response

Osteoporosis

Metabolic bone disorder characterised by low BMD and microarchitectural deterioration → fragility fractures. Often silent until fracture. Up to 80% remain undiagnosed.

🔬

Pathophysiology

Core process: Osteoclast-mediated bone resorption exceeds osteoblast-mediated bone formation → net bone mineral density (BMD) loss → structural weakening → fracture risk.
Ageing → reduced osteoblast activity → declining BMD (senile osteoporosis)
Postmenopausal estrogen deficiency → ↓ estrogen-mediated osteoclast suppression → ↑ osteoclast activity → accelerated bone loss
Ca²⁺ deficiency → ↑ PTH release → bone resorption to restore serum Ca²⁺
Vitamin D deficiency → ↓ gut Ca²⁺ absorption → ↑ PTH release → bone resorption to restore serum Ca²⁺
↓ OPG / ↑ RANKL activation (OPG normally inhibits RANKL) → ↑ osteoclast formation and activation → ↑ bone resorption
Low peak bone mass (achieved ~age 20–29) → less bone reserve to lose before reaching fracture-risk threshold
Inactivity / less mechanical loading → ↓ osteoblast stimulation → less new bone formation
💡
Bone remodelling cycle: Trabecular bone replaced every 3–4 years; cortical bone every 10 years. Structural changes in osteoporosis: fewer trabeculae, thinning cortex, wider Haversian canals → ↓ strength → ↑ fracture risk. Antiresorptive drugs slow osteoclast-mediated loss; anabolic drugs increase new bone formation.
⚠️

Risk Factors

🔒 Non-Modifiable
  • Increasing age
  • Female sex
  • Family history of osteoporosis or fracture
  • Prior fragility fracture
  • Early menopause or late menarche
  • Age at menopause
✏️ Modifiable
  • Smoking
  • High alcohol intake
  • Low calcium intake
  • Vitamin D deficiency
  • Low body weight / weight loss
  • Low protein intake
  • Sedentary lifestyle / physical inactivity / immobilisation
  • Low sex hormone levels
  • Malabsorption
  • Recurrent falls
💊 Drugs That ↓ BMD
  • Glucocorticoids — most common drug cause; ↓ Ca absorption, ↓ osteoblast function, ↑ resorption
  • Aromatase inhibitors (breast cancer)
  • Androgen deprivation therapy (prostate cancer)
  • Excessive thyroid hormone replacement
  • Long-term heparin
  • SSRIs
  • Antiepileptic drugs (esp. hepatic enzyme inducers)
  • Thiazolidinediones
  • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)
🏥 Associated Conditions
  • Hyperthyroidism, hyperparathyroidism
  • Diabetes, Cushing syndrome
  • Sex hormone deficiency
  • Coeliac disease, gastric/bowel resection, bariatric surgery
  • Liver disease, CKD, cardiopulmonary disease
  • RA and connective tissue disease
  • Multiple myeloma, haematological disorders, metastatic cancer
  • Anorexia nervosa / low body weight
⚠️
Falls risk factors also important (falls → fractures): Visual impairment; sedating medications; antihypertensives causing postural hypotension; poor balance/mobility.
🩺

Clinical Features

⚠️
Osteoporosis is silent — no pain or obvious symptoms until fracture occurs. Up to 70% of vertebral fractures are painless.
🦴 Typical Fracture Sites
  • Vertebral compression fractures (most common — often asymptomatic)
  • Proximal femur (hip fractures — most morbid)
  • Distal radius (Colles' fracture — wrist)
  • Proximal humerus
  • Pelvis
📏 Features That May Suggest Vertebral Fractures
  • Height loss ≥3 cm
  • Kyphosis (hunched posture)
  • Back pain (may be chronic)
  • Restricted mobility
⚡ Consequences if Untreated
  • Progressive vertebral compression fractures
  • Hip fractures (↑ mortality, loss of independence)
  • Deformity, chronic pain
  • Loss of function, disability
  • Loss of independence
🔍 Differentiating from Other Bone Conditions
  • Osteomalacia: ↓ mineralisation (not structural); normal bone quantity but poor quality; ALP often ↑; low Ca, low phosphate, very low Vit D; bone pain more prominent
  • Paget disease: High bone turnover, ALP markedly elevated; focal disease; bone enlargement/deformity
  • Myeloma: Lytic lesions; paraprotein on electrophoresis; elevated ESR; consider in fracture without adequate trauma, especially in older adults
🔍

Diagnosis & Investigations

🥇
Gold standard: DXA (Dual-Energy X-ray Absorptiometry) scan of hip and lumbar spine. Measures BMD. Preferred site: total hip (spine BMD can be falsely elevated by OA, arterial calcification, or vertebral fractures).
T-ScoreClassificationFracture Risk Implication
–1.0 or higherNormal BMDNormal
–1.0 to –2.5Osteopenia (low bone density)Elevated but below osteoporosis threshold
–2.5 or lowerOsteoporosisFracture risk approximately doubles per 1 SD ↓ in T-score
Z-score compares BMD to age- and sex-matched mean. Z-score below –2.0 → BMD below expected range → investigate for secondary causes. Use Z-scores for: premenopausal women, men <50 years, children.

Bone turnover markers: CTX (resorption), uNTx/Cr (resorption), P1NP (formation) — mainly for monitoring treatment response, not primary diagnosis.
Investigations for secondary causes:
Routine: serum Ca, phosphate, ALP, 25-hydroxyvitamin D, TSH, liver biochemistry, renal function If secondary cause suspected: PTH, urinary Ca, protein electrophoresis, ESR, coeliac serology, testosterone (men), FSH + estradiol (women), 24h urinary cortisol / dexamethasone suppression test
🎯

Treatment Goals

Primary
  • Reduce fragility fracture risk
  • Strengthen the skeleton
  • Reduce falls risk
Secondary
  • Improve or preserve BMD
  • Reduce further bone loss
  • Ensure adequate Ca + Vit D substrate
💡
Non-pharmacological (essential for all): Adequate dietary calcium (800–1200 mg/day); adequate vitamin D (target serum 25-OHD >50 nmol/L); weight-bearing and resistance exercise; balance/mobility training if fall risk; smoking cessation; limit alcohol; manage secondary causes; minimise corticosteroid exposure; falls prevention (vision, medications review, home hazards, walking aids).
💊

Pharmacological Treatment Ladder

📋
When to start drug therapy:
• MTF (minimal trauma fracture) with T-score in osteopenic or osteoporotic range → start treatment
• Hip fracture even if T-score is normal → start treatment
• T-score ≤–2.5 AND age >75 → start treatment
• Glucocorticoids ≥7.5 mg prednisolone/day for ≥3 months AND baseline T-score <–1.5 → consider preventive therapy
• BMD declining in younger patients with concerning fracture risk → specialist decision
Note: Ca/Vit D supplementation alone are NOT stand-alone osteoporosis drugs — they provide substrate for mineralisation and reduce treatment-related hypocalcaemia.
Calcium Supplementation (if dietary intake inadequate):
Calcium carbonate 1.25–1.5 g PO daily with food (500–600 mg elemental Ca) — First-line; requires gastric acidity for absorption (not ideal with PPIs) OR Calcium citrate 2.38 g PO daily (500 mg elemental Ca) — Better absorbed with ↓ gastric acidity (PPI users) Limit single doses to 500–600 mg elemental Ca. Total >1500 mg/day → ↑ renal stones + possible CV events.
Vitamin D Supplementation (when 25-OHD <50 nmol/L):
Mild (30–49 nmol/L): Colecalciferol 1000–2000 IU (25–50 mcg) PO daily OR Colecalciferol 7000–14000 IU PO weekly Moderate-severe (<30 nmol/L): Colecalciferol 3000–5000 IU PO daily for 6–12 weeks → then maintenance Do NOT exceed 4000 IU daily (associated with ↑ falls/fractures); intermittent very high doses also ↑ risk

First-Line Antiresorptive Therapy
Bisphosphonates (Oral or IV)

Most effective, well-evidenced first-line agents. Reduce osteoclast-mediated bone loss. Choice between oral and IV depends on GI tolerability, adherence, and renal function. Always ensure adequate Ca + Vit D before/during bisphosphonate therapy.

Alendronate (Oral)70 mg PO once weekly, fasting
✅ Oral, affordable, widely used first-line ✅ BMD benefits persist several years after stopping ❌ Upper GI irritation / dysphagia — avoid if history of oesophageal stricture, Barrett's, significant GI disease ❌ Severe renal impairment (CrCl <35 mL/min) — avoid ❌ Absorption reduced by Ca, Mg, Fe, antacids — must separate by ≥30 min
Risedronate (Oral)35 mg PO once weekly OR 150 mg once monthly, fasting
✅ EC formulation may have lower GI effects; less affected by food/antacids ✅ Monthly option improves adherence vs weekly ❌ BMD decreases within 1 year of stopping (unlike alendronate and zoledronic acid) ❌ Severe renal impairment (CrCl <35 mL/min) — avoid
Zoledronic Acid (IV)5 mg IV over ≥15 minutes, once yearly
Pre-treatment requirements (CHECK ALL before each dose):
• 25-OHD >50 nmol/L  |  Corrected calcium 2.10–2.60 mmol/L  |  eGFR >35 mL/min  |  Patient well hydrated
✅ No GI effects — ideal when oral bisphosphonates not tolerated ✅ Yearly dosing → best adherence; BMD benefits persist years after stopping ✅ Most effective bisphosphonate for Paget disease (single dose achieves remission) ❌ IV not acceptable for all patients ❌ eGFR <35 mL/min — avoid ❌ Uncorrected hypocalcaemia — contraindicated (especially if eGFR <35, Vit D deficiency, malabsorption) ⚠️ Transient flu-like symptoms after first dose (common; treat with paracetamol) 🔵 Monitor: renal function, corrected Ca, 25-OHD before EACH dose; Ca + Vit D supplementation during treatment
⏱️
Duration of bisphosphonate therapy:
Standard: Oral → 5 years; IV → 3 years (then consider stopping if not high risk)
High-risk patients (age ≥75, previous hip/vertebral fracture, fracture after starting therapy, hip T-score ≤–2.5): Continue up to 10 years oral or 6 years IV. Specialist for >10 years.
If bisphosphonates not tolerated or contraindicated → Use Denosumab (alternative first-line)

🦷
Osteonecrosis of the Jaw (ONJ/MRONJ) — associated with bisphosphonates AND denosumab (possibly romosozumab): Risk is RARE in osteoporosis treatment. Risk ↑ after dental extractions/implants, periodontal disease, smoking, glucocorticoids, immunocompromise, diabetes, dialysis, prolonged bisphosphonate use. Prevention: Dental review before starting; good oral hygiene; treat dental problems early. Do NOT interrupt denosumab for dental work — rapid bone loss risk. Inform dentist you are on antiresorptive therapy.
🦴
Atypical Femoral Fractures (AFF): Rare; associated with prolonged bisphosphonate/denosumab therapy. Prodromal thigh/groin/hip pain in ~70%. Can be bilateral — image BOTH femurs. If unexplained thigh/groin pain during therapy: femoral shaft X-ray → consider MRI/bone scan → if confirmed, stop therapy and urgently refer.
📊

Monitoring Osteoporosis Treatment

  • Repeat DXA at lumbar spine and hip: 2 years after starting treatment; 1–2 years after a significant treatment change
  • More frequent BMD measurement in: younger patients; patients on high-dose glucocorticoids
  • Annual BMD if on high-dose glucocorticoid therapy
  • If BMD decreases by >5% at any site OR fracture occurs → investigate new risk factors, check adherence, then consider changing therapy
Disease monitoring sequence:
1. Confirm adequate Ca + Vit D (serum 25-OHD >50 nmol/L; dietary Ca 800–1200 mg/day)
2. Bone turnover markers (CTX, P1NP) — can confirm treatment response earlier than DXA
3. DXA at 2 years — if BMD ↑ or stable → continue. If ↓ → investigate adherence and secondary causes first.

Drug Summaries

Bisphosphonates (Alendronate, Risedronate, Zoledronic Acid)Antiresorptive — Osteoclast Inhibitor
Doses
  • Alendronate: 70 mg PO once weekly — empty stomach, full glass water, remain upright 30 min after
  • Risedronate: 35 mg PO once weekly OR 150 mg once monthly — empty stomach (EC formulation may be taken any time with water)
  • Zoledronic acid: 5 mg IV over ≥15 minutes once yearly(Ca + Vit D supplementation for ≥10 days post-infusion in Paget disease; ongoing in osteoporosis)
Mechanism of Action
Bind hydroxyapatite in bone → internalized by osteoclasts → inhibit osteoclast enzyme (farnesyl pyrophosphate synthase in nitrogen-containing bisphosphonates) → ↓ osteoclast function and survival → ↓ bone resorption → ↑ BMD. Long half-life in bone — beneficial effects persist years after stopping.
Indications
  • Osteoporosis — first-line antiresorptive
  • Paget disease of bone (zoledronic acid — treatment of choice)
  • Glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis
  • Hypercalcaemia of malignancy
  • Prevention of skeletal-related events in malignancy involving bone
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Hypocalcaemia — contraindicated (correct before treatment)
  • Severe renal impairment (CrCl <35 mL/min) — avoid
  • Oral bisphosphonates: significant upper GI disease, oesophageal stricture — avoid
  • Dental surgery — ideally complete invasive dental work before starting; inform dentist of bisphosphonate use
  • Women planning pregnancy — long half-life; specialist advice re: timing
Adverse Effects
Common — Oral
  • Nausea, dyspepsia, diarrhoea, headache
  • Oesophagitis, oesophageal erosions/ulcers (if not taken correctly)
  • Musculoskeletal pain
Common — IV (Zoledronic Acid)
  • Transient flu-like illness (fever, myalgia, bone pain) — especially first dose; treat with paracetamol
  • ↑ Creatinine (transient)
  • Hypocalcaemia (especially if deficient in Vit D/Ca)
Rare / Serious
  • Osteonecrosis of the jaw (ONJ/MRONJ)
  • Atypical femoral fracture (prolonged use)
  • Acute renal failure (IV — esp. if infused too quickly or dehydrated)
  • Ocular inflammation (uveitis, scleritis)
  • Severe musculoskeletal pain
  • Severe skin reactions, angioedema
Monitoring
  • Ensure adequate Ca + Vit D before and during treatment
  • Renal function before prescribing (especially IV)
  • Corrected Ca and 25-OHD before each IV dose
  • Dental review before starting antiresorptive therapy where possible
  • Review duration of therapy (see stopping criteria above)
  • Watch for thigh/groin pain during prolonged use (atypical fracture)
Counselling
  • Oral (alendronate/risedronate): Take on an empty stomach with a full glass of plain water. Do NOT eat, drink, or take other medications for 30 minutes. Remain UPRIGHT (sitting or standing) for at least 30 minutes after taking — do not lie down.
  • Separate from calcium supplements, iron, magnesium, antacids by at least 30 minutes (ideally longer).
  • Ensure adequate vitamin D and calcium intake daily.
  • Zoledronic acid (IV): Drink at least 2 glasses of water before and after infusion. Flu-like symptoms may occur in the days after — paracetamol can help.
  • Tell your doctor and dentist you are taking a bisphosphonate before any dental work.
  • Report: severe jaw pain, loose teeth, exposed bone in mouth (ONJ); unexplained thigh or hip pain (atypical fracture); severe bone/joint/muscle pain; pain on swallowing (oral).
  • Regular dental check-ups and good oral hygiene are important.
DenosumabAntiresorptive — Anti-RANKL Monoclonal Antibody
Dose
  • 60 mg SC every 6 months
  • Pre-dose: confirm 25-OHD >50 nmol/L, corrected Ca 2.10–2.60 mmol/L, eGFR >35 mL/min(if eGFR <35 or dialysis: ↑ hypocalcaemia risk — monitor closely)
  • Therapy must be continued indefinitely OR followed by bisphosphonate if stopped
Mechanism of Action
Human monoclonal antibody → binds and inhibits RANKL → prevents RANKL from activating RANK on osteoclast precursors → ↓ osteoclast formation, function, and survival → ↓ bone resorption → ↑ BMD. Effects are rapidly reversible on stopping — unlike bisphosphonates.
Indications
  • Postmenopausal osteoporosis (when oral therapy unsuitable)
  • Osteoporosis in men
  • Androgen deprivation therapy / aromatase inhibitor bone loss
  • Glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Hypocalcaemia — contraindicated (correct before dosing)
  • Pregnancy — contraindicated (contraception required during and ≥5 months after)
  • Stopping without follow-on antiresorptive → rapid rebound bone loss + vertebral fracture risk
  • CrCl <30 mL/min or dialysis — ↑ hypocalcaemia risk; closely monitor Ca
  • Separate from IV iron by ≥4 weeks (severe hypophosphataemia risk)
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Eczema, hypercholesterolaemia, musculoskeletal pain
Rare / Serious
  • Hypocalcaemia (especially with renal impairment) — monitor Ca before each dose
  • ONJ / MRONJ
  • Atypical femoral fracture
  • Hypersensitivity / anaphylaxis
  • Vasculitis, lichen planus
Counselling
  • Do NOT delay or miss doses — injections must be given every 6 months (±2 weeks). Delay >4 weeks can cause rapid bone loss and increases risk of vertebral fractures.
  • This is a long-term (often indefinite) treatment. If your doctor decides to stop it, you MUST transition to a bisphosphonate to prevent rebound bone loss.
  • Tell your dentist you are on denosumab before any dental work.
  • Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D daily.
  • Report immediately: muscle spasms, cramps, tingling/numbness in fingers/toes or around mouth (low calcium signs).
  • Ensure adequate sun exposure for vitamin D or take supplements.
TeriparatideAnabolic — PTH Analogue (Active Fragment)
Dose
  • 20 mcg SC daily (thigh or abdomen)
  • Lifetime maximum duration: 24 months
  • → Follow IMMEDIATELY with antiresorptive therapy after completing course
Mechanism of Action
Active fragment of human PTH → intermittent low-dose exposure preferentially stimulates osteoblast activity more than osteoclast activity → net positive bone formation effect → ↑ BMD. (Continuous PTH → ↑ resorption; intermittent → ↑ formation.)
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Nausea, headache, dizziness, muscle cramps
  • Arthralgia, injection site reactions
  • Hyperuricaemia
Infrequent
  • Hypercalcaemia (check Ca before each injection — draw blood just before injection)
Rare
  • Anaphylaxis / allergic reactions
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Hyperparathyroidism — contraindicated
  • Hypercalcaemia — contraindicated
  • Paget disease, prior skeletal radiation, unexplained raised ALP — contraindicated
  • Skeletal malignancy — contraindicated
  • Age <25 years / open epiphyses — contraindicated
  • Severe renal disease — contraindicated
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding — contraindicated
  • Urolithiasis — caution
  • Do not switch from denosumab to teriparatide — transitional bone loss risk
Monitoring
  • Serum Ca: draw blood just BEFORE each injection (drug causes transient post-injection hypercalcaemia)
  • Ensure adequate Ca + Vit D intake (500 mg elemental Ca + 400–1200 IU Vit D daily)
  • DXA at 2 years; immediately commence antiresorptive therapy after 24 months
Counselling
  • Inject subcutaneously into the thigh or abdomen — rotate injection sites.
  • This treatment has a lifetime maximum of 24 months. After completing the course, you MUST start a different bone-strengthening medication immediately.
  • Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D daily.
  • Report: nausea, vomiting, constipation, thirst, frequent urination, tiredness, or headache (possible hypercalcaemia).
  • Obtain informed consent after fully explaining the 24-month lifetime maximum duration.
RomosozumabAnabolic — Anti-Sclerostin Monoclonal Antibody
Dose
  • 210 mg SC monthly for 12 months(given as 2 consecutive 105 mg injections at different sites)
  • Lifetime maximum: 12 months
  • → Follow IMMEDIATELY with antiresorptive therapy
Mechanism of Action
Binds and inhibits sclerostin (normally inhibits WNT pathway → ↓ osteoblast activity) → WNT pathway activation → ↑ bone formation + ↓ bone resorption. Dual mechanism — uniquely both anabolic and antiresorptive.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • MI or stroke history — contraindicated (↑ CV events in trials)
  • Hypocalcaemia — contraindicated (correct before dosing)
  • Other CV risk factors — use with caution; stop if CV event occurs during treatment
  • eGFR <30 or dialysis — ↑ hypocalcaemia risk
Adverse Effects
Common
  • Injection site reactions, arthralgia, rash
Infrequent
  • Hypocalcaemia, urticaria, cataract
  • Cardiovascular events
Rare
  • Atypical femoral fracture, ONJ, angioedema, erythema multiforme
Counselling
  • Injected under the skin into thigh, upper arm, or abdomen.
  • This treatment lasts 12 months only. After completing, you MUST start a bisphosphonate or denosumab immediately — the bone benefits do NOT persist after stopping.
  • Report symptoms of cardiovascular events (chest pain, stroke symptoms) or hypocalcaemia (tingling, cramps, spasms) urgently.
  • Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D daily.
Estrogen Therapy, Raloxifene & TiboloneHormonal Antiresorptive / SERM
Estrogen Therapy — Dose
  • Oral estradiol / estradiol valerate 2 mg PO daily
  • OR Transdermal estradiol 50 mcg/24h(Women with intact uterus MUST also have progestogen)
Mechanism & Notes
Reduces bone resorption by ↓ osteoclast activity. Slows postmenopausal bone loss. Lower risk of harm if started within 10 years of menopause and in women <60. Long-term use: ↑ breast cancer, thrombotic risks.
Counselling (Estrogen)
  • Report: leg swelling/pain, chest pain, SOB (thrombosis); breast lumps or changes; irregular vaginal bleeding after first months.
  • Discuss risks and benefits carefully — best suited to younger postmenopausal women with another reason for hormone therapy.
Raloxifene — Dose
60 mg PO daily
SERM — estrogenic effect on bone/lipids; anti-estrogenic effect at breast/endometrium. Reduces vertebral fracture risk only (NOT hip or other non-vertebral). ↓ Breast cancer risk. ↑ VTE risk. ↑ Hot flushes. Does not reduce non-vertebral fracture risk — key limitation.
Raloxifene — Precautions
  • VTE history — contraindicated
  • Prolonged immobilisation, surgery — caution
  • CHD, stroke — caution (small ↑ death after stroke)
Tibolone — Dose
  • 2.5 mg PO daily
  • Do NOT start until ≥12 months after last menstrual period
Estrogenic, progestogenic, and anti-estrogenic properties. ↑ Breast cancer risk in women with prior breast cancer. ↑ Stroke risk in women >60. Safety profile less extensively studied than other agents.

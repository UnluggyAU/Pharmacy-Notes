Chronic Non-Cancer Pain

Pain persisting or recurring for >3 months — a biopsychosocial condition requiring multidimensional, self-management-focused care.

🔬

Pathophysiology

💡
Chronic pain is best understood through a biopsychosocial framework. Pain severity does not always correlate with degree of tissue damage — the nervous system itself becomes the source of the problem.
🧠 Central Sensitisation
  • Nervous system becomes hypersensitive and amplifies pain signals
  • Pain can persist long after the original tissue injury has healed
  • Glial cells activate → positive feedback loop of pain signalling
  • Creates a "vicious cycle": pain → sensitisation → more pain
  • Often more important than structural pathology
⚡ Peripheral Changes
  • Nociceptors become sensitised (peripheral sensitisation)
  • Additional stress receptors added to nerve fibres
  • Nerve fibres may become so sensitive even light touch triggers pain (allodynia)
  • Hyperalgesia: exaggerated pain response to normally painful stimuli
  • Spontaneous pain: occurs without any external stimulus
🧩 Biopsychosocial Factors
  • Biological: tissue pathology, nerve changes, genetics
  • Psychological: thoughts, emotions, catastrophising, fear-avoidance
  • Social: relationships, work, culture, isolation
  • All three domains interact — treatment of any one alone is insufficient
  • Pain can be influenced by expectations, beliefs, and environment
⚠️
Pain ≠ tissue damage. The nervous system can generate severe pain even without ongoing injury. This is not "psychological" — it reflects genuine neurobiological changes. Central sensitisation is usually not opioid-responsive.
⚠️

Risk Factors & Burden

👤 Patient Risk Factors
  • Female sex
  • Increasing age (↑ in >65 years: ~1 in 3 adults)
  • Genetic predisposition
  • Physical inactivity
  • Smoking
  • Overweight / obesity
  • Socioeconomic disadvantage
📋 Common Causes
  • Musculoskeletal conditions (arthritis, back pain)
  • Surgery or trauma
  • Endometriosis
  • Neuropathic conditions
  • Migraines / headache disorders
  • Fibromyalgia / nociplastic pain
  • Sometimes no identifiable physical cause
💥 Impact on Daily Life
  • Functional: sleep disturbance, reduced energy, impaired work
  • Social: relationship strain, isolation, reduced intimacy
  • Socioeconomic: ↑ healthcare costs, disability, lost productivity
  • Emotional: anxiety, depression, anger, irritability
  • Up to 5% of people: major impact on function/QoL

🔍

Assessment & Diagnosis

📝 History
  • Pain character, location, severity, duration, pattern
  • Aggravating and relieving factors
  • Function: sleep, work, activities, relationships
  • Psychological: mood, anxiety, beliefs about pain, catastrophising
  • Social: support, work, housing, culture
  • Previous treatments and responses
  • Opioid and substance use history
🩺 Examination & Investigations
  • Full physical and neurological examination
  • Assess for allodynia and hyperalgesia
  • Identify reversible contributors
  • Investigations only if they will change management
  • Imaging often correlates poorly with symptoms — do not overinterpret
📊 Outcome Monitoring
  • PEG Scale: Pain intensity, Enjoyment of life, General activity (0–10 each)
  • Clinically meaningful = ≥30% PEG reduction
  • Sit-to-Stand Test: 5 reps; >15 seconds → falls risk
  • Review at clinic monthly where possible
  • Pain reduction alone is insufficient — function must improve
🎯

Treatment Goals

Primary
  • Restore function and improve quality of life
  • Reduce pain intensity (secondary to function)
  • Support self-management
Functional Targets
  • Return to work / school
  • Improved sleep
  • Social reconnection
  • Physical activity participation
Analgesic Trial Targets
  • ≥30% pain reduction
  • Improved physical/social/vocational function
  • Improved mood/affect
  • Tolerable or no adverse effects
  • No aberrant behaviours
🚨
Complete pain elimination is rarely achievable. The goal is meaningful functional improvement. Analgesics should not be the primary management strategy — they are unlikely to eliminate pain and long-term adverse effects often outweigh benefit.
💡
SMART Goals: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time-contingent. Include goals across vocation, social connection, physical activity, sleep, nutrition, and pain management. Example: "Walk around the park once weekly for 40 minutes within 2 months."
🏃

Non-Pharmacological Management

🚨
Non-pharmacological strategies are FIRST-LINE. Active strategies the patient can use themselves are more effective long-term than passive techniques. Passive-only treatment creates therapist dependence. Overemphasis on analgesics can distract from more effective active strategies.
1
First-line — all patients
Active Self-Management Strategies (Multidimensional)
Physical Activity
Walking groups, pool-based activity, stretching, yoga, tai chi, structured exercise — any form consistently performed
✅ Reverses deconditioning; improves mood, function, and pain severity ✅ Pacing: start at ~80% of current tolerance → increase by 10–20% each week; time-contingent not pain-contingent ⚠️ Normal muscle soreness from restarting activity may be misinterpreted as harm — reassure patient
Psychological Techniques
Patient education, targeted reassurance, CBT, ACT, mindfulness, relaxation, motivational interviewing
✅ Reduces nervous system sensitisation and pain intensity; improves function, mood, and treatment adherence ✅ All clinicians can provide basic techniques; refer to psychologist if more complex
Social Reconnection
Return to work/school, hobbies, peer support, cultural participation, community groups
✅ Social connection reduces threat response → ↓ nervous system sensitisation → ↓ pain intensity ❌ Social isolation worsens pain — address as a priority
Sleep Improvement & Nutrition
✅ Sleep improvement ↓ nervous system sensitisation → ↓ pain. Consistent wake time; reduce screens and caffeine. ✅ Healthy diet (↑ vegetables, ↓ processed foods, ↓ alcohol); smoking cessation in all patients
2
Second-line — alongside first-line strategies, not instead of
Analgesics (Time-Limited) + Invasive Procedures (Selected)
Analgesics (see pharmacological section)
✅ Helpful in some patients for a defined period while self-management is established ⚠️ Aim: integrate self-management, then deprescribe analgesics. Trial deprescribing every 3–6 months. ❌ Benzodiazepines — no role in chronic non-cancer pain; combining with opioids markedly ↑ death risk
Invasive ProceduresSelected patients only — specialist referral
⚠️ Only consider when realistic goals exist and first-line strategies have failed ⚠️ Relief is often short-term — use the window to reinforce self-management ❌ Do not recommend solely on imaging findings — imaging correlates poorly with symptoms
Uncertain Benefit / Potential Harm (short-term only <12 weeks if used):
  • TENS: Weak evidence; may be ineffective in opioid-tolerant patients; can interfere with cardiac implanted devices
  • Acupuncture / Dry needling: Little evidence; small effect size; dry needling must be performed by AHPRA-registered clinician
  • Massage: Short-term reduction only; no medium- or long-term benefit
  • Passive mobilisation: Short-term effect; no evidence beyond 3 months; safer than spinal manipulation
  • Spinal manipulation: Similar benefit to mobilisation but significantly higher risk (rib fracture, radicular pain, vertebral artery dissection) — avoid in children, elderly, connective tissue disorders, fracture risk
  • Trigger point therapy: Unproven pathology; infection risk; no evidence of effectiveness
💊

Pharmacological Treatment

🚨
Analgesics are second-line. Match to pain type. Agree on goals, expected benefit, intended duration, monitoring, and stopping plan before starting any analgesic trial.
💡
Analgesic trial: if benefit not apparent within 4 weeks → deprescribe and consider alternatives. Benefit = ≥30% pain reduction AND improved function AND tolerable adverse effects AND no aberrant behaviours.
1
First-line — nociceptive pain only; not for neuropathic or nociplastic pain
Paracetamol
ParacetamolIR: 1 g q4–6h (max 4 g/day) | MR: 1.33 g q8h (max 4 g/day)
✅ Safer profile than NSAIDs at therapeutic doses — try first ❌ Limited efficacy — only for suspected nociceptive component ❌ Do NOT use for neuropathic or nociplastic pain ❌ Chronic liver disease — ↑ hepatotoxicity risk 🔵 Stop after 1 week if no relief. If helpful, reassess after 4 weeks. Trial deprescribing every 3–6 months.
2

NSAIDs (short-term)

Reassess after 4 weeks. Deprescribe at every review.
3
Pain with neuropathic component — adjuvants are mainstay

Opioids (Last Resort — Short-Term Only)
🚨
Opioids are NOT recommended for routine use in chronic non-cancer pain. High-quality evidence shows little, if any, long-term benefit. Harm is substantial (misuse risk ~45% with long-term use). Central sensitisation is usually not opioid-responsive. Opioids can reduce motivation to engage in self-management.
Opioids (Modified-Release Preferred)Max ≤40 mg oral morphine equivalent/day | Max ≤12 weeks without specialist support
✅ Only if all analgesic trial parameters met; non-opioids adequately trialled; benefits clearly outweigh harms ❌ Assess opioid misuse risk before starting (opioid risk tool) ❌ Benzodiazepines — avoid combining with opioids (markedly ↑ death risk) ⚠️ Consider written opioid agreement; one prescriber, one dispenser; monitor with 4 A's (Analgesia, Activity, Adverse effects, Aberrant behaviour) 🔵 Review 1 week after starting or dose change → then 2 weeks → then every 4 weeks. Consider take-home naloxone if overdose risk.

Drug Summaries

ParacetamolNon-Opioid Analgesic / Antipyretic
Doses (Adult)
  • Immediate-release (IR): 0.5–1 g PO every 4–6 hours PRN (max 4 g/24 hours)
  • Controlled-release (CR 665 mg): 2 tablets PO every 6–8 hours swallowed whole (max 6 tablets = 3990 mg/day)
  • Onset: ~30 minutes (oral); 5–10 minutes (IV)
Mechanism of Action
Exact mechanism unclear. Thought to inhibit central prostaglandin synthesis and modulate descending serotonergic pain pathways. Minimal peripheral anti-inflammatory effect.
Role in Chronic Pain
Limited efficacy in chronic non-cancer pain. Only consider if nociceptive component suspected. NOT effective for neuropathic or nociplastic pain. Usually trialled before NSAIDs due to safer profile.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Chronic liver disease or alcohol misuse — ↑ hepatotoxicity risk; use with caution or avoid
  • Check for combination products containing paracetamol — easy to inadvertently exceed max dose
  • Fasting or malnutrition — ↑ risk; reduce dose if needed
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • ↑ Aminotransferases — especially with 4 g/day for ≥4 days; clinical significance usually unclear
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Hepatotoxicity — at therapeutic doses in predisposed patients (liver disease, alcohol, malnutrition, fasting, enzyme inducers)
  • Severe skin reactions — fixed drug eruption, SJS, TEN
  • Blood dyscrasias — neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, pancytopenia
  • Acute hepatitis
  • Hypersensitivity reactions — rash; anaphylaxis very rare
  • Hypotension (IV formulation)
Counselling
  • Do not exceed 4 g/day — check all products for paracetamol content (many combination products and cold/flu medications contain paracetamol).
  • Do not use more than one product containing paracetamol at the same time.
  • Controlled-release tablets: swallow whole — do not crush or chew.
  • Stop after 1 week if no pain relief. Reassess regularly.
  • Avoid alcohol during use — increases hepatotoxicity risk.
NSAIDsNon-Opioid Analgesic — Anti-inflammatory
Doses (Adult)
  • Celecoxib (COX-2 selective): 100–200 mg PO BD
  • Ibuprofen (non-selective): 200–400 mg PO 3–4×/day
  • Naproxen (non-selective): 250–500 mg PO BD
  • Diclofenac (preferential COX-2): 25–50 mg PO 2–3×/day
  • Meloxicam (preferential COX-2): 7.5–15 mg PO daily
  • Use lowest effective dose for shortest possible duration
Mechanism
Inhibit COX-1 and/or COX-2 → ↓ prostaglandin synthesis → ↓ inflammation, pain, and fever. COX-2 selective agents have lower GI risk but retain renal and CV risk.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Active PUD / GI bleeding history — contraindicated
  • CrCl <25–30 mL/min — avoid; all NSAIDs are nephrotoxic
  • Severe hepatic impairment — avoid
  • Pregnancy — avoid, especially third trimester
  • CVD, heart failure, hypertension — caution; all NSAIDs ↑ CV risk
  • Elderly and asthma — caution (bronchospasm risk)
  • Dehydration — ↑ nephrotoxicity risk
  • Anticoagulants, antihypertensives, methotrexate, lithium — important interactions
  • Triple Whammy: NSAID + ACEi/ARB + diuretic → AKI
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • GI upset, nausea, dyspepsia, diarrhoea
  • GI ulceration/bleeding — especially non-selective NSAIDs
  • ↑ BP, fluid retention, oedema
Rare / Serious
  • Acute kidney injury
  • CV events (MI, stroke) — ↑ with all NSAIDs, especially COX-2 selective
  • Bronchospasm in aspirin-sensitive asthma
Counselling
  • Take with food to reduce stomach upset.
  • Use lowest dose for shortest time needed.
  • Report: black/tarry stools, unusual bleeding, leg swelling, breathlessness.
  • Avoid combining with other NSAIDs including aspirin.
Gabapentinoids — Gabapentin & PregabalinAdjuvant Analgesic — α2δ Calcium Channel Ligand
⚠️
Misuse and dependence are increasingly reported. Gabapentinoids are misused for euphoric and sedative effects even in patients without prior substance misuse history. Monitor for signs of misuse (dose escalation, drug-seeking). History of substance misuse or psychiatric disorders — ↑ risk; avoid if possible. Use lowest effective dose for shortest time possible.
Doses (Adult — Neuropathic / Chronic Pain)
  • Gabapentin — initial: 100–300 mg PO once daily at nightTitrate every 3–7 days: nocte → BD → TDS. Maximum: 3600 mg/24h in 3 divided doses. Frail/>70 years: start lower; max 900 mg/24h, slower titration. Largest dose at night.
  • Pregabalin — initial: 25–75 mg PO at nightIncrease to BD after 3–7 days. Titrate slowly as tolerated. Maximum: 600 mg/24h. Frail/>70 years: lower initial dose, max 300 mg/24h. Uneven dosing (larger dose in evening) may improve sleep and reduce daytime sedation.
  • Assess response at 4 weeks; stop gradually if no improvement. Reassess ongoing need every 3–6 months.
Renal Dose Adjustment
  • Gabapentin: CrCl 50–80: 0.6–1.8 g/day in 3 doses | CrCl 30–50: 300–900 mg/day | CrCl 15–30: 300 mg q2days up to 600 mg/day | CrCl <15: 300 mg q2days up to 300 mg/day
  • Pregabalin: CrCl 30–60: initially 75 mg/day, max 300 mg | CrCl 15–30: 25–50 mg/day, max 150 mg | CrCl <15: 25 mg/day, max 75 mg single dose
Mechanism of Action
Bind to α2-δ protein subunit of high threshold voltage-dependent calcium channels → ↓ calcium influx → ↓ release of excitatory neurotransmitters (glutamate, substance P, noradrenaline). Note: structurally related to GABA but do NOT significantly affect GABA or its receptors. Also modulate descending inhibitory pain pathways.
Evidence Notes
  • Most patients will NOT achieve clinically useful pain relief — manage expectations
  • Evidence supports use in diabetic neuropathy and postherpetic neuralgia; pregabalin in post-traumatic neuropathic pain
  • Little evidence of efficacy for other types of neuropathic pain
  • RCT shows pregabalin is no more effective than placebo for sciatica
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Opioids or other CNS depressants — ↑ risk of CNS and respiratory depression; deaths reported; avoid if possible or monitor closely with lowest doses of each
  • History of substance misuse or psychiatric disorders — ↑ risk of misuse and dependence; avoid if possible
  • Pregabalin — pregnancy: avoid if possible; may increase risk of major congenital malformations. Ensure effective contraception during treatment.
  • Renal impairment — both agents renally excreted; dose reduction required (see table above)
  • Pregabalin — heart failure: use with caution (rare reports of worsening)
  • Absence seizures — risk of aggravation
  • Elderly/frail — ↑ falls, sedation, cognitive impairment; lower doses, slower titration
  • Do not stop abruptly — taper over at least 1 week (withdrawal: anxiety, insomnia, sweating, nausea, diarrhoea; ↑ seizure risk in epilepsy)
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%) — Class
  • Drowsiness, sedation, fatigue, dizziness, vertigo — especially at initiation and dose increases
  • Ataxia, confusion, amnesia, dysarthria
  • Peripheral oedema, weight gain, dry mouth
  • Blurred vision, diplopia
Common (>1%) — Pregabalin specific
  • Irritability, depression, lethargy, insomnia, impaired balance
  • Constipation, muscle cramps, arthralgia
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Agitation, rash, sexual dysfunction
  • Pregabalin: hallucinations, cognitive impairment, urinary incontinence, tachycardia, first-degree heart block
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Respiratory depression — especially with opioids or CNS depressants
  • Severe hypersensitivity — anaphylaxis, angioedema, SJS
  • Rhabdomyolysis, renal failure
  • Pregabalin: heart failure, pancreatitis
Deprescribing
  • <3 months: reduce by 25–30% per week over ≥1 week
  • >3 months: reduce by 25–30% every 2 weeks
Counselling
  • Take the largest dose at night — reduces daytime sedation; drowsiness is expected and usually improves after 1–2 weeks.
  • Do not stop suddenly — always reduce gradually as directed (risk of withdrawal and, in epilepsy, ↑ seizure risk).
  • Avoid alcohol and other sedating medications — additive CNS depression.
  • Take care with driving and operating machinery, especially at initiation and after dose changes.
  • Pregabalin: use effective contraception during treatment. Do not crush or chew capsules.
  • May take several weeks to see full effect — reassess after 4 weeks. If no meaningful benefit, the dose will be tapered and stopped.
TCAs — Amitriptyline & NortriptylineAdjuvant Analgesic — Tricyclic Antidepressant
Doses (Adult — Analgesic Use)
  • Amitriptyline: 10–25 mg PO at night initially → ↑ by 10–25 mg every 7 days as toleratedIf no pain relief by 50 mg → discontinue. If benefit → may titrate up to 75 mg (migraine) or 150 mg (pain management) at night.
  • Nortriptyline: 10–25 mg PO at night initially → titrate weekly → up to 150 mg at night
  • Older/frail patients: lower starting dose, slower titration. Nortriptyline preferred in elderly.
  • Note: analgesic doses are much lower than antidepressant doses
  • Alter dose in increments every 2–3 days as needed
Mechanism of Action
Inhibit reuptake of noradrenaline and serotonin into presynaptic terminals → ↑ descending inhibitory pain pathways. Also block cholinergic, histaminergic, alpha1-adrenergic receptors (responsible for most adverse effects). Sodium channel blocking and NMDA receptor antagonism also contribute to analgesia. Antihistaminergic effect → ↑ sleep.
Nortriptyline vs Amitriptyline
  • Active metabolite of amitriptyline
  • Less hypotension, sedation, and anticholinergic effects — preferred in elderly
  • Serum drug concentration can be used to guide dosage (best-defined concentration-effect relationship of all TCAs)
  • Both equally effective for neuropathic pain at analgesic doses
Precautions & Contraindications
  • Second- or third-degree heart block, severe ischaemic heart disease, cardiac instability (recent MI) — contraindicated
  • MAOIs — contraindicated (serotonin syndrome)
  • High risk of overdose/suicide — avoid TCAs; prescribe small quantities. Overdose carries high risk of fatality.
  • QT prolongation risk — TCAs prolong QT interval; avoid if uncorrectable QT risk factors
  • Epilepsy — ↓ seizure threshold (dose-dependent); use with caution, low doses, slow titration
  • BPH, narrow-angle glaucoma — may worsen (anticholinergic); urinary retention risk
  • Elderly — ↑ falls, confusion, anticholinergic adverse effects; prefer nortriptyline
  • Coronary heart disease — tachycardia may precipitate angina
  • Bipolar disorder — may trigger manic episode
  • Hepatic impairment — halve dose in severe impairment
  • Check BP (supine and standing) before and after starting and after each dose change
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Sedation, drowsiness — usually diminishes over 1–2 weeks; take at night
  • Dry mouth, constipation, urinary hesitancy, blurred vision, mydriasis (anticholinergic)
  • Orthostatic hypotension, sinus tachycardia — ↑ falls risk especially in elderly
  • Weight gain, decreased lacrimation, reduced GI motility
  • Tremor, dizziness, sweating, agitation, insomnia, anxiety, confusion
  • Sexual dysfunction (impotence, ↓ libido)
  • Anticholinergic delirium — particularly in elderly and Parkinson's disease
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Slowed cardiac conduction, T-wave changes, arrhythmias (especially at higher doses)
  • Nausea, hyperglycaemia, allergic skin reactions
  • Manic episodes (in susceptible patients)
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Blood dyscrasias, hepatitis, paralytic ileus
  • Prolonged QT interval, seizures
  • Hyponatraemia (SIADH), increased intraocular pressure
Monitoring
  • BP (supine and standing) before starting, after starting, and after each dose change — orthostatic hypotension
  • ECG if high cardiac risk — monitor for heart block or QT prolongation
  • Review suicidal ideation early in treatment, particularly in young people
  • Therapeutic response usually delayed 2 weeks — do not assume treatment failure before then
Counselling
  • Take at night — causes drowsiness which is useful for sleep. Avoid daytime driving until you know how you react.
  • Side effects (dry mouth, constipation, drowsiness) often lessen or disappear after about 7 days.
  • You may feel dizzy on standing — get up gradually from sitting or lying; sit or lie down if dizzy.
  • Do not stop suddenly — reduce dose gradually to avoid withdrawal effects.
  • Avoid alcohol — additive CNS depression.
  • May take 2–4 weeks to notice full analgesic effect.
SNRIs — Duloxetine & Venlafaxine XRAdjuvant Analgesic — Serotonin-Noradrenaline Reuptake Inhibitor
Doses (Adult)
  • Duloxetine: 30 mg PO mane (morning) initially — 30 mg for first week helps reduce nauseaIncrease by 30 mg every 7 days as tolerated. Maximum: 120 mg/day. CrCl <30: 30 mg/day only.
  • Venlafaxine XR: 75 mg PO mane initiallyIncrease to 150 mg if required. Up to 225 mg may be needed in some patients. CrCl <30 or dialysis: halve dose. Hepatic impairment: halve dose, titrate carefully.
Mechanism
Inhibit reuptake of serotonin AND noradrenaline → enhanced descending inhibitory pain pathways. Noradrenaline reuptake inhibition particularly important for analgesia. No anticholinergic effects (advantage over TCAs).
Duloxetine vs Venlafaxine
  • Duloxetine: contraindicated in hepatic impairment (hepatically metabolised); lower BP elevation risk than venlafaxine
  • Duloxetine: preferred over venlafaxine in patients with obesity (less weight gain); preferred if comorbid depression
  • Venlafaxine: dose-dependent BP ↑; toxic in overdose (ECG changes, arrhythmias, seizures, fatalities)
  • Both: taper on cessation; venlafaxine withdrawal more common
Precautions & Contraindications
  • MAOIs — contraindicated (serotonin toxicity); wait ≥14 days after stopping MAOI before starting; wait 1–2 days after stopping SNRI before starting MAOI
  • Duloxetine — hepatic impairment: contraindicated
  • Duloxetine — strong CYP1A2 inhibitors (e.g. fluvoxamine): contraindicated
  • Hypertension — SNRIs can ↑ BP (especially venlafaxine); control hypertension before starting venlafaxine; monitor BP regularly
  • High bleeding risk or concurrent NSAIDs/aspirin — SNRIs ↓ platelet aggregation; ↑ bleeding risk
  • Renal impairment — duloxetine: low dose at CrCl <30; venlafaxine: halve dose at CrCl <30
  • Bipolar disorder — all antidepressants may provoke mania
  • Risk of hyponatraemia — especially in elderly; check Na at baseline and early treatment
  • Do not stop suddenly — taper over weeks to months to avoid discontinuation syndrome ("brain zaps", dizziness, flu-like)
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Nausea — most common; usually transient (1–2 weeks); take with food
  • Dry mouth, constipation, yawning, sweating, decreased appetite
  • Dizziness, headache, somnolence, insomnia, blurred vision, tremor, rash
  • ↑ BP — venlafaxine (dose-dependent); duloxetine (infrequent)
  • Sexual dysfunction (impotence, ↓ libido), weakness
Infrequent (0.1–1%)
  • Orthostatic hypotension, palpitations, tachycardia
  • Abnormal LFTs, hyponatraemia (SIADH — usually early; may be asymptomatic)
  • Duloxetine: urinary retention
  • Venlafaxine: ECG changes
Rare (<0.1%)
  • Duloxetine: liver failure, GI bleeding, SJS, cutaneous vasculitis
  • Venlafaxine: serious bleeding, acute angle-closure glaucoma, hepatitis, myositis
  • Both: seizures, takotsubo cardiomyopathy
Monitoring
  • BP before starting and regularly during treatment (especially venlafaxine)
  • Sodium (Na) at baseline and early in treatment — especially in elderly or at-risk patients
  • Monitor suicidal ideation early in treatment, particularly in young people
Counselling
  • Take with food in the morning to minimise nausea — nausea usually improves after 1–2 weeks.
  • Do not open, crush or chew capsules (duloxetine, venlafaxine XR) — swallow whole.
  • Do not stop suddenly — always reduce gradually as directed (discontinuation syndrome).
  • Report unusual bleeding, significant mood changes, or markedly increased BP.
  • Be careful with driving and operating machinery until you know how this medicine affects you.
  • Allow 2–4 weeks to assess full analgesic effect.
Opioids (Chronic Non-Cancer Pain)Strong Analgesic — Last Resort Only
🚨
Opioids are NOT recommended for routine use in chronic non-cancer pain. Use only when all other analgesic options have been adequately trialled, benefits clearly outweigh harms, opioid misuse risk assessed, and specialist support is accessible.
Adult Options (Modified-Release Preferred)
  • Buprenorphine patch: 5 mcg/hr → replace every 7 days → titrate by 5 mcg/hr (max 20 mcg/hr without specialist)
  • Morphine MR (12h): 5–10 mg PO BD → ↑ by 5–10 mg every 3 days (max 40 mg/day without specialist)
  • Oxycodone MR: 5 mg PO BD → ↑ by 5 mg every 3 days (max 30 mg/day without specialist)
  • Tapentadol MR: 50 mg PO BD → ↑ by 50 mg every 3 days (max 300 mg/day without specialist)Dual MOA: mu-opioid agonist + noradrenaline reuptake inhibitor. Contraindicated within 14 days of MAOI. Moderate hepatic impairment: reduce to 50 mg once daily.
  • Tramadol MR: 50 mg PO BD → ↑ by 50 mg every 3 days (max 400 mg/day without specialist)Dual MOA: weak mu-opioid agonist + noradrenaline AND serotonin reuptake inhibitor. Serotonin toxicity risk with MAOIs (contraindicated). CYP2D6 metabolised — variable response. Naloxone only partially antagonises overdose. CrCl <30: do not use CR formulation.
  • Overall max: ≤40 mg oral morphine equivalent/day without specialist; max 12 weeks
  • Renal impairment — opioid selection: Avoid codeine and pethidine (toxic metabolite accumulation). Morphine: active metabolites accumulate — reduce dose or avoid if CrCl <50 mL/min. Oxycodone: only weakly active metabolites — preferred in renal impairment (reduce dose). Fentanyl/buprenorphine: no active metabolites — safest in renal impairment. Tramadol: reduce frequency if CrCl <30.
Precautions & Contraindications
  • High opioid misuse/addiction risk — assess with Opioid Risk Tool before starting
  • Benzodiazepines — avoid combining (markedly ↑ death risk from respiratory depression); deaths reported
  • Gabapentin/pregabalin + opioids — ↑ risk of CNS and respiratory depression; deaths reported; avoid or use with extreme caution at lowest doses of each with close monitoring
  • Respiratory depression — titrate carefully; respiratory status and sedation guide dose
  • Hepatic impairment — ↑ opioid sensitivity
  • Renal impairment — metabolite accumulation (except fentanyl which has no active metabolites)
  • Elderly — lower doses, slower titration
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Constipation — manage proactively; does not resolve with tolerance
  • Nausea / vomiting — usually transient
  • Sedation, cognitive impairment
  • Tolerance, physical dependence
Serious
  • Respiratory depression — potentially fatal
  • Opioid use disorder (~45% misuse risk with long-term use)
  • Opioid-induced hyperalgesia — paradoxical worsening of pain
Monitoring (4 A's) & Safety
  • Analgesia: ≥30% pain reduction AND improved function AND tolerable adverse effects AND no aberrant behaviours — all must be present
  • Activity: Improved physical, social, and vocational function
  • Adverse effects: Constipation, sedation, respiratory status
  • Aberrant behaviour: Urine drug testing, collateral histories, prescription monitoring; if present → deprescribe or tighten boundaries
  • Sedation score — early marker of respiratory depression: 0 = wide awake | 1 = easy to rouse | 2 = easy to rouse but cannot stay awake (= early respiratory depression) | 3 = difficult to rouse (severe). Aim for score <2.
  • Laxative: Always start a stimulant laxative (e.g. docusate + senna) as soon as chronic opioid treatment begins — constipation does not resolve with tolerance
Deprescribing
  • <3 months use: reduce by 10–25% every week
  • >3 months use: reduce by 10–25% every 4 weeks
  • Faster reduction if intolerable adverse effects or misuse suspected
Counselling
  • Must be combined with active self-management — not a replacement for it.
  • One prescriber and one dispenser only.
  • Store securely; do not share or sell; dispose of unused medication safely.
  • Do not increase dose without prescriber advice.
  • Avoid alcohol and benzodiazepines — serious respiratory depression risk.
  • Constipation is expected and persistent — use a laxative as needed.
Lidocaine 5% PatchTopical Local Anaesthetic — Localised Neuropathic Pain
Dose (Adult)
  • Up to 3 patches over the painful area
  • Wear up to 12 hours then patch-free interval
  • Longer wear may be considered if pain returns during patch-free interval and skin remains intact — avoid continuous use
Mechanism
Stabilises neuronal membranes → blocks sodium channels → ↓ peripheral nociceptor activity → ↓ localised pain signal generation. Minimal systemic absorption at recommended doses.
Indications
  • Postherpetic neuralgia (localised)
  • Nerve entrapment
  • Localised neuropathic pain where systemic drugs are not tolerated
Adverse Effects
Common (>1%)
  • Local skin reactions — erythema, rash, burning, pruritus at application site
Rare
  • Systemic lidocaine toxicity if misused or applied to broken/inflamed skin (↑ absorption)
Counselling
  • Apply to intact, dry skin over the most painful area.
  • Remove after 12 hours — allow a patch-free period before reapplying.
  • Do not apply to broken or inflamed skin.
  • Wash hands after handling. Do not cut patches.
